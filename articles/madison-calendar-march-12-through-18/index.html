Madison calendar, March 12 through 18

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
March 11, 2015 @ 12:51 pm

Clockwise from left: John Statz plays Saturday at the Crystal Corner; The Homestretch screens Thursday at the Central Library; Swervedriver plays Friday at the High Noon Saloon.

events

Swervedriver, John Statz, Dan Bitney, Tavi Gevinson, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY MARCH 12

Helmet (sold out). High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

While we're bummed that batshit drummer John Stainer won't be re-joining Helmet with his impeccable timing and mile-high cymbal stand anytime soon, we'll settle for Paige Hamilton and his hired-hand band, as they swing through Madison to bang out the entirety of 1994's Betty. Sure, we understand that this leftfield hard-rock monument pretty much paved the way for hours upon hours of copycat distillation from myriad forgettable nu-metal bands that stripped the album for it's chugging drop-D riffs and groove-powered rhythmic syncopation. What those bands left behind were all the jazz-inspired riffs, lush chord voicings, and adventurous detours the album held (see the twangy grit of “Sam Hell" or the abstract Southern rock vibe of “The Silver Hawaiian"). This is why Betty remains timeless and Half Price Books (probably) won't give you $.10 for your used Chevelle CD.


The Homestretch. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Anne de Mare and Kirsten Kelly's 2014 documentary delves into the lives of three homeless teenagers striving to survive and finish high school in Chicago. As the three students make their way in the world with virtually none of the support structure most people take for granted, the filmmakers uncover their will to build a better life and the nuanced issues they face, from the justice system to sexual identity.

Ghost Town. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m.

Screenwriter and Wisconsin native David Koepp will wrap up his visit to Madison with a post-screening discussion of the 2008 comedy Ghost Town, which he wrote and directed. Ricky Gervais stars as a dentist who acquires the ability to talk to dead people, and the results are reputedly much better than that other film in which Ricky Gervais has special powers, 2009's The Invention Of Lying.

FRIDAY MARCH 13

Swervedriver, Gateway Drugs. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m.

With original vocalist-guitarist Adam Franklin and lead guitarist Jimmy Hartridge in tow, Swervedriver visits Madison in support of I Wasn't Meant To Lose You, the Oxford shoegazers' first full-length since 1998's 99th Dream. From the sweetly dissonant riffing and understated vocal melodies of album kickoff “Audiodidact," it's immediately clear that the fuzzy guitar-pop innovators have been relatively unaffected by the aughts' impossible hypercycle of sonic trends and have stuck to what they do best. As if the possibility of Swervedriver ripping out favorites from 1993 classic Mezcal Head isn't enticing enough, the new album is packed with plenty of verbed-out space leads (“Lone Star"), meandering, psychedelic trudges (“Red Queen Arms Race"), and immersively mysterious pop tunes (“Setting Sun") to please all the old heads on its own.


Eroica + Walkover. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Polish filmmaker Andrzej Munk's 1958 black comedy Eroica (7 p.m.) follows two storylines amid the Nazi occupation of Poland. Jerzy Skolimowski's 1965 film Walkover (8:45 p.m.) is the second of four semi-autobiographical films Skolimowski made, centering around his time working in a factory and taking up boxing. This intriguing pairing is part of a running series of Polish films curated by Martin Scorsese.

SATURDAY MARCH 14

Hip Hop Fights For Justice. 30 W. Mifflin St. (outdoors), 5 p.m. (free)

In the wake of the Tony Robinson shooting, a bunch of Madison hip-hop artists—including DLO and Dudu Stinks, who weeks before Robinson's put out a new song about police shootings of unarmed black people, and CRASHprez, whose new album More Perfect confronts that theme in its lyrics and cover art—are holding a free show and gathering donations to benefit Robinson's family. Also on the bill are Rob Dz, K.I.N.G. Kronos, Divida Loca, and DJ Pain 1.

John Statz, Evan Murdock, Kelsey Sprague. Crystal Corner Bar, 9:30 p.m.

The songs on former Madisonian and current Denver resident John Statz's new album, Tulsa, are well-suited to a solitary, reflective listen, but have a ruggedness and openness that suggests the material can easily turn a bit more raucous in this full-band release show at the Crystal, which Statz name-checks in the first verse of “Exposure." Over the years, the singer-songwriter has sharpened his focus: On standout tracks like “Roadkill Zone" and a cover of Radiohead's “Motion Picture Soundtrack," Statz brings off a weary, resilient persona without self-importance. And throughout the album, he gets a ton of tasteful, warm, subtle accompaniment from musicians including drummer Billy Conway of Morphine, guitarist Mark Spencer of Son Volt, bassist Jeremy Moses Curtis (Booker T), and album producer and fellow singer-songwriter Jeffrey Foucault.


Kronos Quartet. Union Theater, 8 p.m.

We'd be really hard-pressed to find a band that's accomplished more than the neo-classical new-age explorers of San Francisco's Kronos Quartet have in their whopping 40-plus years together. With an overwhelming collaboration and commission résumé that includes working with everyone from Paul McCartney to Arvo Part to Bollywood legend Asha Bosle, the avant-classical veterans have most recently lent their devastatingly gorgeous, soaring string talents to the freshly released Tundra Songs, a compilation of tunes penned by experimental Canadian composer David Charke that that features input from Bjork collaborator and throat-singer Tanya Tagaq.


Dan Bitney, Selina Trepp, Anton Hatwich, LeRoy Bach. Mickey's Tavern, 10:30 p.m. (free)

Madison native and Tortoise drummer Dan Bitney plays a free shows here with several collaborators from Chicago's experimental music community. In the first set, he'll join with vocalist and multimedia artist Selina Trepp for a set of improvised electronic music and an audiovisual accompaniment under the name Spectralina. After that, Bitney and Trepp will play two improvised sets with bassist Anton Hatwich (who's played in numerous contemporary jazz outfits in Chicago) and guitarist LeRoy Bach (who has played with Wilco, Joan Of Arc, and Bonnie “Prince" Billy).

Bridget Everett. Majestic, 7 p.m.

The Majestic is billing her as "Comedian Bridget Everett," but, despite appearances at Montreal's Just For Laughs Festival and on Inside Amy Schumer, “comedian" (not unlike like the outfits she tends to wear on stage) isn't enough to contain who and what she is. Everett has described herself as an “alt-cabaret provocateur," which gets a little closer, but even that doesn't do justice to her over-the-top, brassy-and-braless stage show, where torch songs to breasts are routinely interrupted with audience interaction by way of drive-by motorboatings ("You got them little nippy titties/Put 'em in the air/She got them tube-sock titties/She put 'em in the air/I got these beavertail titties/Put 'em in the air!"). It has yet to be seen if she'll have Ad-Rock backing her up here, but her transgressive M.O. is fascinating enough by itself.


The Dead Man And Being Happy + The Use Of A Magazine Rack. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

UW Cinematheque's showcase of contemporary Argentine cinema continues with 2012's dark comedy The Dead Man And Being Happy (7 p.m.) and 2013's continuous, one-take, conversation-centered The Use Of A Magazine Rack (8:45 p.m.).

Why Don't You Play In Hell? Union South Marquee, 11:15 p.m. (free)

SUNDAY MARCH 15

Minnie And Moskowitz. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

John Cassevetes' 1971 comedy Minnie And Moskowitz follows the love that develops between a jaded museum curator (Gena Rowlands) and an unrefined parking-lot attendant (Seymour Cassel). Not unlike Love Streams (which screened earlier this year at Cinematheque and also stars Rowlands and Cassel), this film thrives on the comedy and empathy that Cassevetes wrings from strange matchups and the chaos of life.

King Khan & BBQ Show, Milk Lines, Cowboy Winter. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

With the exception of the wonky, lo-fi hardcore tune “D.F.O." (shorthand for “Diarrhea Fuck Off"), mainstay garage-punk duo King Khan & BBQ Show didn't pack too many surprises on their first album in six years, Bad News Boys. We suppose this is fine for the traditionalist garage-rock set that wants the ability to easily predict what happens 8 bars in advance. But for those of us charmed by seemingly labored-over soul-punk gems by King Khan & The Shrines (i.e. “Welfare Bread" and “Sweet Tooth"), it's pretty tough to get too geeked over a facelessly swinging rocker like “When Will I Be Tamed" or the filthy bugglegum of “Kiss My Sister's Fist." Then again, these tunes likely work much better within the context of the live show, where these two tend to do the work of 4 dudes at once.


MONDAY MARCH 16

Sinister Resonance, Nuggernaut, Fringe Character. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

Madison four-piece Sinister Resonance embraces jazz and a few decades' worth of experimental and aggressive music. On the group's debut album, They Said..., released at this show, sprawling original compositions sit alongside new interpretations of an unlikely mix of sources: Nels Cline, Henry Cowell, and Dub Trio, among others. Trombone player Mark Hetzler, pianist Vincent Fuh, bassist Nick Moran and drummer Todd Hammes are taking on a lot here, sonically and conceptually, and there are some bumpy transitions along the way, but the group also gives itself plenty of room to spread out in conversational interplay. We'll be debuting one of the originals from the album on Thursday.

First By First Wave. Overture Center Promenade Hall, 7:30 p.m. (free)

In a late addition to the Line Breaks Festival schedule, this bill brings together a generous showcase of the hip-hop/poetry/theater artists in UW's First Wave Program. Whereas other events in the ongoing Line Breaks fest sprawl across different media, this will be a more music-focused night with sets from CRASHprez, Lord of the Fly , Broadway, Sean Avery, Amy Alida, and Joe Verge.

TUESDAY MARCH 17

Emerging Women Of Burma. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

Ko Aung Ko and Jai Jai's 2013 documentary Emerging Women Of Burma looks at the struggles of female activists fighting for change in Burma's notoriously repressive, closed-off political climate.

Tavi Gevinson. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m. (free)

The 2014-15 Distinguished Lecture Series slate has been heavy on figures influencing the future of media (see Jad Abumrad, last year, or Ta-Nehisi Coates, coming up next month). Although Isthmus dinged the DLS for booking 18-year-old Tavi Gevinson (in its annual Cheap Shots issue), it might actually benefit all of us in publishing to hear about writer and actress Gevinson's experience founding Rookiemag, an influential and fast-growing publication for teenage girls.

WEDNESDAY MARCH 18

Citizenfour. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

Laura Poitras' 2014 documentary Citizenfour offers one of the most intimate looks at Edward Snowden since he began leaking out government surveillance secrets and fled the country. The film follows Poitras and journalist Glenn Greenwald as they meet with Snowden in a Hong Kong hotel room, and as Snowden hands over more documentation of the National Security Agency's mass surveillance programs.

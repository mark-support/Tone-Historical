Madison calendar, February 26 through March 4

Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
February 25, 2015 @ 8:45 am

Clockwise from left: Cakes Da Killa plays The Sett on Feb. 26; PRhyme plays the High Noon Saloon on Feb. 28; Tig Notaro plays the Capitol Theater on Feb. 28.

events

PRhyme, Toupee, Tig Notaro, Cakes Da Killa, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY FEBRUARY 26

Restorations, Direct Hit, The American Dead, Avenues. Dragonfly, 10 p.m.

With a collection of oppressively polished tunes that marry the grittily emotive voice of Hot Water Music with the guitar-heavy, textural sweetness of Jesu, Philadelphia, PA's Restorations swung hard with Warped Tour-ready bravado on last year's LP3, and no that's honestly not a put-down. Besides perhaps the droning synth organ that creeps in and out of tunes like “Separate Songs" and “All My Home," there's nothing terribly subtle here. And you know what? We're fine with that because, despite the fact that everything—from the emo-dissonant guitar-voicings of “Misprint" to the off-kilter power-drumming of “No Castle" to the gravelly, Americana vocals of Jon Loudon—is charged into the foreground and slammed into the listener's face for maximum effect, the tunes are emotionally charged, infuriatingly catchy, and refreshingly uncool and personal.


Cakes Da Killa, Uniiqu3. Union South Sett, 9 p.m.

As if last year's awesome Hunger Pangs mixtape didn't give us enough to grab on to, New Jersey-based rapper and LGBT ally Cakes Da Killa's acrobatic flow and perspective has never sounded fresher than his work on this year's lushly thumping #IMF EP. His creative decision to mostly work with producers without vowels in their monikers has paid off, as SXYLK, FXWRK, and C¥CLØ have provided warmly restrained dancefloor burners that borrow elements from deep house and Baltimore club music, laying a gorgeous sonic tapestry below Cakes' busy, sexed-up delivery. The standout track is “Do That Baby," a floating duet with Bronx-based CUNTMAFIA affiliate Quay Dash, their back-and-forth harkening back to LL Cool J and Foxy Brown's aggressively provocative classic “Doin It."


Out Of The Fire: The Banned Books Monologues. Overture Center Promenade Hall, through March 1, see link for showtimes.

Forward Theatre Company's monologue festival invites local and national theater artists to perform monologues commemorating the deep and insidious history of efforts to censor great literature. This year features contributions from veteran playwright Liz Duffy Adams and American Players Theatre staple Jim DeVita. The banned-at-one-time-or-another books the performers will talk about should give you some idea of how crazy book-banning has gotten throughout history, ranging from Margaret Atwood's dystopian masterwork The Handmaid's Tale to Dr. Suess' Hop On Pop, which (fucking seriously, people) came under fire in Toronto just last year.

Home. Barrymore, 5:30 p.m. (free)

Writer/director Jono Oliver's 2013 drama Home stars Gbenga Akinnagbe (best known for playing the murderous but somehow likable Chris Partlow on The Wire) as a severely mentally ill man trying to transition from a group home to a more independent, adult existence. Akinnagbe and Oliver will be visiting Madison to attend this screening, which is presented by the Wisconsin chapter of the National Alliance on Mental Illness.

Sans Soleil. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

If you're like me (Chris), there are a handful of Criterion discs you know next to nothing about but what you DO know is profoundly intriguing. Needless to say, their Chris Marker two-fer has long been one I've kept an eye out for whenever I step into a DVD shop. You get the sense from the Jonathan Rosenbaum's Chicago Reader review of Marker's 1982 film Sans Soleil (“A film about subjectivity, death, photography, social custom, and consciousness itself...") that maybe this one that is hard to put into just the words and the best way to absorb it is to go in with as few preconceived notions as possible. Thankfully, the sporadic WUD's avant garde arm, Starlight Cinema, has programmed this to play on Thursday night, so you can go experience it out for yourself.

FRIDAY FEBRUARY 27

Brennan Connors & Stray Passage, Tony Barba/Michael Brenneis Duo. Good Style Shop, 8 p.m.

Saxophone player Tony Barba (of Youngblood Brass Band and, well, seemingly about half of the outfits in Madison that play jazz or require woodwinds) and drummer Michael Brenneis (The New Breed) are teaming up at this show for a fully improvised set, with Brenneis on his drumkit and Barba accentuating his sax with amplification and effects pedals. They're sharing the bill here with the also improvisation-heavy sax/drums/bass trio Brennan Connors & Stray Passage, and at the end of the night, all five musicians plan to join up for a short collaborative set.

Echo Island, Donny And The Robot. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 8 p.m. (free)

Echo Island, the electronic project of Madison's Brad and Kara Hawes, have three releases under their belt that span from catchy, rock-informed instrumentals (2012's debut In Circles) to more abstract, ambient-informed tracks (2012's Metropia and last year's Hivernal). Lately the project has been getting adventurous with remixes, taking on tracks by artists ranging from tender folk outfit Count This Penny to bizarro electro-pop artist Midas Bison. After this show, the Hawes will work on turning Echo Island into a four-piece band and record a new EP.


Winter Sleep. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Turkish filmmaker Nuri Bilge Ceylan's 2014 drama Winter Sleep is adapted from two Chekhov stories, and centers on a married couple and the husband's sister passing a tense winter together in a mountainside hotel.

Johnny Cash Birthday Bash. Majestic, 8 p.m.

SATURDAY FEBRUARY 28

The Tale Of The Princess Kaguya. Union South Marquee, 8:45 p.m. (free)

It's a little bit of a shame that Kingdom Of Dreams And Madness, the Studio Ghibli doc that UW Cinematheque recently screened (and we wrote about extensively), skewed so far in coverage towards Hayao Miyazaki. Yes, Miyazaki is the rock star over there but Isao Takahata, his brother in arms and Ghibli co-founder, is no less of an incredible filmmaker, having helmed Pom Poko, My Neighbors The Yamadas, and Grave Of The Fireflies. Takahata's most recent film, The Tale Of The Princess Kaguya, is based on a 10th century Japanese folktale about a baby girl discovered in a glowing stalk of bamboo, a story generally considered to be the oldest surviving Japanese narrative. Takahata's gorgeous visual style on this picture derives a lot from Japanese calligraphy which is fitting given the history of the story. No matter where you fall on the subs vs. dubs debate, WUD Films has you covered: “the Saturday screening is in it's original Japanese with subtitles, and the Sunday screening is an English dub [with Dean Cain as The Mikado! - ED.]."

Tig Notaro. Capitol Theater, 8 p.m.

You should support Gilda's Club Madison, which does so much to support and help cancer patients and their families, and you can do so at this benefit show by Tig Notaro, the occasionally absurdist comedian whose experiences with cancer (and emotional traumas in general) were explored in depth in 2012 during a set at Largo that Louis CK and many others described as "instantly legendary," (released a couple of months later as LIVE). Tig's in a much better place these days with a fiancee she's over the moon for, and a documentary on her comedy exploits garnering high praise from critics, but don't think she's incapable of raising her own bar for baring it all: At a show at New York's Town Hall last November she followed an improvisational urge and performing the last 20 minutes of the show topless, exposing her double mastectomy scars.


PRhyme, Your Old Droog, Boldy James, CRASHprez. High Noon Saloon, 10 p.m.

DJ Premier, simply one of the great hip-hop producers, formed the collaborative project with Detroit MC Royce Da 5' 9", releasing a self-titled debut album last year that brought out Preem's austere but massively catchy style and an equally lean and effective set of rhymes from Royce. In addition a no-hyperbole legendary producer and an excellent veteran MC, this show's got a nice opening slate of relative newcomers: NYC's husky-voiced Your Old Droog, Detroit's Boldy James, and Madison's CRASHprez (fresh off the release of his debut album, More Perfect).


Touch Of Evil. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

We'd be hard-pressed to argue with someone about which Orson Welles film was his absolute masterpiece, as many folks would go with Citizen Kane for substantially contextual reasons that are pretty tough to dispute. But for some of us on the more ghoulish end of the spectrum, Welles' finest and creepiest work arrived in 1958's Touch Of Evil, which he both directed and starred in. Welles plays crooked cop Hank Quinlan, who lives in a sketchy town on the U.S.-Mexico border, and finds himself at odds when curious casting choice Charlton Heston's Mike Vargas, a Mexican official, begins investigating a bomb explosion near the border. According to the Cinematheque program, the version screening here contains some of the changes to the film that Welles had requested after he was booted from the production.

Klassik, Sean Anonymous, Oye!, Rich Robbins. Union South Sett, 8 p.m. (free)

SUNDAY MARCH 1

Caro Michele. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

MONDAY MARCH 2

Toupee, Coordinated Suicides, Christian Dior. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

Infectiously primal and far too hasty would be the easiest way to describe the Chicago art-punk destroyers Toupee's 2014 7-inch Yeehaw Gramma, which balanced a tightly wound dance-punk earworm (“Yeehaw Gramma") with another tune packed with brain-prodding sonic nihilism (“Water Torture"). Needless to say, we were left drooling for more of Toupee's distinguished brand of catchy sonic abuse, which is exactly what we'll get when Chicago label Moniker drops the band's upcoming full-length this July. At this show, Toupee will be joined by sinister Madison noise-punks Coordinated Suicides, who, despite undergoing a recent line-up change, wrapped up recording their own debut album earlier this year. Neither band has set a release date for their respective records, so this will be a great chance to sample some quality unreleased chaos.


TUESDAY MARCH 3

Hanah Jon Taylor's Birthday Bash. Cardinal Bar, 5 p.m. (free)

Madison-based saxophone and flute player Hanah Jon Taylor's busy past few years have included touring with the Franco-American jazz outfit Watershed; numerous live improvisational collaborations with musicians from Madison, his native Chicago, and beyond; and developing a new live/recording project titled “Real/Surreal" (which he told us a bit about in an interview last year). Hanah's a charming, sometimes irascible gent who'll talk your ear off if you're lucky, but mostly we admire the spirited, emotionally flowing approach to improvisation that he's honed over the decades, so we're looking forward to this celebration of his 65th birthday.

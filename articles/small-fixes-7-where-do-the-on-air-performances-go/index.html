Small Fixes 7: Where do the on-air performances go?

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
April 07, 2015 @ 9:44 am

Madison band William Z. Villain performing on Wisconsin Public Television's "30 Minute Music Hour" show in 2014.

read

Radio stations and other local media can do a better job of showcasing bands who stop in to perform.

Welcome back to Small Fixes, an occasional series that proposes small but meaningful things the City of Madison and other organizations can do to strengthen arts and music in Madison.

I can't tell you how many times a local or touring band I like has gone in to do an on-air set at WSUM, WORT, or even on a Wisconsin Public Radio show, without me ever hearing it.

Now, I know that in most cases I can go to the radio station's website, find the broadcast in the archives, and scroll about until I find the live set. So part of this is just me being lazy. But part of it is a missed opportunity to give the audience something a bit more lasting, accessible, and easily shared by, say, putting a simple edit of the live set on SoundCloud. I get that audio editing can be tedious and a lot of local radio is created by short-handed staffs and enthusiastic volunteers, but this is a simple way for the band, audience and radio station to get more mileage out of something that's already taken a fair bit of effort. While there's no way to do this for every single thing you air that might be of interest, it seems worth doing for something that's fairly unique and that might air at a time when not a lot of people are listening in. It would give more listeners a chance to run across local bands they otherwise might not have checked out. I know if these were more readily available, I'd be sharing lots of them through Tone Madison and our social media accounts.

One thing I've learned the hard way from working in media is that most of the time, readers/listeners really don't want to hear instructions (especially ones that involve digging around in the bowels of a website) or explanations of your writing/design/presentation process or the rationale behind it (especially, say, “we don't post things in an easily shared/embedded format because..."). Not because audiences aren't smart enough, and not even because they don't care, but because explanations and instructions cannot possibly make up for the content or functionality they're not getting. We all get bogged down in that sometimes and it's important to step back and question it. If you're a media person reading this and feel like it's rubbing in how thankless your work can be, well, try to think of it as a challenge to help more people enjoy the value of what you do. People expect their media to be intuitive and easily shareable, and woe to any organization that can't see an opportunity in that.

There are some folks already doing this right. Wisconsin Public Television's 30 Minute Music Hour (hosted and produced by my friend Andy Moore, and due to shoot new episodes this summer), creates high-quality web video from its broadcasts of Wisconsin and national musicians. Isthmus' in-office live sessions (so far focused more on touring artists than local ones, and at first co-branded with the hit-and-miss Communion concert series) are showcased on SoundCloud and on video, and some kind soul put Ryley Walker's January visit to WSUM on YouTube.

I don't say all this to kick our local radio folks in a challenging time for public and community-funded media. They are enormously valuable, from their music offerings to their journalism. They're some of the most dedicated and extremely hard-working people I've met anywhere in media. They're hugely supportive of other independent media, especially the folks at WORT, who work with me to create a Tone Madison tie-in segment. Visiting WORT to work on that every week has given me a new appreciation for just how much work it takes to make local radio happen. A little more follow-through on showcasing in-studio performances would, if in a small way, help turn more people on to the important work our radio stations do, and perhaps even expand the audience for under-appreciated Madison musicians.

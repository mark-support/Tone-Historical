Madison calendar, October 23 through 29

Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
October 22, 2014 @ 8:49 am

events

Naomi Klein, Dave Chappelle, Wyatt Cenac, and more of the best events coming to Madison this week.

THURSDAY OCTOBER 23

Cinematheque: Videodrome. Chazen Museum of Art, 7 p.m.

The greatest of David Cronenberg's "insane flesh mutation" films, 1983's Videodrome follows a TV-station owner (James Woods) as he tries to track down the source of a program that shows people being tortured and murdered, descending into a nightmare world of sadomasochism, psychological warfare, and body-horror hallucinations.

Mastodon, Gojira, Kvelertak. Orpheum, 7:30 p.m.

On Mastodon's last two albums, 2011's The Hunter and 2014's One More Round The Sun, Mastodon still sounds like it's having fun and is even still curious about exploring new dimensions of burly prog-metal. But the overall result is pretty muddled compared to the brilliant, slashing fury of the band's first three albums, Remission, Leviathan, and Blood Mountain. Nothing against growing and evolving—2009's ponderous epic Crack The Skye holds up just fine, and a world with Mastodon is far better than a world without—but Mastodon is turning into one of those bands where the awesome early stuff really does overshadow the new stuff.

Dum Dum Girls, Ex Cops. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

As much as we loved the infectiously surfy, lo-fi pop-rock charms of 2010’s I Will Be, we can’t fault the Dum Dum Girls for trying to escape the horde of Best Coast and Vivian Girls soundalikes by adding shiny production and cranking out a record owing far more to the refined pop gems of The Pretenders than 60s pop groups like The Shangri-las in 2011’s excellent Only In Dreams. Unfortunately, this year’s Too True finds the Dum Dum Girls donning an overproduced, Muzak-goth, Siouxie Sioux makeover, proving that constantly reaching for the next retro vine isn’t the brightest plan for longevity. Either way, Dee Dee Penny’s massive voice will be worth the ticket alone, especially if tunes like “Jail La La,” “Bedroom Eyes,” or their brilliant cover of Big Star’s “September Gurls” make it into the set.

Bad Cinema: Nightmare On Elm Street 2. Central Library, 6:30 p.m.

Don’t get us wrong, we’re overjoyed that the Jack Sholder-directed Nightmare On Elm Street 2: Freddy’s Revenge is being screened anywhere at all, but we’re really not sure if it fits in the Madison Public Library’s Bad Cinema series, which has brought us such classics as The Car and Gymkata in recent months. But whatever, yeah, it is pretty bad. Basically, a new family moves into the Elm Street house and Freddy Krueger begins to possess a teenage boy’s body, using high school student Jesse Walsh (Mark Patton) as a host to kill people for him. Sure, it obviously gets a bit kitschy and cheesy at points: There’s a scene where lead actor Mark Patton inexplicably and oafishly dances around his room to Fonda Rae’s version of “Touch Me (All Night Long)” and there’s another where a man is essentially towel-whipped to death. It’s also probably the weirdest and most challenging watch of the Nightmare series. The film has been massively reanalyzed in recent years as being homophobic or some kind of statement about closeted homosexuality. In other words, this mess is definitely one worth checking out.

Ai Weiwei: The Fake Case. Union South Marquee, 9:30 p.m. Also 10/25, 6 p.m.

In this follow-up to Alison Klayman's 2012 documentary Ai Weiwei: Never Sorry, Danish filmmaker Andreas Johnsen follows the continued struggles of the Chinese artist, who's been repeatedly persecuted and detained for his provocative works and dogged activism. In this most recent chapter, Ai Weiwei strives to maintain his defiant spirit as the authorities bring a trumped-up tax-evasion case against him.

The God Damns, Captured! By Robots, Drunk Drivers, Helliphant. Frequency, 9 p.m.

Raucous Madison power-pop outfit The God Damns plays its first show in three years, along with Eau Claire stalwarts Drunk Drivers and the singularly twisted man-machine hybrid band Captured! By Robots.

FRIDAY OCTOBER 24

Oozing Wound, Yakuza, Running, Father Murphy, Waterworld, Tyranny Is Tyranny. Wisco, 9 p.m.

Chicago's Oozing Wound plays here behind the just-released, Earth Suck, their second album of scuzzy and black-humored thrash metal. Here they head up an abrasive bill that also boasts Chicago noise-rock miscreants Running and Italian experimental psych outfit Father Murphy.

Chris Smither. High Noon Saloon, 7 p.m.

Chris Smither's vocals and acoustic guitar work both embody a self-effacing charm and the confidence of long experience. He's been performing and writing songs since the mid-1960s, and is still adept at channeling folk and blues into records that deftly balance the smooth and the rugged, including the new Still On The Levee. And as a guitarist, he's never flashy but has mastered a layered, conversational style that makes his songs a pleasure to sink into.

Myrmidons, Steel Cranes, Mercury Tree. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m.

Madison trio Myrmidons is working on new recordings of its charmingly bizarre hybrid of surf-rock and math-rock. The band shares the bill here with Oakland rock outfit Steel Cranes and Portland's The Mercury Tree. 

Dave Chappelle. Orpheum, through Sept. 26, see link for showtimes (several are sold out)

First there was one Dave Chappelle show at the Orpheum, and then when that one sold out in a matter of minutes, there was another show, and then when that show sold out in a matter of slightly more minutes, there was yet another show, and on and on until there were six shows across three days. We can understand the appetite: Chappelle went years without doing much comedy before launching back into stand-up tours in 2013, and in the interval his previous work, including Chappelle’s Show, has taken its rightful place in the comedy canon. It would seem like half of Madison already has tickets, but there are still a few seats left for both of his Sunday shows (for now) if you’re a doofus who waited until the last second. If you wanna prep for his upcoming shows, dig this clip of him doing four (4!) hours at The Comic Strip back in 2009.

Wyatt Cenac, Phoebe Robinson. Majestic, 7:30 p.m.

October’s endless bounty of comedy in Madison rolls ever onward. Fans of The Daily Show (which is basically everyone in Madison, right?), might recognize Wyatt Cenac as a correspondent there from 2008 to 2012. Fans of easily offended news outlets might remember him from an episode of This American Life that excerpted a comedy bit of his about the first time he smoked weed. He’ll be playing here behind the newly released stand-up album Brooklyn, the follow up to 2011’s excellent Comedy Person. Comedy nerds who got shut out of tickets for Dave Chappelle’s shows in town owe it to themselves to get to this show if at all possible.

Cinematheque: The Strange Color Of Your Body’s Tears. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m.

SATURDAY OCTOBER 25

We Were Promised Jetpacks, The Twilight Sad. Majestic, 8 p.m.

Those Scots and the dreary, soaring indie-pop bands they keep sending over—they're actually pretty alright, at least on this bill. The cavernous, shoegaze-slathered The Twilight Sad and the relatively slick We Were Promised Jetpacks both have decent new records out this month—Nobody Wants To Be Here And Nobody Wants To Leave and Unravelling, respectively.

Cinematheque: Up To His Ears. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m.

SUNDAY OCTOBER 26

Cinematheque: Saboteur. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m.

MONDAY OCTOBER 27

The Color Of Noise. High Noon Saloon, 6 p.m.

The new film Color Of Noise is the first feature-length documentary to tell the story of legendary Minneapolis noise-rock label Amphetamine Reptile Records. The film centers around AmRep founder Tom Hazelmyer and features interviews with dozens of people involved in the label, including members of Melvins, Unsane, Lubricated Goat, Cows, and Helmet. Hazelmyer and Color Of Noise director Eric Robel will be present at this screening to hold a post-film talk and share some of Hazelmyer's artwork. Oh, and don't forget to read our interview with Robel.

Cinematheque: The Visitor (Stridulum). Union South Marquee, 7 p.m.

TUESDAY OCTOBER 28

Distinguished Lecture Series: Naomi Klein. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m.

With her 1999 book No Logo, Naomi Klein established herself as one of capitalism's most iconic and penetrating critics. Klein's new book, This Changes Everything, argues that to fight climate change, humanity must abandon an economic system enamored with greed and short-term growth.

Twillerama. High Noon Saloon, 7 p.m.

Twillerama is a festival of animated shorts "hosted" by Jeff Twiller and Randy Johnson, the hapless protagonists of an animated series by Twillerama curator Morgan Miller. Other filmmakers in the program include Signe Bauman, whose projects include frank and funny explorations of sex and depression; Kelsey Rose Stark, who has a playfully surreal, mixed-media feel; and Liesje Kraai, whose Twillerama selection "Moons" is billed as "a song for that little dog lost in space" and features music by Josh Ritter.

WEDNESDAY OCTOBER 29

Spotlight Cinema: Eastern Boys. Madison Museum Of Contemporary Art, 7 p.m.

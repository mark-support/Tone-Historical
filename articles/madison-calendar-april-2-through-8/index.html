Madison calendar, April 2 through 8

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
April 02, 2015 @ 11:32 am

Clockwise from left: Ta-Nehisi Coates speaks April 7 at the Union Theater; "Death Wish 3" screens April 6 at the Marquee; Gene Farris plays April 3 at the Cardinal Bar.

events

Ta-Nehisi Coates, Gene Farris, and more of the best stuff coming to Madison this week.

THURSDAY APRIL 2

Har-di-Har, Double Ewes, Lover's Spit. Frequency, 8 p.m.

Madison/Janesville outfit found a compelling and graceful balance between twangy, roots-inspired songwriting and warm electronic productions on last year's self-titled album. They've recently been working on a follow-up, and share the bill here with Madison rock duo Lover's Spit and whimsical St. Paul electronic-pop outfit Har-di-Har.


Central Cinema: La Haine. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

French filmmaker Mathieu Kassovitz's gritty 1995 drama La Haine follows three young men simmering in their status as outsiders in a Paris suburb. An Arab, and African, and a Jew, the main characters spend 24 hours roaming around against a backdrop of riots, police violence, and racist far-right politics.

FRIDAY APRIL 3

House of Love: Gene Farris, Steve Gerard, Wyatt Agard, Lovecraft, Izhai. Cardinal Bar, 9 p.m.

Over the past year, Madisonian DJs Wyatt Agard and Tim “Lovecraft" Thompson have filled the guest headliner slots of their House Of Love residency at The Cardinal (and previously at the now-closed Jolly Bob's) with an admirably varied and high-quality roster of (primarily) regional house and techno DJs and producers, from the likes of Chicago legend and longtime Smart Bar resident Derrick Carter to Madisonian industrial-techno slayer Kiazma. Veteran Chicago tracksmith and seasoned selector Gene Farris returns this week to House Of Love to hammer out another DJ set of infectious grooves, hopefully carrying a smorgasbord of his own mountainous back catalog of dirty, sample-laden deep-house, crisply pounding techno, and all the self-described tech-house resting in-between along with him.


Soul Dance Party, The Tea Heads. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m.

Madison DJs Tom Kay and Roadmaster will be reaching deep into their 45s to spin a night of R&B, with a break in there somewhere for a live set from Madison proto-rock howlers The Tea Heads.

Blind Chance. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Krzysztof Kieslowski's 1987 film Blind Chance—part of a newly restored set of Polish films curated by Martin Scorsese—explores three alternate/possible storylines that all branch off from the simple premise of a man running to catch a train.

SATURDAY APRIL 4

Belle And Sebastian, Honeyblood. Capitol Theater, 8 p.m.

Scottish outfit Belle And Sebastian's 1996 debut albums, Tigermilk and If You're Feeling Sinister, have held up beautifully as their current output begins to seem more and more trivial. Now, on this year's Girls In Peacetime Want To Dance, leader Stuart Murdoch and band still craft plenty of sly, swinging melodies, but the execution feels like a tepid middle ground between inoffensive indie-folk and inoffensive electronic pop. And nearly 20 years on, it's a bit sad to see a writer of Murdoch's gifts not branching out into, well, something beyond his tales of misfits told with a Morrissey-inspired mix of tenderness and schadenfreude. Sure, that's the charm of it, but the stuff Belle And Sebastian's writing now feels a long way from that first discovery of a bizarre little world where lonely kids make life-size models of the Velvet Underground in clay.


10 Rillington Place. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

British director Richard Fleischer's 1971 true-crime tale 10 Rillington Place stars Richard Attenborough as serial killer John Christie, who was executed in 1953 for killing at least six people in his London apartment. John Hurt co-stars as Christie's neighbor Timothy Evans, who in 1950 was wrongly executed for two of Christie's murders in what the British government later admitted was a miscarriage of justice.

SUNDAY APRIL 5

Taking Off. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

Czech filmmaker Milos Forman has a well-deserved and wildly varied reputation with Western audiences—it's a special director who can pull off One Flew Over The Cuckoo's Nest, Amadeus, The People Vs. Larry Flynt, and Man On The Moon as beautifully as Forman did. But his pre-Cuckoo's Nest work is pretty neglected here in America, particularly 1971's Taking Off, which stars Buck Henry and Lynn Carlin as a couple of suburban parents trying to experiment with the counterculture while searching for their runaway daughter. Screening here in a 35mm print, the film has never been released on home video in the U.S.

Al Scorch's Country Soul Ensemble, Jack Klatt. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

Chicago singer, songwriter and banjo player Al Scorch and his Country Soul Ensemble play thoughtful, dark and sometimes blisteringly fast bluegrass that often recalls the punk-infused acoustic attack of Split Lip Rayfield. But just as often, Scorch and band make room for atmosphere, character studies, and biting satire on the 2012 album Tired Ghostly Town.


MONDAY APRIL 6

Death Wish 3. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m.

Death Wish 3's position amongst the prestigiously inept Cannon Films legacy and its 6% rating on Rotten Tomatoes should be more than enough to reel you into director Michael Winner's brutal 1985 exit from the curiously resilient franchise. Following directly in the footsteps of Death Wish II's unnecessary, blood-soaked kaleidoscope of gunfire and sexual violence, Charles Bronson's merciless vigilante badass Paul Kersey goes to visit an old war buddy named Charley, who's of course murdered by a gang. Naturally, everything then goes to shit and Kersey kills a bunch of people. Here's hoping that Death Wish 4, the film where the franchise completely and hopelessly flies of the rails, will also be screened soon.

TUESDAY APRIL 7

Ta-Nehisi Coates. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m. (free)

In a media landscape awash in outrage-bait and self-important thinkpieces, Ta-Nehisi Coates' writing for The Atlantic has emerged as a patiently built island of absolutely vital discourse. His 2014 piece “The Case For Reparations" and 2012's “Fear Of A Black President" are among those rare journalistic pieces that immediately become essential to one's understanding of America's past and present. While he's best known for writing about race, Coates also has written extensively about everything from music to comics, so we're expecting a challenging and wide-ranging talk from Coates at this Distinguished Lecture Series event.

Jazz In Exile. Goodman South Madison Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Chuck France's 1982 documentary Jazz In Exile chronicles the history of African American jazz musicians who formed an expatriate community Europe, fleeing the discrimination and lack of artistic appreciation they found in the States. The film features interviews with a host of significant jazz figures, including saxophone player Dexter Gordon, bassist Richard Davis, and drummer Elvin Jones. Chuck France, who is based in Madison, will be at this event for a post-screening Q&A.

WEDNESDAY APRIL 8

Morality Crisis, Poney, Bereft, Tyranny Is Tyranny. Frequency, 9 p.m.

Minneapolis' Morality Crisis plays a charred, grandiose configuration of hardcore aggression and gnarled, dissonant melody. Much as it draws on technically warped heroes like Converge and Coalesce, Morality Crisis' approach also has a healthy amount of smart-assed attitude and gratifying sludge. The band plays here behind a new EP, Surrounded By Dweebs. Also on the bill are Wisconsin prog-metallers Poney, Madison metal outfit Bereft, and brutal post-rock outfit Tyranny Is Tyranny, who debuted a new song on Tone Madison this week.

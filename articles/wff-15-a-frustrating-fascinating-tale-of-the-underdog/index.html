WFF ’15: A frustrating, fascinating tale of the underdog

Chris Lay
@TotesChris
April 03, 2015 @ 11:07 am

Acrobat Maya Pawar in "Tomorrow We Disappear."

watch

"Tomorrow We Disappear" chronicles an Indian artists’ colony’s battle against developers.

"If something is precious, you shouldn't let it die." —Rahman Shah

We, as humans, want Rudy to get his shot at some time on the field. We want Gene Hackman to overcome his personal problems and take his team all the way to the state championship. And by God do we want the Tune Squad to rally in the final moments and beat the Monstars. Rooting for the underdog is in our DNA. It's that desire to see the low brought high that makes the Wisconsin Film Festival documentary Tomorrow We Disappear such a frustrating and simultaneously fascinating experience.

Tomorrow We Disappear, from co-directors Jim Goldblum and Adam M. Weber, tells the story of the world's largest street-artists' community, Dehli's Kathputli Colony, as its members fight to defend their homes from encroaching developers who have teamed up with the government to turn their colony into a high-rise.

While the Kathputli Colony is home to some 2,800 families, the filmmakers Goldblum and Weber selected three artists to focus on: Puran Bhatt, a puppeteer who takes on a significant role in the struggle to keep their homes; Maya Pawar, a teenage acrobat who can bend long metal rods with only her neck; and Rahman Shah, a magician who can't seem to put on a single show without running afoul of the cops. Shutting down a community is one thing, but shutting down a magic show mid-trick (can't you let the man at least get to the ta-da?!) is one of the, unexpectedly, cruelest things I've ever seen on film.

There's definitely a benefit of honing in on these three and get to know them a bit more intimately, but the overall effect for me was to lose sight of the forest by way of focusing on just a few of the trees. At no point in the film did I get a real good idea of the actual scale of the colony, or the real breadth of the talents contained therein.

If this film convinced me of anything, it's that the generalization that artists have no clue how to navigate bureaucracy is not merely a Western one. A lot of the footage during the lumpy middle section of the film is of infighting among the members of the colony (at least the few who managed to make it to the meetings with the developers) as to what should be done in the face of the very real possibility that, after years of unfulfilled threats, they will finally be uprooted from the slums they call home.

Ever since I read Mike D'Angelo's Citizenfour review, I've asked myself the same question he does regarding documentary films: "Why am I watching this instead of reading it?" Tomorrow We Disappear is long on things that would be better off left to print and photo journalism and sadly relatively short on actual footage of the street-art culture that is at risk of being ground out of existence.

The trailer offers, to great effect, a number of glimpses at colorful puppets and fantastic feats of wonder, showcasing the cultural legacy that's at stake here, but it features most of the best visuals and skips over the not-nearly-as-sexy business of meeting with government officials and developer representatives that concerns a good chunk of the film.


Luckily for the spoiler-averse, it's impossible to spoil the true third act of the film, since Kathputli Colony's profoundly worthy struggle is still unfolding before us in real time. The downside of this fact, is that the film as a discreet object ends with a bit of a whimper, sending us to a website to get the rest of the story.

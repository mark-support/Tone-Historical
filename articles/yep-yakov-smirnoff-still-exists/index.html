Yep, Yakov Smirnoff still exists

Chris Lay
@TotesChris
September 26, 2014 @ 8:34 am

read

Chris Lay ponders the now post-Soviet comic, who plays Friday at the Overture Center.

(Photo Credit: ADit Graphics.)

It's debatable as to whether or not we, as a country, ever needed Yakov Smirnoff to provide comic relief for the Cold War, and yet there he was—stuck to our collective shoe like so much toilet paper (“In Russia, toilet paper lines wait in YOU!") popping up in commercials and a predictably short-lived sitcom predictively named 'What A Country!" before peaking in 1988 as the entertainment at that year's White House Correspondents' Dinner.


Say what you will about his comedy, but he's had an interesting life and more than made the most of his 15 minutes. And he's playing this Friday at the Overture Center. UPDATE: Overture has announced that the show will be postponed because the disruption of flights through Chicago has hindered Smirnoff's travel plans. We'll let you know once the show is re-scheduled.

Yakov started his comedy career working cruise ships in the Black Sea before moving to America (What a country!), first in New York and then in LA, finding a home in Mitzi Shore's famous Comedy Store where he built his act and eventually uttered his famous catchphrase for the first time.

At the time he was living with Andrew Dice Clay of all people, who would later go on to star in 'The Adventures of Ford Fairlane' alongside an animatronic koala, and whose post-'80s career arc would not be dissimilar to Yakov's.

Of the top three most-viewed YouTube clips that pop up when you search “Yakov Smirnoff," only one is of him doing comedy. The top hit is a throw-away 'Family Guy' gag, and the third most-viewed clip is an early-'90's bit from 'The Ben Stiller Show' correctly pointing out that after the fall of the Berlin Wall, Yakov's “What a country!" schtick was dead in the water.


Somehow he made it through the '90s and the aughts, holing up in the theater he owns in Branson, Missouri, which. when filled to its 2,000 seat capacity would be holding just under one fifth of the city's entire population.

He tweaked his routine to be less culture clash and more war of the sexes, and he got a degree in “positive psychology," teaching a college course on the subject when he doesn't have a one-man show on Broadway or a deadline looming for his AARP Magazine column.

So, is Yakov Smirnoff a good comedian? At one time... maybe kinda sorta.

His jokes are at best two dimensional and rely on relatively simple wordplay and the novelty of a catchphrase he must've sold his soul at the crossroads to have gotten so much longevity from.

Despite the amount of politics around the reason for his very existence as an entertainer, he refuses to take sides, which cuts the teeth out of his whole act while broadening his audience to include those who have literally replaced their teeth with dentures.

All that said, do I want to see Yakov Smirnoff live this weekend? Absolutely.

From everything I can tell, Yakov seems to have morphed into a motivational speaker who waxes humorously on the topic of relationships.

Imagine John “Men Are from Mars, Women Are from Venus" Gray twisted up with Gallagher (the good one) but replace their smarm with a naive enthusiasm, and substitute the sledge-o-matic with an impassioned and occasionally tearful speech about him becoming an American citizen and you get the idea of the sort of show you're gonna see on Friday.


While the crowd he attracts might look a lot like the same folks who would attend a Blue Collar Comedy comedy show, Yakov has more patriotic integrity than all of those doofuses combined.

Watch a few of his interviews, and you get the very real sense that Yakov is honestly and sincerely deeply happy to be in America and he's never taken his freedom here for granted having experienced life in the Eastern Bloc.

The guy paid $100,000 to put a mural of The Statue of Liberty with an American Flag in the shape of a heart that he painted himself (“a pointillist-style piece, with one brush-stroke for each victim of the attacks") on the side of a building overlooking the World Trade Center rubble, going so far as to humbly insist that his name not be associated with it publicly.

Your move, Bill Engvall.

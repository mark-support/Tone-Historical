Not your average citizens: Color Of Noise director Eric Robel

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
October 21, 2014 @ 5:44 am

watch

The new documentary about Amphetamine Reptile Records screens Oct. 27 at the High Noon Saloon.

Eric Robel's new film Color Of Noise is the first feature-length documentary to tell the story of Amphetamine Reptile Records, a Minneapolis label that during the '80s and '90s put out one of the great bodies of noisy underground music. The film centers on AmRep founder Tom Hazelmyer and his gift for bringing together a diverse roster of miscreants, from performance-art-inflected Midwesterners Cows and God Bullies to the brutally austere New York outfits Helmet and Unsane to Australian band Lubricated Goat to Nashville's epically brutal Today Is The Day. Robel and Hazelmyer will visit the High Noon Saloon on October 27 to screen a two-hour cut of the film, showcase some of Hazelmyer's artwork, and hold a Q&A.

Color Of Noise is very much a fan's documentary. Robel (who is based in New York City and used to play in Cows frontman Shannon Selberg's other band, Heroine Sheiks) seems to approach his subjects with a raffish camaraderie, delighting in Hazelmyer's predilection for guns, meat, and political incorrectness. But while Robel doesn't exactly puncture the larger-than-life personas Hazelmyer and many of his collaborators project, he's also taken a very thorough look at the tenacity and business acumen that made AmRep successful. Color Of Noise is Robel's first film as a director; he says his next project will also involve underground music from the '80s and '90s, but won't say more than that. Ahead of the screening, Robel spoke with me about making the film and the challenges of interviewing Jello Biafra.

Tone Madison: As a fan and someone who's played with some of the people in the film, did you learn anything while that surprised you while making the film?

Eric Robel: I guess one thing would be—I knew some of these people, and they were already friends—but admittedly, the entire cast of this story are not your average citizens. The biggest surprising thing for me was how warm and really nice, gentle people most of these guys were. From a fan's point of view, you would be very intimidated by some of these bands, especially going to see them 20 years ago. it was really impactful stuff to see. So sitting down and talking to someone personally, you really get to uncover and see the gentle human side of them.

Tone Madison: It's clear from a lot of the interviews that you're having fun with a lot of these people and the interviews are pretty good-natured, but are you also trying to present sort of an unvarnished picture of what they're like too?

Eric Robel: No, not particularly or specifically, but definitely, one of my goals for this film, while I was interviewing, was to try and pull some outlandish stuff. I really wanted to get these guys to tell me stuff that was, you know, not very PC. I wanted to hear all the dirt—I guess a little bit of an exploitative side of their stories. I knew it would be great entertainment, so getting all that stuff was playing a delicate balanced role of using some of that material without offending anybody. It was kind of like, pull what you can and try to make it as funny as possible.

Tone Madison: Who ended up being the most challenging interview?

Eric Robel: I think Jello Biafra was probably a challenging one, because he's much more of a professional than I am by any means. Even being interviewed, he's just been interviewed so many times over the past 30 years that half of my questions were just, "Nope, I'm not answering that one. Next question, please." So he was challenging in a professional way. He was teaching me a little something about direction.

Tone Madison: This being your first feature, what did it teach you about making films, and what are you thinking about what you'd like to do next?

Eric Robel: I work very methodically and I'm a little anal in the way I work, so I was overdoing everything. So I guess what I learned best was I don't need to overdo everything next time. It was like, every interview, three hours long, two cameras, back it up. My editorial probably would've gone a little faster if I'd been more efficient with the thought process. Fortunately, I had a really good editor who was able to help me sort out things quickly.

Tone Madison: You've said in previous interviews that you first got into AmRep while growing up in Baltimore and discovering Helmet and bands like that. So it's interesting that you come to this from an East Coast perspective, because in the Midwest there's a lot of pride attached to things like AmRep and Touch & Go being part of this region. What were some of the reasons AmRep stuck with you over the years?

Eric Robel: I've always been attracted to alternative cinema, alternative art, alternative music, everything that's kind of pushing the envelope. I think that's what Hazelmyer's whole thing was about. He likes really visceral, impacting music and art, and everything in his life is kind of that way, and I share some of those same attributes.

Tone Madison: What other plans do you have for promoting the film?

Eric Robel: One of my goals is to try and get some kind of regular short-run theatrical release. The film we're showing on this tour, the reason we're doing it is really for the fans. We're showing a director's cut that's about a half-hour longer than the theatrical cut. We have lots of plans for extras as well, not only just complete live performances but new interviews that I'm conducting. There are stories behind the film that aren't apparent when you watch the film.

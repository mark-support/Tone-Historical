Madison calendar, May 7 through 13

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
Chris Lay

Clockwise from left: Poncho Sanchez plays the Union Theater on May 9; Tom Brosseau plays the Shitty Barn on May 13; Paul Johnson plays the Cardinal on May 8.

events

Tom Brosseau, Poncho Sanchez, Busking for Books, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY MAY 7

Vetiver, Eric Johnson. Frequency, 8 p.m.

When Vetiver mastermind Andy Cabic emerged from the psych-folk ether in 2004 with his self-titled debut, many heads were turned by his raw, breathy folk tunes and the rich, tasteful harmonies that colored them in. However, what nabbed the lion's share of attention (and perhaps what helped make the album so great), was Vetiver's insane cast of guest musicians, which included Mazzy Star's Hope Sandoval, My Bloody Valentine's Colm O'Ciosoig, Joanna Newsom, and Cabic's biggest supporter and co-signer, Devendra Banhart. Unfortunately, the last decade's worth of Vetiver albums found the songwriter trying to twist the explorative and spacious tunes of that great first record into an overproduced, Muzak-ready, and financially viable balloon animal. This year's Complete Strangers, while coated in lush, pristine production and musicianship, frays off the loose threads of “Angels' Share" or “Oh Papa" and ultimately sounds like the work of a guy trying to jam the Time Life AM Gold collection through the indie-rock lens to sleepy results. Who knows? Maybe the live set will pump a bit more urgency into the newer tunes.

The Limits Of Control. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

There was a lot of well-deserved “return to form" accolades paid to Jim Jarmusch's most recent film, Only Lovers Left Alive, and it's true that Jarmusch's decade of work that preceded it was, professionally speaking, seemingly a frustrating one. His films three films from the aughts garnered mixed reviews, culminating with 2009's The Limits Of Control. While the NY Post meant “This is one of those movies that's too cool to have a plot" as a criticism in their review of Limits, in all honestly they ended up pretty much nailing Jarmusch's apparent aesthetic intentions. Sure the plot is practically a sketch of a hint of a whisper and the characters speak in sometimes circular little riddles, but the film, shot in various locations around Spain, is gorgeous, and the thrumming soundtrack by Japanese metal outfit Boris will make even the most solidly bolted down theater seats start to rattle.

FRIDAY MAY 8

Daikaiju, Knuckel Drager, Fire Retarded, Roboman. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m.

In a summit of over-the-top masked surf-rock spectacle, Madison band (and onetime Exene Cervenka backing band) Knuckel Drager shares the bill here with Huntsville, Alabama's Daikaiju, who infuse their own take on surf instrumentals with a sense of prog-rock drama and kitschy Japanese monster movie imagery. Knuckel Drager member Rob Oman will play a set in his one-man, surf-punk project Roboman, and Madison garage-punk standbys Fire Retarded will be on the bill as well.


House Of Love with Paul Johnson, Wyatt Agard, DJ Lovecraft, Tre Ginjahvitiz, Dave J. Cardinal Bar, 9 p.m.

Chicago producer and DJ Paul Johnson has earned himself a place in house-music history with a series of singles during the 1990s, culminating in the dancefloor hit “Get Get Down." Johnson had the distinction of spinning at the last House of Love night held at the now-closed Jolly Bob's, and returns here for what we're anticipating will be another deep-reaching DJ set.


Celebrity + Okay, America! Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Cinematheque's double feature on Friday pays tribute to the prolific American filmmaker Tay Garnett, who launched his career during the silent era. First up is his 1928 silent boxing comedy Celebrity, which will be accompanied with live piano from David Drazin (who specializes in silent-film scores) and will screen on a special 35mm print from the Library of Congress. After that comes Garnett's 1932 film Okay, America!, a biting tale of politics and cynicism based on the career of journalist Walter Winchell.

Crown Larks, William Z. Villain, Mr Jackson. Memorial Union Terrace, 9 p.m. (free)

SATURDAY MAY 9

Busking for Books. State Street, 1 p.m.

This annual fundraiser for Madison-based Literacy Network, which helps Dane County adults with basic literacy training and English-language classes for non-native speakers, has grown into one of Madison's most charming spring traditions. At corners up and down State Street, local musicians will put on all manner of acoustic performances, busking for donations that will all go to support Literacy Network's efforts. Each year brings familiar Madison acts and unpredictable one-offs—highlights in the schedule this time around include small-instrument-wielding charmers The Tiny Band, German Art Students singer/guitarist Annelies Howell performing solo, the Forward! Marching Band, and multi-dimensional loon DB Pedersen.

Tyranny Is Tyranny, Smoke Signals, Sinking Suns, High Priests. Mr Robert's, 8:30 p.m.

Kentucky post-punk trio Smoke Signals balance delicate melodies with dexterously contained aggression on the 2013 EP The Collapse. Even as the band swings through a complex blend of styles, from the understated math-rock of “Sentinels" to the burly riffs of “Collapse," it finds strength in compactness and efficiency, paring each song down to tight, twisting figures and eerie yet vulnerable vocals. On this show that makes for a good pairing with Chicago band High Priests, who play full-on unhinged and lurching noise-rock. The similarly brutal and austere Madison outfit Sinking Suns and bleak post-rock outfit Tyranny Is Tyranny share the bill here.


Poncho Sanchez And His Latin Jazz Band, Tony Castañeda's Latin Jazz Group. Wisconsin Union Theater, 8 p.m.

A masterful percussionist and veteran bandleader, the Texas-born Poncho Sanchez has also become an expansive ambassador of Latin jazz, blending its fleet, funky rhythms with colors and inspirations from across the jazz spectrum, also incorporating ideas from R&B. Sanchez's discography as a bandleader stretches back to the late '70s, when he was still playing with vibraphonist Caj Tjader's band, and his most recent studio release, Chano Y Dizzy, is a collaboration with trumpeter Terence Blanchard that pays tribute to Dizzy Gillespie's collaborations with early Latin jazz innovator Chano Pozo.


SUNDAY MAY 10

MayDay MayLay. Alliant Energy Center, 5 p.m.

Whoo boy, this one's a doozy. We live in a sad, twisted world where thrash-metal legends Anthrax—who boast not only 34 years of existence, but also Scott Ian's immortal goatee—are somehow billed below bland, whiny angst-rockers Three Days Grace and Copenhagen, Denmark's premiere barbed-wire tat-rockers Volbeat. We have so many questions, like--are they really gonna give a band with 10 albums a pathetic, 30-minute opening slot? Will the set largely be dominated by tunes from the band's upcoming album? If you're going for Anthrax, is the show really worth the $40-plus price tag? At least those going for Belladonna and company won't be forced to sit through Volbeat's “Heaven Nor Hell," which effortlessly blends everything that's wrong with The Offspring with everything we hate about Nickelback. Maybe you'll also get a chance to buy a bag from Anthrax drummer Charlie Benante's new Benante's Blend coffee line. We'll leave it to you to weigh the pros and cons, but we'd suggest holding out for an Anthrax headlining tour.

TUESDAY MAY 12

Marilyn Manson, Knee High Fox. Orpheum, 8 p.m.

Since the early nineties, we've gotten to watch Marilyn Manson shapeshift into a full cast of bizarro cartoon characters—from a Wonka-obsessed sleaze-rocker to a bustier-donning, self-described antichrist to an androgynous Bowie-worshipping glam-rocker to a grill-sporting goth baller. Manson's latest full-length, The Pale Emperor, finds the dark one returning to form with a modified Hitler Youth haircut (oh, that Manson and his penchant for appropriating fascist imagery!) and further stripping out the industrial and metal elements of his heyday to play jangly biker-rock (seriously, fucking listen to “Birds Of Hell Awaiting" and tell us it doesn't remind you of Alannah Myles' “Black Velvet") for the melodramatic Hot Topic set. While the new material won't even come close to touching the relevance of 1996's Antichrist Superstar, an expertly packaged and genuinely creepy blend of Trent Reznor-produced industrial-metal and glam-rock that amazingly pushed millions of impressionable teens to question the existence of God, it's still probably worth grabbing a ticket to see his notoriously intense live show.

WEDNESDAY MAY 13

Tom Brosseau, John Davey. Shitty Barn (Spring Green), 7 p.m.

North Dakota native Tom Brosseau feels like he's off in another world from everyone else in his generation who plays folk songs, and that world's at once more bleak and more wondrous. His persona strikes a rarified balance of whimsy, self-deprecation and stoicism, something that served him well even going back to early albums like 2007's Grand Forks. Brosseau's recent work still brings that balance across, while subtly expanding the emotions and narratives he takes on. On this year's Perfect Abandon, co-produced by occasional PJ Harvey collaborator John Parish, Brosseau begins with “Hard Luck Boy," a story of a boy being abandoned by his mother in a department store, but even there, he's at once unflinching and taking everything in wistful stride. And on tracks like “Island In The Prairie Sea," Brosseau reminds us he's still a master of gentle but transportive storytelling.


Food For Thought, Food For Life. Central Library, 6:30 p.m.

Still life with Yes(head)

DB Pedersen
@dbpederseno
July 27, 2014 @ 2:58 pm

read

Madison musician DB Pedersen reflects on Yes' July 25, 2014 show at Overture Hall.

(Editor’s Note: DB Pedersen is a musician, throat singer and cosmic mischief-maker here in Madison, and also the biggest Yes nut I know. So I asked him to review the British prog band’s show this past Friday at the Overture Center, during which they performed the 1972 albums Fragile and Close To The Edge in their entirety along with some of the obligatory hits and some new songs. Here is DB’s report in mostly unfiltered form. DB performs and records under his own name and in a variety of collaborative settings. Follow him on SoundCloud and Twitter. —Scott Gordon)

During Yes’ show Friday at the Overture Center, I was transported across 42 years of my veteran saga of fandom, though with somewhat of a View-Master effect.

I could be a snot and say that I've never seen a bigger collection of air-guitar aficionados—the intelligentsia meets ‘Wayne's World’—if it weren't for the fact that a friend once told me of my own bass playing: “I can tell you're a big Chris Squire fan.”

I'll indulge a comparison to Yes’ 2013 set set at the Wisconsin State Fair at the motor speedway, which would have orbited the moon if they had only been opening for “Puppet Show,” but I even enjoyed that one as well.

True story: if it had ever dawned on me that being in a Yes cover band greatly increased one's chances to actually be in Yes, I think I would have taken a different river a few decades back. I'm a mimic, and I spent my (stoned) childhood belting away at Yes tunes and teaching myself what I didn't know was vocalese by imitating the arpeggiation of the instrumental parts.While replacement frontman Jon Davison can meet and even exceed original vocalist Jon Anderson's range, I think Davison’s timbre is that of a penny-whistle playing an oboe's part. Davison certainly fits the bill enough in hippie archetype that, through cataracts and tinnitus, one might feel in the presence of Anderson long enough to vegetatively hold on to the dream of dying during “Total Mass Retain,” from Close To The Edge.

While redolent with much of Roger Dean's decades-long contribution of artwork, the visual displays during Friday’s show were limited to Jumbotronic homages and efficient LED usage, which certainly saved Yes the cost of a phalanx of roadies and Hurst-tools (Jaws of Life) to trot out Neil DeGrasse Tyson-based nebulae fiberglass monstrosities of sculpture, the kind that Yes band made their name on, perhaps preferring to wear their dinosaur skin as a loose garment. I commented to my friend during the conspicuously Stravinsky-less opening montage of “a look back” at all of Yes’ album covers (do I scream “FUCK YEAH!” for ‘Tormato’?), that this sequence was “very PB-Yes, if you asked me.”

Look, the opening number, ‘Siberian Khatru,’ is definitely the subconscious root cause of my becoming a Siberian-esque poly tonal singer and nature mimic. At the age of 12, I had so much THC releasing from my fat cells and this music blasting through my headphones that of course I would become this music. Perhaps I'm attacking providence, but it's good clean fun. This is how I show my love to my teachers, thankful that I don't have the scars left over from my poorly executed Yes logo tattoo to begrudgingly explain to anyone who might see me shirtless (bullet dodged there). A friend of mine once went hauling ass through the Jose Marti airport in Havana, Cuba, to chase after Rick Wakeman solely for MY love of Yes. He asked, “What the hell are you doing in Havana?” and “Didn't you used to wear a silver cape when you toured with Yes?” Sir Richard amicably replied: “The ministry of culture flies me and my mates out here every year to play 'The Six Wives of Henry VIII,’ and yes, I still wear a cape, but now it's gold!”

Fittingly, the sound experience on Friday was of a Yes-like caliber (and really, the band that appeared Friday at the Oveture is the best Yes cover band to date) and Steve Howe and Chris Squire turned in wonderful performances. I had to snicker, however, that Squire had to visually cue someone off stage to make the loop-sample for “Schindleria Praematurus” in lieu of using a tool I take for granted: a foot pedal.

Speaking of foot pedals, Davison missed his for the 1st “Waaaah!” of Close To The Edge, a harmonizer, I can only assume, as the note that came out was like the woeful bleat of a reed of grass blown over the end of a garden hose. He spared us the “hose-again” torture by nailing it the next two times, thank god.

Another technical oddity, in my opinion, was that since Yes directly sampled Wakeman's opening to “Close To The Edge,” seamlessly going into the song from a strong starting point, why couldn't they have done the same with “Five Percent For Nothing,” the opener of side two of ‘Fragile’? Although a friend pointed out later that drummer Alan White appeared ill of health onstage, his donning headphones to get through a 31-second piece written and performed originally by Bill Bruford portended a woeful rendition that should have been left on the bus outside the theater. The sound of the needle dropping on the vinyl broadcast through the PA would've taken every warrior stoner and veteran listener to that magical shangri-la of the first time they'd ever heard it, letting the band kick “Long Distance Runaround” squarely through the bong-end goal posts for a perfect score.

With keyboard player Geoff Downes in the mix, I thrilled at my teenage dream that this “Heaven and Earth and greatest hits” tour might pull one out of left field and lay down at least one track from ‘Drama.’ “Tempus Fugit” would have been an apt tribute to the author right there on the stage, but we're in Hits/FM country, so “I've Seen All Good People” would have to suffice.

I was shoehorned out of my seat for an encore with “Owner Of A Lonely Heart,” because a bald eagle with a snake in its talons had been circling over me at the bus stop the day before (“Dude; It's a sign!”), and for the first time, I watched Steve Howe “own” this song's guitar solo, which he's customarily relegated to any of the keyboard players over the years. It's good to know that one can break through a barrier, even at the age of 67, and make it one’s anthem before falling off the edge with his snobbery intact.

The middle two tunes, between the two bookends of Yes’ “classic” material were from the album they released just this week, called um, er... Big Unremarkable? [Ed: It’s Heaven And Earth.] I simply haven't paid much, if any, attention to what Yes has been doing for the last 10 years. Sorry. I only go on these journeys to check the efficacy of my spiritual breathing apparatus and hopefully come out with a clearer head than befits my fragile child's mind (that's getting into Jim Morrison country).

I guess a lot of us old bags don't need to be visually cued by spinning yoginis with christmas tree chakra lights to know why we're here. After all, we surrender our souls all over Madison, attending warrior trainings, kirtan, toning, ritual, pagan celebrations, meditations and whatnot. WE GET IT. We spun like Sufis on the cover of Be Here Now on the coffee table every chance we got while ecstatically screaming along to "Heart of the Sunrise." Our parents or landlords replaced the carpets we ruined with incense burns fully four times Ten True Summers ago, but maybe it's an extension for the kiddies who might ask in their first epiphany: “Does Sponge Bob have a high vibration, too?”

I guess I miss the good old days when Yes shows were opened with a couple of classic Merrie Melodies cartoons. 5,000 people at Winterland laughing at “Monsters are the most in-teresting people!” through a spotlit hash-curtain will never happen again, but people actually get a kick out of bringing the kiddos. I'm glad it was me.

Madison calendar, February 5 through 11

events

Julianna Barwick, Con Solo, Demetri Martin, Paper Wasp, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY FEBRUARY 5

Crash. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Based on the J.G. Ballard novel of the same name, David Cronenberg's Crash stars James Spader as James Ballard, a man who develops a sexual appetite for car wrecks after getting into a head-on collision that ends up killing the other driver and injuring his wife Dr. Helen Remington (played by Holly Hunter), who survives the crash. Immediately after the incident, Ballard and Remington inexplicably get turned on by the situation, start an affair, and begin a creepy journey deep into their new fetish. If we're being honest, the unsettling kinks of Crash would probably be more comfortably digested alone in a bedroom. However, if you're feeling bold and in need of a severe mindfuck, this movie rules.

Love Savers: Mr. Jackson, William Z. Villain. The Wisco, 9 p.m.

Two of Madison's most lovably eccentric acts, synth-powered R&B artist Mr. Jackson and cracked gypsy-jazz outfit William Z. Villain, team up for an early Valentine's Day show of sorts, billed as “Love Savers." William Z. Villain will be giving its first full-band performance in a while, featuring drummer Ramon Gooden, and the set will include some new material, plus some covers of love songs from the 1920s and more. Mr. Jackson, whose album The Golden Hour Groove Session made our best Madison records of 2014 list, might have some surprises and collaborations in store as well.

Daniel Sloss. Comedy Club On State, through Feb. 7, see link for all showtimes

Scottish comedian Daniel Sloss is something of a brogue wunderkind, hitting the big time in his home country with his Jimmy-Carr-meets-Bo-Burnam-on-caffeine stylings before he was even out of his teens. We get the feeling that Sloss's first real forays into American markets this year are setting the scene for a larger spread if his brand of awkwardly delivered comedy sticks. With any luck, we'll be seeing more of him, but he's got some dues to pay round these parts still.

FRIDAY FEBRUARY 6

Demetri Martin. Barrymore, 7 p.m.

Comedy Bang Bang has made alternative absurdist comic performance art a nearly commonplace experience, but Demetri Martin has been doing it longer than anyone around. Prop comedy with a giant notepad became his signature closer ages ago, but he also integrates music into his act in ways that you won't find in a conventional club show. No telling whether he'll be transitioning to reading tweets on stage as part of a move to all-digital farce, but he seems to be well on his way to adapting his visual wordplay to tinier screens. Perhaps this Barrymore show will feature some new SnapChat-based material?

Wizard World/Comic Con. Alliant Energy Center, through Feb. 8, see link for full schedule

This lumbering beast of a package-deal touring convention makes a Madison stop, featuring appearances from Bruce Campbell, William Shatner, Eliza Dushku, Edward James Olmos, and other luminaries (plus not-really-luminaries) of the sci-fi/cult movies/fantasy/comics realms. We've got a deep-digging preview from Chris Lay.

Julianna Barwick, Muuny. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 8 p.m. (free)

Julianna Barwick's one of electronic music's most gentle voices, but also one of its most compelling, building delicately layered compositions that primarily use loops of harmonically rich, wordless vocals (full disclosure: we helped put on her last Madison show). Her most recent release, last year's Rosabi EP, was released in partnership with the great craft brewery Dogfish Head, along with a collaborative new brew of the same name, but that doesn't mean there's anything gimmicky about it—though it apparently samples the sounds of Dogfish Head's brewing equipment, Barwick's meditative, swooping vocal phrases still tastefully predominate.


Night Light: Twin Peaks party. Central Library, 8 p.m. (free)

The Central Library's Night Light series is pulling out just about all the geeky stops in this celebration of David Lynch's endlessly weird, gorgeous, soulful show Twin Peaks. Brad Dukes, author of an oral history of the show, will give a talk, and a crew of local musicians including Julian Lynch and members of Lovely Socialite will perform interpretations of the show's score. (Hear more about that in our interview with musician Brian Grimm.) Tandem Press will be showcasing prints by Lynch himself, and artist Craig Grabhorn has prepared a Lynch-style design that patrons can have printed on bags or shirts. The night will also feature coffee, donuts, pie, and drinks from Forequarter.

Love Streams. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

UW Cinematheque's celebration of the output of Cannon Films continues with one of John Cassevetes' final films, 1984's Love Streams, which stars Cassevetes and Gena Rowlands as a pair of siblings struggling with alcoholism, divorce, and a few frivolous animal purchases.

Eric and Heather ChanSchatz: 22nd Century Opening Celebration. Madison Museum Of Contemporary Art, 6 p.m. (free)

New York-based artists Eric and Heather ChanSchatz, who specialize in creating works informed by collaborations with communities around the world, will celebrate their new large-scale exhibition at MMOCA with help from Madison Afro-Peruvian jazz band Golpe Tierra and eclectic Madison bluegrass outfit No Name Stringband. At 6:30, the artists will give a talk on their new works.

The Flavor That Kills, Paper Wasp, Negative Example. Mickey's Tavern, 10:30 p.m. (free)

Late in December, Madison band Paper Wasp quietly released an EP called Hemlock, which weaves together respectable, moody rock from Steve Shah's drums, Chris Mohar's bass, and Justin Sprecher's keyboards. The EP's strongest moments include twisty instrumentals “Dark Current" and “Destruction Myth," but vocal-centered songs like “Golem" also come through nicely, in bursts of restrained but cathartic post-punk. Here Paper Wasp shares the bill with soul-rockers The Flavor That Kills, who released a new album, Pablo's Hippo's, last fall, and former Tar Babies frontman Bucky Pope's latest project, Negative Example.


SATURDAY FEBRUARY 7

Con Solo, Coby Ashpis. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. (free)

On 2014's The Crimson Future: Part One (released by the Madison-based Catch Wreck imprint), Milwaukeean beatsmith Con Solo melts layers of lush, ambient chord voicings and entrancing melodies over detailed, downtempo grooves, pushing the textural mindplay of Tangerine Dream or Windy And Carl through a contemporary experimental hip-hop lens. Though occasionally dipping into breakbeat territory with the gorgeous “The City Of Gemini" or post-FlyLo stagger of “Dream Manifestation," Solo (a.k.a. Brandeaux Calrissian) maintains a nocturnal, moody pulse throughout. Also on the bill is Madison-based maximalist producer and sample acrobat Coby Ashpis, whose new EP Loafer is the first significant local release of 2015. Read more in our recent interview with Ashpis.

Gardener, Kyle Landstra, Conjuror. Good Style Shop, 8 p.m.

One of Chicago's finest contemporary new-age synth jammers, Kyle Landstra had quite a prolific year in 2014, which included a whopping six releases. However, our favorite of the six is the sparkling slab of blissful textures and soaring leads Landstra contributed to a split with The Original Flowering Earth on German tape label Sicsic. Landstra's side is split into two movements, "Rising Into The Fog" and “We Communicated Through Beams Of Light," with the former finding Landstra sticking to what he does best—cosmic drones with crawling melodies that freeze time as they spin around the listener's head. Madison-based synth-whisperer and improvisor Conjuror, who spoke with us recently, will offer a far darker and dronier take on meditative synthesis with his opening set.

Downfall Of Gaia, Zebras, Bereft, The Central. The Wisco, 9 p.m.

Perhaps it's unfair to suggest that the members of Germany's Downfall Of Gaia (who are currently scattered across Berlin, Hamburg, and New York City) come from the same ilk of so many other artists that traversed beyond the exhausted metalcore landscape to venture deeper into the frozen sonic climes of doom and black metal, but we certainly got the inkling when we were pummeled by the progressive filth of 2014's Aeon Unveils The Thrones Of Decay. This certainly isn't a sleight or accusation to DOG, but we can definitely hear it in the crushing precision of drummer Michael Kadnar's double-kick drum attack, as it syncopates and blasts below the bleak riffing of guitarists Dominik Goncalves dos Reis and Peter Wolff throughout the album, not to mention the atonal guitar shrieks of “To Carry Myself To The Grave" or the depressive half-time groove and subsequent breakdown in “Of Stillness And Solitude." While some purists may nitpick and whine about bastardization, sometimes it's awfully nice to hear a couple genres—ones that all too often get really same-y and formulaic—get a fresh, technically proficient spin, and there's still plenty of throat-busting vocals, reverb, and bleak dynamics present here to keep the extreme metal folks happy. This one's definitely worth checking out.

Othello. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Orson Welles had a long and rich relationship with the works of Shakespeare, from his early days as a stage actor to his never-realized plans later in life to make a film of King Lear. His 1952 adaptation of Othello, starring himself in the title role, is regarded as one of the finest film version of a Shakespeare play.

SUNDAY FEBRUARY 8

Micro-Wave Cinema: Butter On The Latch, Thou Was Mild And Lovely. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

The scrappy local independent-film series Micro-Wave Cinema gets its spring season going with this double-header of short features by Josephine Decker, who will also be giving a post-screening Q&A via Skype.

Russ Johnson Quartet. Brink Lounge, 3 p.m.

A jazz trumpeter who currently lives in Milwaukee but has found an artistic home in Chicago, Russ Johnson plays here with the Chicago-based musicians who joined him on the solid 2014 album Meeting Point: drummer Tim Daisy, bassist Anton Hatwich, and bass clarinet player Jason Stein. Yes, it's an afternoon show at the Brink (we swear there's free continental breakfast in that place somewhere), but the brash, explorative originals and improvisations on Meeting Point make it more than worth turning out. Read more in our recent interview with Johnson.


Polka Press Open House. 1019 Williamson St., 1 p.m. to 5 p.m. (free)

Madison printmaking collective Polka Press celebrates moving into its new workspace on Willy Street (under Nature's Bakery) with an afternoon of drinks, snacks, prints and other works for sale, and the chance to print your own Valentine.

For Love And Gold. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m.

In the second of two preliminary rounds, the annual stand-up competition will plow through the last half of the 82 contestants who signed up this year. Read our preview for more.

Madison calendar, March 26 through April 1

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
March 25, 2015 @ 1:04 pm

Clockwise from left: ZZ Top plays Overture Hall on March 30; DVS1 plays the High Noon on March 27; "Pharaoh" screens at Vilas Hall on Friday.

events

DVS1, Brown Derby's final Crystal Corner gig, a D.C. punk documentary, and more.

THURSDAY MARCH 26

Wisconsin's Mining Standoff. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Director Brad Lichtenstein and his Milwaukee-based 371 Productions made the finest documentary yet about Wisconsin in the Scott Walker era, 2012's As Goes Janesville—which took an assertive public-interest political stance, but not at the expense of deep-digging, compellingly paced filmmaking. 371 followed up in 2014 with the short documentary Wisconsin's Mining Standoff, which premiered last summer on Al Jazeera. The documentary looks at how mining company Gogebic Taconite wielded political influence to change the state's mining laws in a bid to dig a huge iron mine in Ashland and Iron Counties in northern Wisconsin. While GTAC has since called time-out on the project, it's still a noxious example of the state of legislative politics and environmental protections in Wisconsin This screening, presented by Madison film blog LakeFrontRow, will be followed by a discussion with 371's Devon Coupery and Sierra Club activist Dave Blouin.

FRIDAY MARCH 27

DVS1. High Noon Saloon, 10 p.m.

With Minneapolis' Zak Khutoretsky's (a.k.a. DVS1's) rich history of brain-massaging DJ sets and tasteful synth-and-drum-machine-powered techno releases on prestigious labels ranging from Berghain resident Ben Klock's Klockworks to Detroit legend Derrick May's Transmat label to his own HUSH imprint, this is a great show for all the local heads to bring along anyone who hasn't ventured far from the confines of snoozy tech-house or over-polished and heavily compressed EDM. It's also worth noting that there aren't any openers or extra DJs on this show, so DVS1 will be providing a mighty three-to-four hour journey all his own.


Pharaoh. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Polish director Jerzy Kawalerowicz's 1966 epic Pharaoh chronicles the political intrigue of Egypt in the 12th century B.C. Adapted from Polish writer Bolesław Prus' novel for the same title, the film follows a young prince as he schemes against Egypt's high priests for political power and takes the country to war. The film screens here in a new digital version, restored to its original three-hour length.

SATURDAY MARCH 28

Czarbles, Myrmidons, Transformer Lootbag. Mickey's Tavern, 10:30 p.m.

We at Tone Madison can't pretend to write at all objectively about Madison trio Czarbles. Bassist Matt Skemp, guitarist Mark Sauer, and drummer Jeff Sauer make up one of our favorite bands, and they have had way too many years to scramble our brains with short but fiercely dense and complex math-rock instrumentals. Speaking of too many years, this show will mark the band's first release since its 2007 self-titled debut album. The new Nauseau Trois, recorded in 2012 at Electrical Audio in Chicago, spans all of six songs and 12 and a half minutes. Highlights "Our Old Home" and "Hel" seek out further permutations on the band's meticulous approach to songwriting and graceful yet lurching rhythm—a mission that would seem perverse if it weren't so bizarrely catchy and playful. Sure, they may take for-fucking-ever to get new music out, but they do not disappoint. Another Madison instrumental outfit, Myrmidons, will be celebrating their own new EP at this show. Read more about that and hear a new Myrmidons track in our interview this week, and also don't miss the night's rare live set from Madison noise-rock rippers Transformer Lootbag.


Midas Bison, Leet Moteef, Gallant Ghosts, Joey Broyles, Mr. Jackson. Dragonfly Lounge, 9 p.m.

Producer and singer Sidney Johnson, aka Midas Bison, moved away Madison last fall just as his hyperstimulated yet soulfully vulnerable electronic pop was beginning to make a mark. On his most recent EPs, Glazer and Wavey Daze, Johnson combined effusive beats and synths with vocals that blend whimsical pop hooks with wide-eyed personal revelation. Currently living in El Paso, Johnson plays here to celebrate his birthday and the release of a new Midas Bison EP, Trios.


SUNDAY MARCH 29

Jobs, Noxroy, Nude Human. Bright Red Studios, 7 p.m

Jobs, formerly known as Killer Bob, has members spread across New York City, Minneapolis and Madison (the latter being bassist Rob Lundberg, who we interviewed this week), and on previous EPs they've made skittering, sharp-edged instrumental rock that's neither too pompous nor too easy to grasp. They'll be playing here ahead of the June release their first album under the Jobs name, Killer Bob Sings, which is the band's first release to incorporate vocals. Also on the bill is Noxroy, the wide-ranging experimental project of locally based guitar player and synth manipulator Andrew Fitzpatrick (Volcano Choir, Cap Alan, All Tiny Creatures), and Madison ambient duo Nude Human.


MONDAY MARCH 30

Salad Days: A Decade of Punk in Washington, DC (1980-90). High Noon Saloon, 7 p.m.

Scott Crawford's 2014 documentary Salad Days charts the evolution of punk and hardcore in Washington D.C., from the early-'80s detonation of Bad Brains to the emergence of heady post-punk bands like Fugazi and Jawbox. Crawford, who actually experienced all this as a young fan and zine producer growing up in the city's punk scene, has created a focused, deeply researched film, featuring extensive interviews with some of the scene's key figures (including Ian MacKaye and activist Mark Anderson), and he's refreshingly uninterested in myths or clichés. Crawford focuses on the people who shaped the scene's musical and social ups and downs, and comes through with a nuanced portrait of a community that was never quite as simplistic or strident as some made it out to be. We'll have an interview up with Crawford later this week. The filmmaker won't be present at this screening, but WORT-FM DJ El Serpentine will be spinning punk records before and after the film.

Cymbals Eat Guitars, Split Single, Julian Lynch. Frequency, 8 p.m.

Split Single is the pop-focused project of Jason Narducy, who plays bass in Bob Mould's current scorching trio and has been filling in on bass on recent Superchunk tours. Despite working with two of the tightest, feistiest rock outfits going, Narducy takes a more reflective approach on Split Single's 2014 album Fragmented World. Songs like “Last Goodbye" and “Made For Breaking" show that Narducy knows how to write a punchy melody of his own, and also knows how to reconcile that with gentler production. While the songs have a bit of power-pop DNA, Narducy also embraces his share of airy synth melodies and even some strings here and there. But most of all, he just takes his time and lets the tunes breathe a little bit. It's not that much of a departure, and it's a welcome one besides.


ZZ Top, Ben Miller Band. Overture Hall, 7:30 p.m.

We already loved 'The Top' long before we read that article in The New York Times that came out in anticipation of ZZ Top's Rick Rubin-produced 2012 effort La Futura. The interview revealed vocalist-guitarist Billy Gibbons' affinity for fellow Houstonian and late chopped-n-screwed hip-hop legend DJ Screw and he influenced a bit of the album, which really sums up much of what we love about this fucking band. The 46-year veterans have always taken the nastiest and least pretentious music possible in minimal blues-rock and jammed it through a relatively eclectic bouquet of aesthetic filters. Be it the light post-punk influence on 1983's Eliminator or the new wave and Italo disco synth splatterings on 1985's Afterburner or even the abstract flirtations with hip-hop on La Futura, they've always somehow managed to take one of the most base and insufferable genres of music, pump it full of sleaze, and elevate it to a higher level—be it simply interesting or undeniably infectious. Go watch a three-piece band that a stadium could barely hold spin their guitars around, shred solos that make you feel like you're being slapped in the face with chicken fingers, and pump out a couple hours' worth of glorious cheesy anthem-rockers that actually stand the test of time and don't feel all that nostalgic.


WEDNESDAY APRIL 1

Brown Derby. Crystal Corner Bar, 9:30 p.m.

For about 10 years, Madison country band Brown Derby has held down a Wednesday night gig at the Crystal Corner Bar (at first weekly and then monthly), and over time it grew into one of those reliable weekly gigs that Madison takes for granted but shouldn't. Throughout a few lineup changes, the band consistently delivered fun, diverse sets of country covers and originals, anchored in John Kunert's charming baritone vocals and Andrew Harrison's masterful, prickly guitar leads. Over time the gig also spawned its own weird little universe of stage banter and in-jokes, like the band's annual coloring contest (yep), Buddy Holly tribute sets, and even a Stone Temple Pilots Tribute night. During those years, bassist Nick Brown released his first solo record, and Harrison began playing with Count This Penny. Plus that one night they served hot dogs for some reason. Anyway, this will be the band's final Wednesday night at the Crystal, but the band will keep on playing shows and recording, as we reported earlier this month.

"Are the clouds going to get really abstract?": Andy Rubin at Gallery Marzen

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
February 09, 2015 @ 12:00 am

Detail from "Mexican Red Cloud" by Andy Rubin.

read

The Madison artist talks about his new show of paintings, "The Weight Of Weather."

In his new show, The Weight Of Weather, running through Feb. 28 at Gallery Marzen, 2345 Atwood Ave., Madison artist Andy Rubin plays with natural forms—especially clouds, rock formations, and in one case a termite tower—in meditative, gently abstract takes on landscape paintings. Rubin, who has worked at UW-Madison printmaking institution Tandem Press since 1989, cites the influence of form-warping architects including Frank Gehry and Antonio Gaudí, which comes through in the vertiginous-but-light structure in “Box Kite Tower" and the elegantly twisted rock formations in “Brunelleschi's House." But when browsing this show you mostly can't help but notice a ton of clouds: Clouds that look like gracefully looping ribbons, as in “Absolem's Cloud," and clouds with improbably firm geometric edges, as in “Mexican Red Cloud." Many of the works are painted directly on wood, including wood pieces that he's combined and built out according to whim: A small panel at the top of “Anatomy Of The Cloud" seems to show the main body of the painting in miniature. Rubin met up with me recently to talk about the show. Marzen is open 11 a.m. to 5 p.m. Tuesday through Friday, and noon to 4 p.m. on Saturdays.

Tone Madison: What interests you so much about clouds?

Andy Rubin: It's not so much that it's something in particular like that, it's focusing in on one element and just kind of drawing it over and over again. “Anatomy Of A Cloud" and “Cumulo Nimbus Grande" are two of the latest. Probably about four years ago, I was doing very realistic, illustrative-type work, figures, doing very domesticated scenarios, mowing the lawn, going on picnics, things like that. Somewhere along the line I gave it up. I work at Tandem Press and I make art for other people all day long. It takes a lot of energy to do that, but it also is very satisfying, so the urge to do my own stuff was sort of put on the wayside, and then a friend challenged me about four years ago to get back into it, and it's since been evolving. Instead of going back to the old ideas, I went back to something that a lot of kids do as they grow up, which is that they doodle, and that's a way to kind of keep concentrating, like at staff meetings, or when you're in class. I'd take all my doodles and tear up all the little parts of them and put them into an envelope. My doodles are very landscape-oriented and they're kind of architectural. A lot of them refer to Frank Gehry-type architectures where things are cubistic and they just kind of pile up in blocks, or the landscapes do that or the rocks do that. Eventually the clouds were the most fun to do, because they became these abstract patterns, and especially the piece “Mexican Red Cloud," was sort of a turning point. This idea of making this sort of quilted pattern, almost like a rug or a maze, or just a series of sort of abstract mark-making, in the clouds, kind of have led me away from the landscape part and more into the idea of clouds. It's not so much that I really love clouds—yeah, they're cool and people see shapes in them—but that's not what I'm doing. And if you notice a lot of time, [my clouds] aren't really organic, they're not loose and fluffy, they're not any illusionistic kind of thing, they just become a structure for me. I guess they're getting more and more abstracted as time goes on.

Tone Madison: And several of these are paintings but also kind of pieces of woodwork, in that you're painting on pieces of wood but assembling them together in very arbitrary ways.

Andy Rubin: Almost like furniture.

Tone Madison: Is that at all inspired by your work as a printmaker, where you work with a lot of tactile forms, carving and so forth?

Andy Rubin: [I used to do] a lot of woodcarvings where the wood would be the finished object, and I kind of let that go for a while and now I'm coming back to it. I have one in my basement right now where there's this cloud-ish thing, and there's gonna be a whole lot of carving in it too, so there will be pieces activated by paint and pieces activated by the carving of the wood as if those were brushstrokes.

Tone Madison: Is part of that sort of straining against the two-dimensional limitations of painting and printmaking?

Andy Rubin: Intellectual things like that are always afterthoughts. You want to be in the moment, and I really love the idea that even when I'm not in the moment and I go back through my little envelope of doodles, those remind me of that fresh idea. Doodling is a subconscious, letting your mind wander kind of thing. I constantly try to go back to those doodles when I feel like the work is getting a little contrived.

Tone Madison: “Neo-Modern Termite Tower" is one that has a combination of woodwork and painting, with etched elements mixed in among the painting, and it's made of two wood panels of different sizes put together.

Andy Rubin: These are kind of transitional pieces between the landscapes and the clouds. Part of the dilemma was, I do these small pieces, and I've got friends who paint large, and I'm very jealous of that. I wanted to paint large, and some of the paintings in this show are large, and I'm very comfortable with it. But there was a point, in 2013 or 2014, where it was like, “I've got all these small pieces, and they're OK, but somehow they seem they should be bigger." [On the upper panel], instead of using paint I'm using stain, and you can see a lot of tape marks in the stain, so that I could build up a pattern. A lot of the colors that I'll pick for the clouds will homogenize together. I'm not trying to go crazy with the clouds. I'm picking oranges, reds, and yellows, or teals and greens and blues, so that it kind of looks more homogenous. But this also shows that there's a lot of scraping and carving in the wood.

Tone Madison: Given the title, was this meant to be a little more playful or humorous than some of the other works in the show?

Andy Rubin: Yeah, because my daughter goes, “God, Dad, what are you gonna call it? 'Tower, Pink'?" Which would be typical of me. Or “Pink/Tower." But she goes, “No, let's look at something. It looks like architecture. Neo-modern gives it that idea that it's back to that Frank Gehry design." I try to keep some humor in it.

Tone Madison: And getting back to the clouds, we've got the exhibit's title painting, “The Weight Of Weather," which showcases the variety of ways you paint clouds—the one on the left is heavy and blocky, and the one on the right is sort of this flowing ribbon.

Andy Rubin: To me, the joke is the title. On the right side I've got this sort of beautiful white wash and I've got this doodle-y mark. Someone at the opening actually asked me if it was all one brushstroke. No, it's literally the opposite of a quick brushstroke. This is carved and painted and labored over. Thank god he thought it looked so quick and easy. Some things that come off really quick look really laborious, and some that are really laborious hopefully have a freshness to them. Where the title came from is my daughter said, “Wow, that's a lot of weight in the sky."

Tone Madison: What do you want to do next?

Andy Rubin: Well, there's always that element of fear: “Do I really want to go abstract? Are the clouds going to get really abstract?" And now I'm beginning to think, “Should they be so hard-edged?" That's why they're so loose on the inside, because the edges have a sort of hardness to them. I'm hoping it goes more in the direction of those clouds, where the clouds become bigger and have these shapes that can become abstract and become related to something else.

Madison calendar, April 9 through 15

Clockwise from left: Jerrod Carmichael plays the Comedy Club On State April 9 through 11; Kiazma plays the Cardinal Bar on April 10; Chuck Prophet plays the High Noon on April 15.

events

Jerrod Carmichael, the Wisconsin Film Festival, a YGB benefit, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY APRIL 9

Wisconsin Film Festival. Through April 16, multiple venues, see link for film guide.

The Wisconsin Film Festival is always a big beast to grapple with—this years 162-title lineup, and the feeling of option paralysis it induces, are par for the course. And there's a lot that we're trying to digest about how the festival experience has changed over the years, but it's that ambitious scope and tasteful curation that counts the most. Leading up to this year's fest, we've looked at some of the big standouts and reviewed selections The Iron Ministry, Almost There, Tomorrow We Disappear, Stinking Heaven, and Uncle John. Plus, see this handy guide to filmmakers making in-person appearances.

Jerrod Carmichael. Comedy Club On State, through April 11, see link for showtimes.

It's been a couple of years now since Jerrod Carmichael has been to Madison, but he put on one of the most memorable shows we've ever seen at the Comedy Club On State, and that's saying something. With the stage presence of someone twice his age, Carmichael effortlessly slid in and out of his material, riffing with the audience the whole time and improvising bits that always managed to coalesce and somehow morph into punchlines that were more than the sum of their parts. In those few intervening years since he's passed through our neck of the woods, everyone else has apparently caught up to him. He's had a Spike Lee-directed special on HBO last year, the first ever filmed inside LA's famous Comedy Store, and later this year his NBC sitcom, The Carmichael Show, will be hitting the airwaves. This is a dude who begs to be seen in the loose environment of a comedy club, so we'd recommend catching him here.


InDIGenous: Paul Hastil. Central Library, 7 p.m. (free)

InDIGenous, a four-part concert series featuring Midwestern jazz musicians and their new original compositions, kicks off with Madison pianist Paul Hastil, who's most frequently seen playing the Rhodes at the New Breed Jazz Jam on Tuesday nights at the Cardinal. At the weekly jam, Hastil often gets to mix in his own compositions with an array of other material from across the jazz canon. Beyond the fleet and harmonically rich material he showcases in that setting, we honestly couldn't tell you what Hastil's originals are like, because he so rarely gets a chance to show off the compositions he's stockpiled over the years. At this show, he'll get the rare chance to put his original material front and center, with help from guitarist Louka Patenaude, bassist Nick Moran, and drummer Michael Brenneis. The series continues each Thursday in April.

FRIDAY APRIL 10

House of Love: Hyperactive, Centrific, Kiazma, Wyatt Agard, Lovecraft. Cardinal Bar, 9 p.m.

This week's installment of Wyatt Agard and Lovecraft's DJ residency House Of Love takes a detour into deep, crushing techno with a live set from Madison-based, Berlin-style industrial techno producer Kiazma (a.k.a. Jackson Nyman). While we're still waiting on Nyman to release a proper album or EP, his last couple years on Soundcloud show an evolution from the heavy and evolving tech-house turf of “Conviction" to the blistering, Tresor-flavored, and functional dance-floor filth of his most recent tune—a minimal-techno banger called “Sunder," which finds the young producer expertly fleshing out and furthering a dark niche in contemporary dance music that Madison's been dying to have filled. You'll be able to further your techno fix with DJ sets from Chicago's Hyperactive and Minneapolis' Centrific.

Joe Pug, Field Report. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m.

When the now-historical Eau Claire-based (and later Raleigh, NC-based) folk-pop explorers of DeYarmond Edison split up in 2006, it spawned some pretty heavy sonic offspring. Vocalist-guitarist Justin Vernon went on to form Bon Iver and begrudgingly win a grammy, while Bassist Brad Cook, keyboardist Phil Cook, and percussionist Joe Westerlund went on to form mind-bending, avant-folk monster Megafaun. Finally, in 2011, fifth member Chris Porterfield kickstarted the gorgeously textured, Milwaukee-based project Field Report. The project's second full-length, last year's Marigolden, is packed with wandering, guitar-driven folk-pop tunes that are sometimes laced with drifting synth-textures and tasteful electronic rhythms, while Porterfield's earnest crooning soars over the top. Here, you'll get the Field Report material in more sparse form, as Porterfield plays a solo set opening for another solidly folk-rooted but still refreshing singer-songwriter, Austin's Joe Pug.

Foxygen, Alex Cameron. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. (free)

Look, maybe it's OK that Foxygen is of those bands that slyly and rather skillfully repurposes all those little subtle Pavlovian tics of great rock records from Revolver to Transformer, with a reliance that goes beyond the inevitable factor of influence and appropriation. Maybe it even takes some wit and ambition to be that constantly and blatantly referential and pull it all off with the flippant, smart-assed vibe of 2013's We Are The 21st Century Ambassadors Of Peace And Magic and last year's ...And Star Power. But at a certain point it all begins to feel trivial, as if it's sucking up more attention than it really justifies in musical substance. Whatever. Supposedly this is the band's farewell tour. Be there or feel like you vaguely missed out on something trendy, or something?

SATURDAY APRIL 11

Madison Hip Hop Fest. Union South, 7 p.m. (free)

The second annual Madison Hip Hop Fest focuses entirely on local hip-hop acts, with a small but still diverse lineup. It ranges from the nationally recognized producer DJ Pain 1 to less-appreciated Madison-based artists like the sharp, thoughtful MC Sincere Life, who just released his Fallen Crowns EP. And those artists, from around the Madison community, share the stage here with younger artists currently studying in UW-Madison's First Wave hip-hop arts program, here in the form of young collective Re8idence, two of whose members, Heru and Jonnychange, recently released a new EP. Before the music, there will be a panel discussion featuring various members of the local hip-hop community in Union South's Gallery 1308.


Chris Hardwick. Barrymore, 8 p.m. (sold out)

As one of the more enterprising comedians (-turned film executive) out of the comedy podcast boom, host of possibly the first game show to actually understand the internet, and human incubation chamber for television recap material, one can sometimes forget that Chris Hardwick is, in fact, a standup comedian. Previous tours have seen him marry the success of his live Nerdist podcast shows with his act, affording him a brief set recycled over a few too many tour dates, but this upcoming tour promises fresh material that he's been working over since recording a special in 2012, on the road between seemingly endless YouTube and television projects. Hardwick has a frenetic energy to his standup that can be a lot of fun if you're into his increasingly specific trademark of nerdy-but-still-cool-promise humor–he's probably caters more to existing fans than haters, which generally makes for a better show most of the time anyway. He excels at conversations, and his personable energy shines through in an act that often sucks fans and their own nerdy obsessions into his orbit in lieu of rigidly constructed chunks of material. Basically, you probably know if you're going, so yeah.

Rachele Eve, Asumaya, Autographite. Bright Red Studios, 6:30 p.m.

Far deeper than just an impressive live, loop-building affair, Madison-based musician Luke Bassuener's long-running Asumaya project brilliantly blends the jagged, political post-punk of Gang Of Four with dubby bass guitar lines and an assortment of the worldly, polyrhythmic pulses and instrumentation he discovered and embraced in his many years traveling to Ghana as a teacher and Peace Corps volunteer. Bassuener's freshly released full-length The Euphemist finally gives us tangible versions of tunes like the kalimba-powered “A Game Of Who Names It," privilege-pondering ballad “And Lucky," and four-dimensional bouncer “Import Society." The LP also showcases a couple of meandering, instrumental collaborations with Bassuener's friends in the Ghana-based Bawku West Collective. Autographite, an experimental collaboration between Madisonian performance artist Daiquiri Jones and Christian Dior's Spencer Bible, will kick things off, and Grand Rapids, Michigan singer-songwriter Rachele Eve will close out the evening.

Luxvid, Sweet William, Hypnoscript. Good Style Shop, 8 p.m.

Milwaukee musician Dania Luck has been making austere, propulsive, and darkly catchy synth jams for years under the name Stacian. Luxvid, her new project with fellow Milwaukee artist Pagan Woman, isn't necessarily a departure from Stacian, but shows plenty strength of its own on a new self-titled cassette/digital release. Tracks like “X" and “Trench" build on sharp, at times playful melody and primordial rhythms that evoke the primordial beginnings of electronic music, seemingly untouched by the cliches or tempos of anything trendy. Luxvid shares the bill here with Madison house artist Sweet William and lush Madison sound-collage weaver Hypnoscript.


The Low Czars. Mickey's Tavern, 10:30 p.m. (free)

Madison's The Low Czars are a cover band fixated on power-pop and its predecessors, drawing on gems like The Who's "Substitute" and The Flamin' Groovies' "Shake Some Action," and deeper obscurities from across the reaches of R&B and garage-rock. It's a thoroughly obsessive exercise, but never veers into cheesy, slavish tribute-band territory.

Robbie Fulks. Stoughton Opera House, 7:30 p.m.

Yes, Chicago country veteran Robbie Fulks gets up to Madison several times a year, but he's as rewarding to listen to as ever. His 2013 album Gone Away Backwards is the equal of anything he's done since he began putting out solo albums in 1996, a rich collection of hard-bitten narratives and characters displaced by personal and financial crisis. Plus, hearing Fulks dig into his back catalog—and, knowing Fulks, spout some abrupt smart-assed banter—should be a real treat in the intimate confines of the Stoughton Opera House.


SUNDAY APRIL 12

Young, Gifted and Black Coalition Benefit Show. Frequency, 4 p.m.

The Young, Gifted and Black Coalition has rapidly become a necessary and radical voice on racial justice in Madison and Dane County. This fundraiser show for YGB features a diverse slate of Madison artists, including MCs Rob Dz and Ant Da Hope Boy, singer-songwriter Aaron Scholz, playful pop act Ladyscissors, and doom-metal blasters Grotto. It's great to see such a variety of people lining up to aid YGB's persistent efforts—more community-spirited stuff like this, please.


WEDNESDAY APRIL 15

Chuck Prophet, Nick Brown. High Noon Saloon, 7:30 p.m.

A massively under-appreciated songwriter and guitarist, San Francisco's Chuck Prophet has a 25-year run of solo albums to his name, in addition to collaborations with heavies like Alejandro Escovedo. His latest album, 2014's Night Surfer, showcases a voice that's deceptively hard-won: Tracks like “Wish Me Luck" and “Lonely Desolation" lead with Prophet's drawling, sly-witted vocals and graceful, bright weave of rock and country styles, but they subtly tug at you with something emotionally and harmonically dense that can't be dismissed as just more likable straight-up rock.

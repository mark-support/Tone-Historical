WFF ’15: Last-minute short takes

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
April 09, 2015 @ 10:05 am

"Don't Think I've Forgotten: Cambodia's Lost Rock And Roll"

watch

Notes on seven more films we previewed ahead of Thursday’s 2015 Wisconsin Film Festival kickoff.

The 2015 Wisconsin Film Festival kicks off Thursday night. Before you head out, here are are thoughts upon viewing seven additional films in this year's lineup.

World Of Tomorrow (April 12, 3 p.m., Capitol Theater)

Animator Don Hertzfeldt's Everything Will Be OK trilogy (which screened at the 2012 WFF, complete with a great in-person visit from Hertzfeldt) was an emotional and a stylistic revelation for those of us who discovered Hertzfeldt through the mind-bendingly warped humor of his 2000 short Rejected. Hertzfeldt's rough-and-tumble stick-figure style evolved into something more visually experimental, touchingly incandescent and shaded with subtlety. His new short, World Of Tomorrow, in which a little girl is contacted from the future by one of her clone-descendants, lets us enjoy both the classic, twistedly funny Hertzfeldt and the current, exponentially ambitious and wistful Hertzfeldt.

A Hard Day (April 10, 4:30 p.m., Union South Marquee and April 13, 6:30 p.m., Sundance)

South Korean director Kim Seong-hun's A Hard Day has the backbone of an over-the-top thriller, but a comedic eye for bad behavior and its sprawling consequences. Lee Sun-kyn stars as a corrupt detective who, per the title, is simultaneously juggling his mother's funeral, an internal-affairs investigation, blackmail, and the body of a man he just accidentally hit on the road. In its third act, A Hard Day begins to feel a bit more like a conventional (but still very fun) action-laden blowout, but up front it masterfully threads suspense into grotesquely funny scenes of a desperate man straining custom and good taste to its breaking point.

Young Bodies Heal Quickly (April 11, 11:30 a.m., Vilas Hall and April 12, 6 p.m., Sundance)

The two brothers in Andrew T. Betzer's drama Young Bodies Heal Quickly don't come across as particularly evil or malicious. Rather, like the protagonists in Of Mice And Men or even Cormac McCarthy's Child Of God, they just seem to have been fired into the universe at an angle that proves disadvantageous for everybody else. One (Gabriel Croft) is in his late teens or early 20s, the other (Hale Lytle) can't be older than 12, but together they get tangled up in all kinds of destruction, apparently more out of boredom than anything else. As they flee home after the younger accidentally kills a girl, the pair encounter people who live on a strange line between fantasy and real consequences (from jealousy-fueled sexual relationships to Vietnam War reenactments). Sean Price Williams' cinematography balances idyllic beauty with a constant but subtle tinge of unease, and Betzer's characters and curt dialogue work in a bit of humor and quirk (including an argument about whether crabs swim) without disrupting that atmosphere.

Speculation Nation (April 10, 2: 15 p.m., Vilas Hall and April 11, 11:30 a.m., Sundance)

In their documentary Speculation Nation, former Madisonians Sabine Gruffat and Bill Brown travel to Spain for an activist's eye view of that country's housing crisis, touring huge, now-abandoned residential projects and following the protest movement that springs up around squatters' communities of displaced people. While the sheer vastness of empty new housing stock and the movement to populate old Roma caves in Granada are pretty fascinating, the most engaging aspect of Speculation Nation is the sheer ambition and nerve of activists across Spain, who confront banks and local governments through an array of bold tactics, including full-scale occupations of vacant new developments.

Roar (April 12, 9 p.m., Sundance)

We've read lots of the stuff that's been written about Roar being one of the most WTF?! films of all time, and I was skeptical. But damned if Roar doesn't live up to the hype. You can quantify the maulings, the stitches, and the other various injuries to the cast and crew, but it is impossible to exaggerate the sheer cinematic insanity that went into making Roar.

Crime Wave (April 15, 9 p.m., Sundance)

So wonderfully surreal and criminally(!) under-seen, especially given the tremendous influence John Paizs' work had on Guy Maddin, especially his My Winnipeg. Imagine John Waters eating some mushrooms and then sincerely remaking Kentucky Fried Movie and you're on the right track to understanding why a crisp new print of this 1985 film is such an exciting find in the WFF lineup.

Don't Think I've Forgotten: Cambodia's Lost Rock And Roll (April 12, 2:30 p.m., Union South Marquee and April 15, 3:45 p.m., Sundance)

If you like your psych-rock history with a healthy dash of geopolitical context (and you should!), then this is the doc for you. For real, watch this, and then make sure you snag the soundtrack from Dust To Digital when it drops later this year because the music is amazing, and is otherwise incredibly hard to track down, as we found out when we were kicking around the idea of sharing a playlist of some of the songs.

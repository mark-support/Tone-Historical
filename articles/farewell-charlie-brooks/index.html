Farewell, Charlie Brooks

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
January 04, 2016 @ 1:31 pm

Charlie Brooks performing at the High Noon Saloon in August 2015. Photo by Ankur Malhotra.

read

The Madison singer was a dashing performer from the old-school Motown mold.

Charlie Brooks, a Madison singer and Milwaukee native known for performing with Clyde Stubblefield’s Funky Mondays band and his own band Charlie Brooks And The Way It Is, died Saturday, January 2 after several years suffering from cancer and other ailments.

The January 11 edition of Funky Mondays at the High Noon will go on in Brooks' honor.

As a younger man, Brooks worked as a touring backup singer for Motown acts—including, he told The Madison Times in 2013, The Supremes and Sly And The Family Stone. Thanks to that background, he boasted a dashing showmanship rare among musicians in town, and had a gift for drawing shy and reserved Madisonians onto the dance floor.

Like many people in Madison, I grew to admire Charlie through his regular appearances at Clyde Stubblefield’s Funky Mondays. When that residency was at The Frequency, well, Stubblefield might have been the star, but Charlie made it a blast, accentuating covers of well-worn classics like “I Heard It Through The Grapevine” and “Let’s Stay Together” with moves that ranged from flashy spins to charismatic mid-song speeches. (My favorite of the latter came during one cover of “What’s Goin’ On”, when Brooks stretched out a pleading hand toward the crowd and said, “Ladies and gentlemen, we’ve all got to live on this piece of dirt together…”) He also took a lot of pride in how he looked onstage, and he looked fantastic—always a sharp suit just on the right side of loud.

He dealt with many health problems in recent years, including cancer, hepatitis C, and kidney disease. During the ups and downs of that, he kept up a pretty busy performance schedule at venues ranging from Madison bars to the Mineral Point Opera House. When I interviewed Charlie for Isthmus back in 2012, he told me that just a few days earlier he’d passed out after playing Fourth of July show in Mount Horeb, but he added, "I think if I could sing out of a hospital bed, I would do it that way.”

Over the years, fellow musicians put together many benefit shows for Charlie and his family, including one in November at the Knuckle Down Saloon, where he held down a regular gig. His family also started a crowdfunding page where you can donate to help with Charlie’s medical costs.

People have been sharing their memories and photos of Charlie on Facebook this weekend. One of my favorites is a set of photos Joe Engle took of Charlie performing at Funky Mondays in 2009.

I didn't know Charlie well on a personal level, but I treasured the joy and charm that he brought to the stage. Farewell and thank you, Charlie.

I’ll add information here about memorial services once it’s available.

Madison calendar, November 13 through 19

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
November 12, 2014 @ 11:00 am

read

Nots, Suzuki Junzo, Bill Bryson, and more of the best stuff headed to Madison this week.

(Pictured: Nots, playing Saturday at the Crystal Corner.)

THURSDAY NOVEMBER 13 

Brother Ali, Bambu, DJ Last Word, Mally. High Noon Saloon, 9 p.m.

Minneapolis’ Brother Ali has produced one of the most doggedly big-hearted, emotionally restorative bodies of work in hip-hop. From 2003’s Shadows On The Sun (which was reissued last year) to 2012’s Jake One-produced Mourning In America And Dreaming In Color, he’s kept his eye on a balance of personal struggle and a bigger social vision.


Garfunkel & Oates. Barrymore, 8 p.m. 

Kate Micucci and Riki Lindhome, who perform as Garfunkel & Oates, were originally set to be part of last month’s Madison Comedy Festival but had to postpone at the last minute, probably related to their IFC show that premiered to excellent reviews earlier this year. They’re making good on their promise to return and will be bringing their charmingly cracked take on ukulele-driven folk music, with song like “Handjob, Blandjob, I Don't Understand Job” “I Would Never (Have Sex with You)” and “Sex With Ducks.”


Broad City Live. Majestic, 8 p.m. (sold out)  

It’s always weird when TV shows hit the road and do live performances. We honestly have no idea what’s going to happen when Ilana Glazer and Abbi Jacobson bring their a live version of their Comedy Central show Broad City to town for a sold-out crowd. Standup? Probably. Script read-throughs? Possibly. Sketch comedy? Maybe. Special guests? Who knows! That said, Glazer and Jacobson are both Upright Citizens Brigade Theatre alumni, and Amy Poehler wouldn’t executive produce just anyone’s show (right?), so there’s reason to hope for a solid show.

Bob Mankoff. Wisconsin Institutes For Discovery, 7 p.m. 

Bob Mankoff, the cartoons editor at The New Yorker, visits the UW-Madison campus to discuss the magazine’s famed caption contest.

A Night At The Movies. The Wisco, 9 p.m. 

We’re in an era that sees many underground musicians foregoing the expense of hiring an actual music video director or visual artist and instead piecing together snippets of found footage and obscure public-domain film clips and jamming them through effects in Final Cut. The results of said trend often range from being totally engaging to infuriatingly tasteless, which is why we were left slack-jawed by Madison-based director Michael Doyle Olson’s brilliant, honey-dripped video for Mister Jackson’s sexed-up, R’n’B cruiser “The Golden Treatment.” At this event, Olson will present a wealth of new and recent music videos from local artists including Mister Jackson to gypsy-folk spaceman William Z. Villain. While we’re not sure how smoothly an event like this can go down at a notoriously (and endearingly) loud venue like The Wisco, a spot known for loud-mouthed biker dudes, an insanely loud jukebox, and four TVs tuned to four different stations (again we do love the place), but it’s definitely a celebration of a corner of visual art that we’re excited to finally see blossoming on a local level, so this one’s highly recommended.

Cinematheque: Goodbye To Language. Marcus Point Cinema, 7 p.m. 

UW Cinematheque wants to install 3D-projection equipment at its screening room in Vilas Hall, so to raise money and make a case for the value of 3D films, it’s screening Jean-Luc Godard’s latest, Goodbye To Language, in 3D. Tickets are $20 (but it benefits a program that brings dozens of free screenings to Madison throughout the year), and the event will include a post-screening discussion with UW-Madison film professor David Bordwell.

FRIDAY NOVEMBER 14 

My Brightest Diamond, Dosh & Ghostband. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m. 

Singer and multi-instrumentalist Shara Worden’s project My Brightest Diamond harnesses her operatic vocal range and flair for the theatrical into stately, at times deceptively playful songs. MBD’s 2006 debut album, Bring Me The Workhorse, still holds up nicely (and that’s saying a lot for a record that came out in a time just lousy with lushly arranged, chamber-ish indie-whatever stuff) thanks to the graceful melodies and shadowy charisma of songs like “Dragonfly” and “Golden Star.” The new album This Is My Hand shows Worden still has a handle on so much of what makes the project stand out, especially her ability to shift through a variety of arrangement styles—from the over-the-top martial percussion of opener “Pressure” to the slinky progression of “Resonance”—but still give it all a cohesive, slightly menacing persona. Plus, it’s always worth seeing the category-smashing Twin Cities mastermind Dosh, who opens up this show with his collaborator Ghostband.


Coward, Tyranny Is Tyranny, Myrmidons. Dragonfly Lounge, 9:30 p.m. 

Missouri instrumental-rock outfit Coward is perfectly capable of making thudding, strong-arm math-rock jams—the band has two drummers, after all—but that’s not really what it does. Instead, the band’s 2012 self-titled album uses the heavier elements with restraint and puts graceful, intricate guitar and keyboard melodies in the foreground. The result is a set of music that’s too tuneful and curious to get trapped in any of the usual instrumental-rock cliches.


Night Light: Art Car Derby. Central Library, 8 p.m.

During this installment of the Madison Public Library’s monthly after-hours series, the library’s Bubbler space will host a miniature car race on a 50-foot track built by the Bubbler program’s artist-in-residence, Justin Bitner. The event will also celebrate new exhibitions throughout the library from artists Julie VonDerVellen, Chris Murphy, Kent Williams, Keith Nelson, and Max Puchalsky.

Cinematheque: Fedora. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m.

As a follow-up to last week’s screening of Sunset Blvd., Cinematheque presents a later collaboration between director Billy Wilder and actor William Holden. In fact, 1978’s Fedora—which wasn’t screened for a long time and has recently been restored—has a lot of plot elements in common with Sunset Blvd., making it something of a bizarre and long-neglected companion piece to the earlier film.

Turkeyfest Day 1: Fire Retarded, The Honey Slides, Waterworld, The Tea Heads. Mickey’s Tavern, 10 p.m.

SATURDAY NOVEMBER 15 

Turkeyfest Day 2: The Hussy, Digital Leather, Nots, Proud Parents. Crystal Corner Bar, 10 p.m.  

Perhaps the best reason to check out Madison’s annual garage-punk party Turkeyfest is Saturday’s performance from Memphis electro-garage destroyers Nots, whose just-released debut album We Are Nots is packed with pummeling, synth-powered rockers featuring all the guitar filth, synth wiggles, and infectiously sassy vocal shouts we could ever want. Omaha synth-punks Digital Leather returns with its infectious, Screamers-influenced weirdo-punk anthems. Digital Leather also recently released a split with local proto-grunge destroyers (and Turkeyfest organizers) The Hussy, who will round out the bill along with Madison punk wizards Proud Parents.


W. Kamau Bell, Zach Sherwin, Alan Talaga. High Noon Saloon, 7 p.m. 

As a city, Madison is politically bummed the hell out these days. Not that a single comedian is going to come and lift all our spirits with jokes specifically about our troubles, but self-described “socio-political comedian” W. Kamau Bell is the kinda guy who would absolutely understand where we’re at and why our hearts are currently so heavy. Bell’s stand-up is conversational and approachable but also has an unabashed bite to it, which makes him just about right for the moment.


El Valiente, Cribshitter. Mickey’s Tavern, 11 p.m.  

Madison band Cribshitter have just released a new single ( give it a listen), “Jellyshoes,” which features a guest vocal from Phox’s Monica Martin and comes with a B-side, “Fantasy Factory,” a tribute to the estimable locally brewed IPA of the same name.

Cinematheque: J’Accuse. Vilas Hall, 2 p.m. 

Cinematheque: The Big Parade. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m.

SUNDAY NOVEMBER 16 

Anna Vogelzang, Whitney Mann, Dietrich Gosser. Old Sugar Distillery, 11 a.m.

Madison’s Anna Vogelzang is celebrating her new EP, Driftless, with a brunch show. Perhaps a tad precious, but the songs (including “Why Not,” which we wrote about earlier this week) have a sturdiness and maturity behind their friendly folk-pop exteriors.

Cinematheque: Rare Hitchcock. Chazen Museum of Art, 2 p.m.

Cinematheque’s massive series of Alfred Hitchcock’s features takes a detour here with a two-hour program of archival footage, including two short films Hitchcock made to support the Allied war effort, as well as trailers and test footage. And a lot of this isn’t easy to see anywhere, which is why Cinematheque is partnering up with the Academy Film Archive for this screening.

Suzuki Junzo, Amoebaggedon, Autographite. Mickey’s Tavern, 10 p.m.  

On deconstructionist guitar weirdo Suzuki Junzo’s 2013 album, Portrait Of Madeleine Elster, waves of feedback and drones gradually became a cyclone wrapped around Junzo’s minimal strums. “Dance Of The Inhabitants Of The Invisible Tower In The Ring Of Holy Mountain,” for instance, is center on one just chord repeatedly strum in myriad ways as strings and synthesizer slowly creep their way into the mix and the result is breathtaking. He plays here behind a new release, 7.


Micro-Wave Cinema: Menthol. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. 

Micah Van Hove’s 2013 drama Menthol follows a group of high-school friends who reunite in their 20s for a night of drinking and fun that ends up turning violent. After the screening, Van Hove will join the audience via Skype for a Q&A.

Henry Rollins. Stoughton Opera House, 7:30 p.m. 

Henry Rollins had something of a for-fuck’s-sake moment earlier this year with a  supremely misguided column on suicide, published in the aftermath of Robin Williams’ death. It’s definitely among the shittier works of former Black Flag members, and yes, we’re counting Greg Ginn’s idiotic fucking jam bands. After the backlash, Rollins backed off it a little bit and admitted he “had some work to do” on learning about mental health, so we don’t have to stay pissed at the guy or define him by this. If anything, his spoken-word performances, at once polemical and self-deprecatingly funny, have banked up years of goodwill, and it’s exciting to see him bring one to the Stoughton Opera House. So, it’s not always clear just what goes on in the blocky head of his, but at least he’s trying to learn and evolve as he goes.

MONDAY NOVEMBER 17 

Cinematheque: Escape From New York. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. 

One of the most delightfully warped sci-fi action films of all time, John Carpenter’s 1981 classic Escape From New York stars Kurt Russell as eye-patch-clad war-hero-gone-bad Snake Plissken. In post-World War III New York in 1997 (Christ…), Plissken has to save the United States president (Donald Pleasance) when Air Force One crashes and the president is captured by prisoners. The film is packed with all the dystopian eyepatches, Isaac Hayes, and epic synth-work any nerd could ask for and you shouldn’t miss it.

Distinguished Lecture Series: Bill Bryson. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m. 

The Iowa-born and UK-residing Bill Bryson has amassed a body of travelogues and popular histories that tend to feel reassuring in their approachable erudition and occasionally stinging humor. From the childhood memoir The Life And Times Of The Thunderbolt Kid to the linguistic history The Mother Tongue: English And How It Got That Way, Bryson’s work lets the reader have both lively curiosity and a feeling of sinking into something well-worn and familiar. And more often than not that something is diverting and pretty damn funny. He gives this Distinguished Lecture Series talk behind his latest, last year’s One Summer: America, 1927. Read more in our preview.

TUESDAY NOVEMBER 18 

Will Johnson, Jeremiah Nelson. Kiki’s House Of Righteous Music, 8 p.m.  

Between leading the great Denton, Texas band Centro-Matic and collaborating with artists including Jay Farrar and the late Jason Molina, songwriter Will Johnson has also turned out some sparse, arresting solo work. His 2012 album Scorpion showcases the murky but beautiful world Johnson can create with little more than voice and guitar, which is how he’ll be playing at this house concert.


WEDNESDAY NOVEMBER 19 

Nuts In The Ballroom: Crash & Burn. Brink Lounge, 7:30 p.m. (also Nov. 20)

Madison’s Bricks Theatre company brings back its storytelling series with a night of true tales centering on “the indignities, and surprising victories, of utter failure.”

Spotlight Cinema: Zero Motivation. Madison Museum Of Contemporary Art, 7 p.m.

The fall Spotlight Cinema season wraps up with Israeli director Talya Lavie’s new absurdist comedy about a group of young women working in a dysfunctional military human-resources office.

Horse Feathers, John Mark Nelson, Sarah Jackson-Holman. Memorial Union Play Circle, 8 p.m. 

It wouldn’t be fair to mistake Horse Feathers, the longtime project of Portland songwriter Justin Ringle, for just any act crossing delicate folk with delicate string and horn arrangements. Ringle’s songwriting has always had a bit of an insular edge, and a stronger grasp on melody than a lot of his contemporaries. He’s said that he tried to lighten up a bit on the newest Horse Feathers album, So It Is With Us, and it does feel a lot more bright and open than anything he’s done before, but that doesn’t seem to come at the expense of the songcraft that’s made Horse Feathers worth listening to so far.

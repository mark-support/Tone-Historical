Madison calendar, April 16 through 22

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
April 15, 2015 @ 11:45 am

Clockwise from top left: "Polyester" plays April 16 at the Wisconsin Film Festival; Six Organs Of Admittance plays April 22 at The Frequency; Saba plays April 18 at Union South.

events

Six Organs Of Admittance, Saba, Marc Maron, and more of the best stuff happening in Madison this week.

THURSDAY APRIL 16

InDIGenous: Devin Drobka's Bell Dance Songs. Central Library, 7 p.m. (free)

Milwaukee jazz drummer Devin Drobka's 2014 album Bell Dance Songs channelled eight of Drobka's original compositions through a conversational, flowing quartet featuring guitarist Jay Mollerskov, saxophone player Chris Weller, and bassist John Christensen. Drobka is young and adventurous, but instead of showing that through abrasion or overt eccentricity, he and his band take a subtle, patient, approach to complexity and improvisation. As part of the InDIGenous series, which aims to highlight regional jazz artists and their original works, Drobka and company will return to some of the material from last year's album and also break out new compositions.


Wisconsin Film Festival Closing Night. Sundance Cinemas Madison, see link for all showtimes.

The Wisconsin Film Festival's lineup is always a bit overwhelming to sift through, but the 2015 fest wraps up Thursday with a couple of clear highlights. David Gordon Green's new film, Manglehorn, starring Al Pacino and Holly Hunter, screens at 6:30 p.m., and its tale of a lonely ex-con locksmith (Pacino) holds out promise for continuing the good streak Green has been on—even some rough early reviews still leave us curious. The proper finale, though, comes at 8:45 p.m. with John Waters' Divine-starring 1981 comedy Polyester, complete with the “Odorama" scratch-n-sniff card gimmick Waters created for the film's initial release. You have to take the chance on smelling what Waters wants you to smell, but hasn't the magnificent old filth-monger earned that?

Marc Maron. Barrymore, 7:30 p.m.

Noted Dead Luke and The Hussy fanboy Marc Maron's recent stand-up work, as captured on 2011's album This Has To Be Funny and 2013's special Thinky Pain, is a great deal more tight and focused than the oft-frantic monologues that open Maron's WTF podcast episodes. But it's just as fraught and raw and itchingly self-aware in its unpacking of Maron's neuroses and imploding personal relationships. Though the podcast has given him a huge boost after years of grinding along as a comic, and his IFC show Maron gives us a more elaborate, semi-fictionalized take on his gloriously messy life (and lots of close-ups of Maron's podcast mic and goatee, for some reason), it's just as exciting to see Maron getting more expansive and confident (if not too comfortable) as a stand-up.

Matt Braunger. Comedy Club On State, through April 18, see link for all showtimes.

Matt Braunger recently followed his 2012 stand-up album, Shovel Fighter, with the new Big Dumb Animal, which deepens his style and digs into some personal stories and reflections on aging and masculinity, but without weighing down Braunger's nimble, accessible delivery. Braunger's comedy is often laced with a sense of the absurd, but it's pulled off with a conversational grace.


Endurance Improv Fest. Atlas Improv Co., through April 19, see link for all showtimes. (free)

Madison improv-comedy troupe Atlas Improv celebrates its 11th anniversary with a weekend of free performances, culminating in an 11-hour improv marathon slated to run from 4 p.m. Saturday to 3 a.m. Monday.

FRIDAY APRIL 17

Matthew Bindert: Large-Scale Woodcuts Opening. Edgewood College Gallery, 5 p.m. (free)

Madison-based artist Matthew Bindert's woodcuts have a texture that evokes digital overload as much as it does the texture of a hand-carved block, and their effect is both dazzling and grim. His new exhibition, running through May 17 at Edgewood College Gallery, showcases his evolving mix of printing and painting techniques in a set of large works that abstractly but powerfully meditate on themes including globalization and environmental degradation.

Derrick Buisch: Monstercity Opening. Memorial Union Porter Butts Gallery, 6 p.m. (free)

UW-Madison art professor Derrick Buisch's paintings blend abstraction with stinging, playful colors and draw on the punk influences he soaked up as a youth in the 1980s. Given the title of this new exhibition at the Memorial Union, it might have some themes in common with the delightfully bonkers monsters he's painted over the years.

Lover's Spit, Christian Dior, Knute. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

While we're still digging the messy entanglement of guitar-driven shoegaze pop and Quicksand-channeling post-hardcore of Christian Dior's crudely recorded Patriot Glass/Dioria double EP, it doesn't quite do justice by the enrapturing tightness of the Madisonian duo's live set. Tunes like “Kill Urself" and “Gengar" truly come alive when guitarist-vocalist Spencer Bible's busted, grunge-y chord progressions flood the room through his dual amp setup, while his desperate howls creep above, through a soured cocktail of effects and drummer Mike Pellino's jaggedly busy drumwork pummels below. Sure, we're definitely hoping for a better-recorded document from these refreshing noise-punks, but we also can't recommend checking out their viscerally jarring and tight live show enough.


The Soft Moon, Noveller, Kiazma. Frequency, 9 p.m.

With the past few years seeing dozens upon dozens of new entries into the synth-powered archives of industrial, EBM, and minimal wave music, we've come to applaud a band like Oakland's The Soft Moon for making an album like the freshly released Deeper. Producer and mastermind Luis Vasquez foregoes the golden standard of choosing a conservatively primal approach and pretending it's 1982 for crafting a polished, sonically unnerving, and dynamic album that makes every effort to live up to its namesake. With tunes that range from the tensely unraveling death-dance of “Black" to the longingly catchy vocals and goth-rock backdrop of “Wasting" to the darkened electro and dizzying rhythmic programming of “Wrong," Deeper definitely runs the gamut of creepy electronic music tropes, but does so while showcasing Vasquez' meticulous ear for production and an emotional undercurrent that sweeps the listener in. We're extra stoked for the live set, as Vasquez is known for bringing a drummer, bassist, and heavy visual components with him. Just as worthwhile here are Austin/NYC guitar explorer Noveller—more about her in our interview this week—and an opening set from Madison techno artist Kiazma.

William Elliott Whitmore, Christopher Gold. Majestic, 9 p.m.

Golden Donna, Beau Devereaux, Weevis Prance. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 9 p.m. (free)

SATURDAY APRIL 18

Saba, Pivot Gang, CRASHprez, Lord Of The Fly, 3rd Dimension. Union South Sett, 8 p.m. (free)

Saba's 2014 mixtape, ComfortZone, finds the Chicago MC at home amid almost relentlessly suave, swirling grooves (mostly produced by Saba himself), but the rapping itself isn't all laid-back. As nicely as he can ride a mellow groove, Saba's just as likely to use his flows to create tension and contrast, making for standout moments like “Westside Bound" and “Marbles." Saba will be playing here with his Pivot Gang crew, and a nice slate of Madison-based folks will be opening: The ambitious, racing-brain CRASHprez, the lovably off-kilter Lord Of The Fly, and young Madison crew 3rd Dimension.


Tone Madison/MadCity Music Exchange Record Store Day Party: Cowboy Winter, Mr. Jackson, WORT DJs. Bandung's Nutty Bar, 1:30 p.m. (free)

As part of MadCity Music Exchange's Record Store Day goings-on, we at Tone Madison are hosting a free afternoon show next door at Bandung Indonesian Restaurant's Nutty Bar. Madison's Mr. Jackson will kick things off at 1:30 p.m. with beautifully eccentric, synth-based R&B tunes from his 2014 album The Golden Hour Groove Session and an in-the-works new record he's calling Black Gandalf. Fellow Madisonians Cowboy Winter are on at 2:30 p.m. with a set of blazingly earnest, punk-inflected garage-soul, and will have freshly released cassette copies of their 2014 EP, Girl Trouble. Before and between bands, DJs from WORT-FM will be spinning records.

Call Me Madam. Vilas Hall, 7 pm. (free)

Walter Lang's satirical 1953 musical stars Ethel Merman as a Washington socialite who becomes the United States ambassador to the small fictional country of Lichtenberg.

Jose Gonzalez, Olof Arnalds. Majestic, 9 p.m. (sold out)

SUNDAY APRIL 19

They Might Be Giants. Barrymore, 7 p.m.

It seems hopeless to try and briefly sum up all the strange and lovable things They Might Be Giants' 33-years-and-counting run has encompassed. But it's clear enough that the band has remained ambitious, charming, and inspired in its blend of eclectic songcraft and far-flung nerdiness. They play here just a couple days before releasing a new album, Glean.

The Hired Hand. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

Peter Fonda's 1971 directorial debut is an eccentric, quietly subversive take on the Western, starring Fonda as a drifter who comes back to his wife after many years away and tries to win her back by working on her farm.

WEDNESDAY APRIL 22

Six Organs Of Admittance, Elisa Ambrogio. Frequency, 8 p.m.

We won't even pretend to begin to understand Six Organs Of Admittance guitar sage Ben Chasny's “Hexadic" musical system, which his PR team claims is the driving force behind his latest album of the same name. However, what we did recognize immediately upon giving Hexadic a spin is that it's a massive leap from the Fahey-tinged post-rock and psych-folk leanings of Chasny's previous efforts into a bleakly deconstructed and cosmic purgatory all its own. From the ominous fuzz-waltz of the album's first single “Wax Chance" to the slanted rhythm and guitar strangulation of “Maximum Hexadic" to the delicate, circling-shark guitar-play of “Future Verbs," Chasny's hands never seem to land on obvious notes or easily resolving chords, as his new conceptual limitations find him often trudging down the path of most resistance possible. If you, like us, are enjoying Hexadic and desperately seeking a way to make sense of the force behind it, it'd be a good idea to see Six Organs tour behind its most recklessly mysterious album to date.

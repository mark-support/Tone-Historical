Studio Ghibli’s vast window of discovery

Chris Lay
@TotesChris
January 19, 2015 @ 1:06 pm

Clockwise from bottom left: "The Tale Of The Princess Kaguya" screens Feb. 28 and March 1 at the Union South Marquee; "The Wind Rises" screens Jan. 24 at Vilas Hall; "Kingdom Of Dreams And Madness" screens Jan. 23 at Vilas Hall.

watch

Three upcoming screenings in Madison revisit the great Japanese animation institution.

In your life, everyone gets a pretty awesome little window between adolescence and a knee-jerk “seen it before" callousness. You get to live in a time period where everything is absolutely new to you, and it's truly magical. For me, one such window opened up with Princess Mononoke. I was a sophomore in high school. The Japanese-language audio had been swapped out for readings by Claire Danes, Billy Crudup, Billy Bob Thornton, Minnie Driver, and Gillian Anderson, which struck me at the time as exceptionally high-profile talent for an anime. “They got Billy Bob Thornton and Gillian Anderson?!" I clearly had a warped sense of fame. It was violent and funny, visually stunning and kind of fucked-up. It had a strong, complex female lead at its core, and all the characters had such wonderfully nuanced motivations. It was from this mysterious place, Feudal Japan, that I realized I knew practically nothing about. Above all, though, it was that rare environmental cautionary tale that didn't beat its tree-hugging intentions into your skull over and over. Ferngully this was not. Princess Mononoke, it turns out, was only the tip of a massive iceberg of startlingly high-quality animation flowing out of Japan's Studio Ghibli.


Studio Ghibli, if you're unaware, is the Japanese animation house that, since the mid-80s, has generated animated classic after animated classic. The studio was founded by visionary writer / directors Hayao Miyazaki and Isao Takahata, along with producer Toshio Suzuki. Together they've turned out Castle In The Sky, Ponyo, Grave Of The Fireflies, My Neighbor Totoro, Pom Poko and almost a dozen other films that have all been critical and commercial successes. If you've seen just one of their films, you probably saw the followup to Mononoke, 2002's Spirited Away, which has raked in somewhere in the neighborhood of $330 million worldwide and went on to win the Oscar that year for Best Animated Feature.

I was maybe fourteen or fifteen years old when Mononoke came into my life. People who claim that their high school years were “the best years of their life" are obviously weirdos who should've gotten out more, but maybe we all take for granted what a really great liminal time period high school is for so many things. While it might not be the best age to go out and interact with members of the opposite sex (women are scary!), it's great for being exposed to new cultural things. New movies, artworks, book, graphic novels, I dunno... anything... It was right around then that friends turned me on to Tarantino and the Coen Brothers, Samurai flicks and Das Boot, Akira and Terry Gilliam movies that weren't Monty Python movies.

The reason I bring all this up now is to highlight the fact that so many Studio Ghibli films, Miyazaki's in particular, happily exploit this awkward and fertile, transitional moment. I mean, it's not reinventing the wheel to have protagonists who are coming of age and discovering things about themselves and the world as they move through the plot of a film. Take for instance Chihiro in Spirited Away, who has to save her parents after they've been turned into pigs, Whisper Of The Heart's coming of age love story between a 14-year-old and a young violin maker, or Kiki's Delivery Service, which Miyazaki claimed was about a character traversing “the gulf that exists between independence and reliance." There's a rarified air of works that are able to tie everything together so effortlessly and with such fantastical style and grace and with such profoundly graceful consistency. The more I think back to the films Ghibli's put out, the less possible it seems to over-compliment them.

Miyazaki, now 74, has threatened retirement in the past, saying of previous films that they would be his last, but it seems like it's sticking this time. The documentary Kingdom Of Dreams And Madness, which screens this Friday (7:00 pm) at UW Cinematheque, covers the history of the studio while also following Miyazaki through the stages of crafting The Wind Rises, which would end up being his final film, and will be screening at Cinematheque on Saturday (2:00 pm).


Mami Sunada's Kingdom Of Dreams And Madness is a humble little affair of a documentary, despite the unprecedented “unfettered" access that the director was given to Ghibli over the course of the year leading up to the release of The Wind Rises. Animation is a generally solitary and uninteresting medium when we're talking about a visual documentary, so if you're looking for a detailed flow chart of the studio's process, you'll be out of luck. Most Ghibli fanatics, though, are likely looking for a deeper examination of Miyazaki, which the film does a decent job of doing. Even if it comes across as a little fawning over the creator, you get to see occasional glimpses of his creative idiosyncrasies, however slight they might seem in the big picture. You'll also get to see some of the business of running the “studio" end of the Ghibli organization, joining Toshio Suzuki at tables for conference calls where he discusses the best ways to prod Miyazaki and Takahata to meet their deadlines, as well as going over the target demographics of their various tie-in merchandise.

Want to watch Miyazaki in the moments that he finishes the storyboard for The Wind Rises? That perceptible moment of great and quiet relief anyone who's ever barely met a deadline has felt is in there too. Sadly, Kingdom Of Dreams And Madness is designed to focus squarely on Miyazaki. Takahata, the seemingly more complex half of Ghibli's visionary duo, is only occasionally mentioned and even less often glimpsed, each time more or less defined in contrast to Miyazaki. Takahata gets about as much screen time as the cat who prowls Miyazaki's studio.


Just two years ago, UW Cinematheque ran a 14-film Ghibli retrospective of sorts on Sunday afternoons at the Chazen Museum of Art, presenting lost gems as well as some of the studio's best-known works on pristine new 35mm prints. If you saw all of those back then, then you definitely owe it to yourself to get out there this weekend, and if you missed them... then consider this your penance, if penance was supposed to be something free and awesome.

If the two-fer this weekend isn't enough Ghibli to greedily gobble up (sorry), Takahata's The Tale Of The Princess Kaguya, which was produced and released relatively concurrently to The Wind Rises (and to equal critical acclaim), will screen at Union South's Marquee Theater on Saturday, Feb. 28 (8:45 pm) and Sunday, March 1 (3 pm).

Residential: Prentice Berge on his poster art for Nattspil DJs

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
April 20, 2015 @ 12:00 am

Highlights of Berge's Nattspil poster art from 2014 and 2015.

events

“The reason why I did it, it wasn’t advertising, it was to fire up the DJs."

Welcome to Residential, where Tone Madison meets the best acts holding down regular gigs on local stages and decks, from sturdy weeknight house bands to excellent and under-appreciated DJs. This time we take a bit of a detour to ask Madison restaurateur Prentice Berge about the poster art he creates for the DJs who spin seven nights a week from 10 p.m. to 2 a.m. at Nattspil on King Street.

On the surface Berge's posters, which you'll likely only see if you follow the Nattspil Facebook page or are Facebook friends with someone who DJs there, run flagrantly counter to the low-key, minimalist aesthetic Berge has cultivated at Nattspil itself. Berge, who also books the DJs, is a self-taught Photoshopper run amok, exuberantly appropriating images ranging from retro computer ads to the cover of Jethro Tull's Thick As A Brick and frequently finding silly ways to incorporate his DJs' faces and names into bizarre contexts. Nattspil could get away with presenting its DJ lineup a bit more self-seriously—regulars like Nick Nice, Phil Money, Lauren Franchi, Vilas Park Sniper, and DJ Zukas are adept at digging deep into their musical knowledge to sculpt the mood of the place, and in a given week the sounds can span from house to disco to hip-hop to far-out psych experiments—so it's admirable that Berge takes a more playful, at times in-joke-y approach. Berge, who also designs posters for the other musical endeavors he's involved in around town (including Hoot 'N Annie and the Madison Song Circle) talked with me recently about finding inspiration for his posters on the Internet and from the DJs themselves.

Previously on residential: DJ Phil Money | The New Breed Jazz Jam

Tone Madison: Do you have a favorite person to do posters for?

Prentice Berge: When it comes to the DJs, like with all art, the more personal connections you have, the more you know someone, the more you can play with ideas. I've got DJs like Nick Nice that have been there since day one. He's a great day and a Madison institution. Vilas Park Sniper. Those are people that I've known for a long time and I know their personalities, and I know people like DJ Trichrome, and I know Nathan Johnson Zukas really, really well. We're buddies. I know here I can push buttons or be absurd—utterly absurd. For instance, DJ Trichrome, there's this one where I take his little head logo and stick it on a Dolly Madison cakes thing, and it's a Victorian woman and lace and things like that. I can put him on a female body or a very skinny male at the back of a swimming pool with five Barbies sitting on the side of it and have fun.

Tone Madison: You do seem to have a lot of fun with Zukas in particular, just doing stuff with Nathan's face and whatnot. How much of that is a product of you knowing him?

Prentice Berge: I know him really well and it's disco, and disco as a musical form is meant—I mean, think about the clothing, the men and women, it was all about pushing identity and boundaries, the silk shirts and the rayon shirts open to the belly button and stuff like that. I've got Amos Smith, who's a house DJ, and looking for underground homes and caverns and underwater homes and Edward Scissorhands-type communities to plug his name and face into is really fun. I really love old albums, especially '40s, '50s, '60s, '70s. There's some really funny art that's that there that I don't really have to do all that much with. I just steal it. Truth be told, if Harry Fox went after me like he goes after me in music, I'd be out of business, because I borrow every single day. Every single piece is something that I found on the internet. Well, that's not absolutely true. Some of them come from constructions that I do myself, but it's all montage, and montage means that you're begging, borrowing, and stealing all over the place.

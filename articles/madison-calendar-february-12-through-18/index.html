Madison calendar, February 12 through 18

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
February 11, 2015 @ 11:36 am

Clockwise from bottom left: The Kenny Reichert Band plays the Rathskeller on Feb. 13; the Pedrito Martinez Group plays the Capitol Theater on Feb. 12; and Pete Davidson plays the Memorial Union Play Circle on Feb. 12.

events

Pete Davidson, Machine Head, Kenny Reichert, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY FEBRUARY 12

Pete Davidson. Memorial Union Play Circle Theater, 7 & 8:30 p.m.

As you are probably aware, Saturday Night Live will celebrate its 40th anniversary this coming Saturday with an epic star-studded three hour long spectacular looking backwards at their past. If you want to see the future of SNL though, you surprisingly have no farther to look than UW's own recently-renovated Frederic March Play Circle this Thursday, where SNL's resident young person Pete Davidson will be holding court for two free shows. Of all the current SNL rookies, Davidson's been the strongest standout, bringing standup chops honed in the NYC circuit to a number of memorable millennial-centric Weekend Update spots. It's not clear if the folks at WUD gave this show the promotion it's due, but the Play Circle only seats 168 but, so maybe show up early if you want a spot.


Distinguished Lecture Series: Bill T.Jones. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m.

A widely lauded force in contemporary dance, Bill T. Jones will give a talk here ahead of a Feb. 14 performance at the Union Theater.

Print Generation + Wavelength. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

It's something of a rarity for UW Cinematheque to venture deeply into the waters of the avant garde, but this double-header will be an exception. The headliner here is J.J. Murphy's Print Generation, which is one minute of film, consisting of 60 one-second shots, repeated 50 times with each repetition degrading slightly from the previous one. We love seeing the wild and crazy stuff that Rooftop Cinema brings to MMoCA every summer and Starlight Cinema was bringing to the Frederic March Play Circle back around 2008 or so (and will occasionally be bringing again?). While this two-fer isn't associated with either of those programs, it's spiritually akin to the sort of conceptually freaky cinema odds and ends that you only get an occasional chance to see here in Madison. Before Print Generation they'll screen Michael Snow's Wavelength which UW Cinematheque describes as a “landmark of structural cinema". For those who don't “get it" there will be a discussion afterwards with Murphy, who is also a UW-Madison film professor.

Pedrito Martinez Group. Capitol Theater, 7:30 p.m.

Madison is getting a few Afro-Cuban musical treats this year, and a couple of them are combined this Thursday at the Overture: UW-Madison artist-in-residence and Buena Vista Social Club ringleader Juan de Marcos González will give a lecture ahead of this show by younger percussionist and bandleader Pedrito Martinez. Martinez has played with a variety of jazz and rock acts, from Bruce Springsteen to Arturo O'Farrill, and his four-piece Pedrito Martinez Group channels Afro-Cuban music with both a brash immediacy and a nuanced sense of history.


Jim Jefferies. Barrymore, 8 p.m.

Whether simply being brash and happily offensive really makes for a distinctive persona in comedy seems pretty iffy these days. When you've got someone like Anthony Jeselnik (who plays the Comedy Club On State next weekend) refining brutal humor and red-flag subject matter into such a concise art form, Jim Jefferies (who is admittedly funny in a boorish, booze-soaked, trampling-on-all-the-PC-ness way) ends up coming off like an Australian Doug Stanhope by comparison. Jefferies plays here in the wake of last year's Netflix stand-up special Bare.


Machine Head. High Noon Saloon, 9 p.m. (sold out)

In 24 remarkably consistent years (despite myriad line-up changes), Oakland arena-thrashers Machine Head have carved out a massively crushing and distinct sound while never shying away from slicing up whatever contemporary developments and trends are budding in underground metal and bending them to their own will. Be it flirtations with rap-metal in 1999 on the Ross Robinson-produced The Burning Red, the melodic metalcore influence on 2003's Through The Ashes Of Empires, or the doomy trudge of “Beneath The Silt" on 2014's Bloodstone & Diamonds, what remains consistent are the mighty dynamics, pummeling syncopation, insane riffing, and vocalist Rob Flynn's head-rattling growls. We're not sure when Machine Head's popularity scaled down to that of a band that could fit into the High Noon Saloon, but creatively they've been on a warpath since ex-Vio-Lence guitarist Phil Dremmel joined the band in 2002. The foursome, now sans original bassist Adam Duce, are currently touring behind the annoyingly-overproduced, but thrash-heavy and infuriatingly technical Bloodstone & Diamonds, which means this will be a great time to catch them.

Ringworm, Vermillion, Dos Males, Unnecessary Gunpoint Lecture.Wisco, 7 p.m.

FRIDAY FEBRUARY 13

Kenny Reichert Band. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 5 p.m.

Young Milwaukee jazz guitarist Kenny Reichert's new album Interpretations offers a set of polished, reflective compositions, but it's also a great showcase for Reichert's fluid, restrained playing and the personalities of the band that will join him at this show—trumpeter Russ Johnson, drummer Devin Drobka, and bassist John Christensen. Highlights like the title track and “Cascades" may start off quiet and unassuming, but deliberately unfold into a lot of tender, stately playing from all four musicians. Let's hope that doesn't get too marred in the chatter and popcorn smells of the Rathskeller on a Friday at happy hour, because it's contemporary jazz worth sinking into.


Local Love Fest: The Kites, Droids Attack, Cold Black River, Emerald Douglas. Frequency, 9 p.m.

This show, along with a second one on Saturday at The Frequency, celebrates the 2015 edition of the annual Local Love Fest compilation, in which Madison bands cover each others' songs, sometimes reaching pretty far—one highlight this year includes stoner-rock trio and LLF ringleaders Droids Attack covering a song by now-defunct Madison hip-hop outfit Star Persons, for instance. Look for that and another selection from the new comp on Tone Madison soon.

Barfly. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

With a screenplay by Charles Bukowski and a noteworthy performance by Mickey Rourke as an alcoholic writer, Barbet Schroeder's 1987 film Barfly is among the highlights of Cinematheque's ongoing series on the prolific studio Cannon Films.

Cary Elwes. Monona Terrace, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Local H, The Traveling Suitcase, Bron Sage. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m.

SATURDAY FEBRUARY 14

The Lady From Shanghai. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Orson Welles' 1948 film The Lady From Shanghai, staring Welles and Rita Hayworth, is considered one of his finest contributions to film-noir. It screens here in a new digital restoration.

White Arrows, Chappo. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 8 p.m.

Currently touring behind 2014 album In Bardo, Los Angeles popsters White Arrows basically sound like they're trying to pick up where MGMT left off when they ditched the obnoxiously infectious hooks of 2007's Oracular Spectacular. Look, we don't give a shit about MGMT and we certainly aren't chomping at the bit for an excuse to drop their name, but that's exactly what In Bardo sounds like. From the weird vaguely nasal twang of the vocals, the quirky, synth-laced pop grooves, the lazy rhythms, the tone of the bass—basically, fucking everything. But hey, if that's your thing, feel free to ignore the strategic undertones and dive right into some potently catchy and detailed tunes like the “Kids"-esque, galloping dance-rock of “We Can't Ever Die" or plucky electro-ballad “Nobody Cares" and pretend that Congratulations never happened.

SUNDAY FEBRUARY 15

Zap Mama And Antibalas. Union Theater, 8 p.m.

New York's Antibalas have a respectable and fierce grasp on Afrobeat but aren't afraid to take stylistic risks with the form, especially on 2012's self-titled album. So we're intrigued to see what results when they come here on with African folk-channeling a capella group Zap Mama, for a night that will span both separate and collaborative performances.

Nikki Lane, Jonny Fritz, Hugh Bob. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

Martin Sexton, Peter Mulvey. Majestic, 8 p.m.

The Passionate Thief. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

WEDNESDAY FEBRUARY 18

Behemoth, Cannibal Corpse. Orpheum, 7 p.m.

It's been a long time since my you had to special-order Buffalo gore-metal legends Cannibal Corpse's 1993 classic Tomb Of The Mutilated from the local record store (because no one in town would just carry it) and then hide it so your dad wouldn't see the album cover, which featured one bloody, rotting body performing cunnilingus on another bloody, rotting body. (This writer even got the whole “if anybody asks, you didn't get this from me" talk from the clerk.) It's pretty mind-blowing that, 17 years later, a band boasting whistle-along classics like “Entrails Ripped From A Virgin's Cunt" and “I Cum Blood" is now headlining at the fucking Orpheum while such a formerly massive draw and far more mainstream band like Machine Head is playing at a comparatively tiny spot like the High Noon Saloon in the same week. The brutal quintet is currently pushing its latest pile of blast-beats, blurry guitar-shred, impossible basslines, and charred-throat gurgles in last year's A Skeletal Domain. Co-headlining the show are Polish titans Behemoth, who sound as grim and focused as ever on 2014's The Satanist.

The Hand That Feeds. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

Robin Blotnick and Rachel Lears' 2014 documentary The Hand That Feeds dives into the increasingly visible plight of low-wage restaurant workers, following a group of immigrant laborers who went up against their employers at a deli in New York City to protest low pay and exploitative business practices.

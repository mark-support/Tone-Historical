Dangerous theater and rising redundancy at Wisconsin Film Festival

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
March 13, 2014 @ 2:00 am

watch

Wisconsin Rising and Dangerous Acts... offer two very takes on political protest.

It would be unfair to directly compare 'Dangerous Acts Starring The Unstable Elements Of Belarus' and 'Wisconsin Rising,' yet each film can teach us something about the other's portrayal of dissent and the abuses of the powerful.

Both 2014 Wisconsin Film Festival selections are documentaries about thwarted but persistent dissenters, but apart from that the circumstances, and therefore the expectations, differ wildly. For 'Dangerous Acts,' director Madeleine Sackler and crew had to smuggle footage out of Belarus, which since 1994 has been under the de facto dictatorship of Alexander Lukashenko and has cracked down on political opposition with swift brutality. 'Wisconsin Rising' director Sam Mayfield fought for access to the Wisconsin State Capitol alongside participants in the 2011 protests opposing Act 10, the state law that stripped collective bargaining rights from public employees. The 14 senators stuck it out valiantly in Rockford, but the leaders of Belarus Free Theatre ended up having to seek political asylum in the UK just to be able to pursue their art and raise their children in peace.

Now, this isn't to diminish the arrogance of Wisconsin Governor Scott Walker and our state legislature, or the social and economic displacement that public workers face in 'Wisconsin Rising,' or the chilling effect upon free speech of arresting protestors. But there can be little argument that the protagonists of 'Dangerous Acts' have it worse, running an underground dissident theater company in the face of censorship that is, yes, far more brutal and capricious than even the worst that has taken place in our Capitol. They also face the prospect, as one subject puts it, that their children may only ever know one leader in their entire lifetime.

The theater performances in 'Dangerous Acts' are expertly unsettling, and that quality rubs off onto Sackler's direction. The film has the power to shock even those who are inured to footage of riot cops and mass arrests. In one peaceful protest in the documentary, the crackdown begins not with a line of riot cops, but with plainclothes officers slipping in among the protestors in pairs, occasionally snatching one into a windowless van—each arrest an eerie kidnapping.

The performers depict this, and other trials of life under dictatorship, with sparse, resourceful staging that leaves ample room for the surreal, the absurd,  the grim, even the tender. As they rehearse a show in New York City after Belarus' corrupt and violent 2010 elections, four of the actors handle a small figure made of newspapers. They gently walk it across the stage,  stand it up on a chair in front of a chalkboard on which a window is drawn. The figure reaches out to a chalk image of the sun—and then, gracefully and hopefully, it flies. According to a New York Times review of the show, 'Zone Of Silence,' this scene depicts "a doomed little girl (the real-life subject of a widely reported attempted adoption by an Italian couple)." What Sackler shows us in this rehearsal of the scene, though, is its wondrous air of kindness, charged with the actors' painful separations from their own children, and, in the case of one actor, grief for a son who hung himself at age 10. Even in a film full of jarring moments and subversive art, this scene takes the viewer off-guard, transporting us from political outrage to astonishing empathy and vulnerability.

Not only will this scene make anyone cry who isn't already crying (it's just wrenchingly beautiful), it embodies the greatest achievement of the film: That Sackler's vision is big enough to not simply reduce her subjects to one-dimensional dissidents. Sackler just seems to get that, while it is vital that art not shy away from politics, it is equally vital that even the most overtly political art reach the audience on a primal human level.

What 'Dangerous Acts' mercifully lacks is a sense of self-congratulation. If you need that, you can dose up with 'Wisconsin Rising,' yet another documentary that seems entirely too preoccupied with chronicling and honoring the citizens and activists who occupied the Wisconsin state Capitol in February 2011. And I agree with their cause, by the way, but in a mere three years we've seen several documentaries of basically the same bent—see also 'Forward' and 'We Are Wisconsin.' (Helpfully, our local media outlets tend to cover each film without mentioning the existence of the others and the obvious possibility of redundancy.)

Full disclosure: I became irretrievably sick of "Wisconsin uprising" docs last year after watching the 'Forward' trailer, yes, just the trailer, because even a pretty liberal person only needs so much dramatized protest footage and commentary from a slew of lefty usual suspects. I concluded several months back that Brad Lichtenstein's excellent 'As Goes Janesville' was the last Wisconsin-centric documentary I ever needed to see about this era in American politics. And no matter how much I agree with Walker's opponents, I don't need another documentary that treats them with fawning credulity. But I really tried to let 'Wisconsin Rising' show me something different than what I was expecting. I held out hope that the film could break out of the insular lefty box as compellingly as 'As Goes Janesville.' (Note how 'As Goes Janesville' declines to treat Madison as the acropolis of class struggle, and primarily shows us the Capitol protest through the eyes of a struggling United Auto Workers member.) 

To Mayfield's credit, 'Wisconsin Rising' does ponder the nuances of the protests and failed attempt to recall Walker, particularly later on in the film, analyzing the trade-offs between electoral strategies and direct, workplace-based action and the rifts between labor activists and Democratic Party leaders, and Wisconsin's history in pioneering public unions and unemployment insurance. It includes brief interviews with Walker and some of his supporters.  Yet too often it falls into a rhythm of protest clips and lefty talking heads. And Mayfield *does* use those lefty talking heads voraciously, featuring interviews with Dennis Kucinich, Jim Hightower, Kelda Helen Roys, Robert McChesney, Ruth Coniff—come on, leave some for the hacky Wisconsin-protest documentaries of the future!

Perhaps the most frustrating thing about the film is that it almost entirely glosses over the Democratic primaries in the gubernatorial recall. Mayfield spends plenty of time on specific twists and turning points of the protests themselves, many of which are admittedly worth celebrating (the occupation of the Capitol, the participation of firefighters and cops, then-Mayor Dave Cieslewicz's proclamation that City of Madison police would not help to remove protestors from the building, the Fox News palm-tree incident, perhaps even the utterly fucking stupid fake David Koch phone call. Am I talking about these as if most of them are pretty much just in-jokes among Madisonians and the politically obsessed, as opposed to indelible moments in history? Yeah, cause most of them are.) But we've been over those, haven't we? How about taking a look at the Democratic primary and the flaws of Tom Barrett as a candidate, the folly of voting down Kathleen Falk or Kathleen Vinehout and going with an overly mild-mannered candidate who lost to Walker in the last election? At the very least, leaving the primary right out of the narrative puts a severe dent in the film's credibility. (So does the first expository title in the film, which states: "Wisconsin lawmakers strip collective bargaining rights from citizens, rolling back eight decades of wisconsin state history and progress for working people." Not technically inaccurate, but it's a bit sloppy to just say "citizens" as opposed to specifying .)

And that brings us to what is most upsetting about documentaries like this: Each one represents a missed opportunity to go beyond protests, unions, Citizens United and the Koch Brothers. How about even deeper inquiries about where unions and the left are to go from here? How about juxtaposing Wisconsin with the experiences of the many other states that elected heavy-handed conservative governors in 2010? How about questioning whether the (yes, courageous, heartening) participation of Madison firefighters and cops in the protests really represented the feelings of their counterparts around the state (it'd be great if it did, but just to have the question ASKED at this point would be refreshing)? How about asking what unions might have done over time to harm their own cause? Or just anything that doesn't return time and again to that well of Capitol protest footage?

A common sign at the Capitol protests read: "Governor Walker: The whole world is watching." Films like 'Wisconsin Rising' seem designed to ensure that the only folks watching are those already converted. Despite asking a few important questions we American lefties need to ask, it just seems too intent on coddling us. It should make us all the more grateful for the visceral shock of 'Dangerous Acts.'

Madison calendar, April 23 through 29

Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
April 22, 2015 @ 10:49 am

Clockwise from top left: Le1f plays Union South on April 24; Janeane Garofalo plays the Majestic on April 29; and Chinchano plays the Central Library on April 23.

events

Janeane Garofalo, Paramount Records, Le1f, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY APRIL 23

The Rise And Fall Of Paramount Records. Multiple Venues, through April 26 (free)

This ambitious three-day event, hosted by the UW-Madison Center for the Humanities, considers the history of Wisconsin's own Paramount Records, which between the late 1910s and early 1930s put out dozens of recordings from many of the absolute essentials of American blues, jazz and folk music—all in an effort to churn out a cheap product that would help its parent company, the Wisconsin Chair Company, sell record-player cabinets. The occasion is the release in 2013 and 2014 of the two-volume box set The Rise And Fall Of Paramount Records, 1917-32, a joint project of Jack White's Third Man Records and the late John Fahey's Revenant Records. Revenant co-founder Dean Blackwood, music journalist Amanda Petrusich, and several UW-Madison scholars will take part in panel discussions about the music itself and its unlikely history. On Saturday morning, the Madison Public Library's Bubbler space will host an interactive session of art and listening featuring Madison musician Simon Balto and artist Matthew Bindert. Learn more in our interview with Dean Blackwood.

Hiss Golden Messenger, Phil Cook. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

North Carolina folk act Hiss Golden Messenger cultivated a cryptic, ruminative air on its early releases for mighty Americana underdog labels like Tompkins Square and Paradise Of Bachelors, but often with MC Taylor providing an accessible melodic foundation throughout. Last year's album Lateness Of Dancers, the outfit's first for Merge, bears just a little extra polish that feels like part of a natural projection, and doesn't sacrifice Taylor and multi-instrumentalist Scott Hirsch's knack for bridging the familiar and a whiff of mystery. Megafaun's Phil Cook will both play an opening set here and perform as part of HGM.


InDIGenous: Chinchano. Central Library, 7 p.m. (free)

In his band Chinchano, young Chicago-based percussionist Juan Pastor draws upon the variety of folk and jazz sounds he soaked up in his native Lima, Peru, and brings them into expansive conversation with an equally broad array of North American jazz influences. The project's self-titled 2014 album shows Pastor emerging as a composer who grasps the fundamental jazz tradition of setting off unforced, serendipitous-feeling collisions of styles. He plays here as part of the InDIGenous series, which showcases regional artists and their original compositions.


Todd Snider, Rorey Carroll. Barrymore, 7:30 p.m.

Nashville songwriter Todd Snider has persistently sported a volatile, smart-assed persona, and he usually comes off as smart enough to twist that into something beautiful. That doesn't mean that his 21-year solo discography is without rough patches or muddled gestures, but when Snider's work hits the right balance, it bursts with careening narratives and humor that leaves an emotional sting after the initial belly laugh. He plays here behind last year's memoir I Never Met A Story I Didn't Like and album Cheatham Street Warehouse.


Reel Love LGBT Film Festival. Union South Marquee, through April 26. (free)

WUD Film's four-day fest is a diverse take on contemporary LGBT cinema, ranging from last year's dignified, moving Love Is Strange (Thursday) to more transgressive fare like the French thriller Stranger By The Lake (Saturday) and Peter Strickland's Duke Of Burgundy (also Saturday). Even if you're still recovering from the Wisconsin Film Festival, it's a deep enough lineup to warrant checking out the full schedule and digging in.

Ben Bailey. Comedy Club On State, through April 25, see link for all showtimes.

Sometimes a career takes odd directions. Ben Bailey started out as a stand-up and then found himself as host of the surprises game show for folks just looking to get to the other side of town on a little Discovery Channel show called Cash Cab. Now, Bailey's back on the comedy circuit (his new Comedy Central Presents special airs this Sunday), and will find himself on stage all weekend at The Comedy Club On State. If someone in a bachelorette party drunkenly bleats-out “Hey! It's the Cash Cab guy!" towards the beginning of his set, well, the show's paid for itself already.

FRIDAY APRIL 24

Charlie Parr. Stoughton Opera House, 7:30 p.m.

Duluth-based singer and guitarist Charlie Parr has spent years winding his way through formidable, gnarled renditions of folk and blues songs, with a delivery that alternates between conversationally unassuming and staggeringly powerful (what Parr wrings from sturdy standards like “God Moves On The Water" borders on frightening), not to mention the excellent instrumental detours of last year's album Hollandale. But his new album Stumpjumper is special in that it focuses squarely on Parr's voice as a songwriter—10 of its 11 tracks are originals, and standouts like “Remember Me If I Forget" and “Over The Red Cedar" show he has much to say beyond the folk traditions he grasps so well. The lyrics on those and other Stumpjumper tracks evoke a sense of people, values, and memories coming unglued amid the assault of time and human fickleness. It's still good, gritty, self-effacing fun on its surface, but as always, you can hear something deeper tugging at the man.


Outfest: Le1f, DJ Lizzy T, Drag Queen Bingo. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. (free)

Since NYC rapper and producer Le1f first emerged on our radar with his minimally pounding, club-ready production on Spank Rock and Big Freedia's “Nasty," we've kept a watchful eye on his evolution as a full-on rapper. The results, which culminated in 2014's Hey EP, have been heavily rewarding. Featuring a few self-produced tracks (and a couple contributions from Dubbel Dutch and 5kinAndBone5) that drift through the minimal, crunk-leaning vibes of “Hey," the Southern-fried trap bent of “Sup," and the sub-rattling, downtempo bass workout of “Buzz," Le1f''s smooth and swaggering delivery spits dynamically above the heady backdrops with playful precision. Le1f's performance is part of Outfest, a collaboration between WUD Music and the LGBT Campus Center to celebrate the former's Out And About Month.

SATURDAY APRIL 25

Busking For Books. State Street, 1 p.m. (free, donations requested)
Update: this event has been postponed until May 9 due to weather concerns.

This annual fundraiser for Madison-based Literacy Network, which helps Dane County adults with basic literacy training and English-language classes for non-native speakers, has grown into one of Madison's most charming spring traditions. At corners up and down State Street, local musicians will put on all manner of acoustic performances, busking for donations that will all go to support Literacy Network's efforts. Each year brings familiar Madison acts and unpredictable one-offs—highlights in the schedule this time around include small-instrument-wielding charmers The Tiny Band, German Art Students singer/guitarist Annelies Howell performing solo, the Forward! Marching Band, and multi-dimensional loon DB Pedersen.

*hitmayng, Somewear Sound, The Visis, Asumaya, Gentle Brontosaurus. Frequency, 9 p.m.

Despite *hitmayng mastermind Ian Carroll's insistence that he'll be changing his alias soon, the Madisonian producer, DJ, and curator of his label Catch Wreck (which have seen a couple killer releases this year in rapper CRASHprez's More Perfect. and sample-wizard Coby Ashpis' Loafer EP) has already done plenty to keep his moniker fresh in everyone's heads this year. Between lending his slick production on the aforementioned More Perfect. and an immersive split with Greenville, South Carolina-based producer Eli Colburn that features scattered recordings from the last few years called Lost Tapes, Carroll's current live set will hopefully feature a window into any new turns his spacious, meandering, and sample-laden grooves are taking.

Proud Parents, Fox Face, Dirty Sads. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

Proud Parents are one of the more melodic and power-pop-oriented bands to spring recently from Madison's busy little garage-punk scene. Here, the band celebrates a new live cassette recorded last year at the Crystal Corner Bar, and it's a scruffy but charming introduction to the band's work. Hear more about it in our interview with members Tyler Fassnacht and Claire Nelson-Lifson, whose old band Giant People also plays a reunion show at Mickey's on Monday.

An Evening At Maria's: Cris Plata, Tim Radl, Craig Werner. 1444 East Washington Avenue, 7 p.m.

SUNDAY APRIL 26

Two-Lane Blacktop. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

James Taylor and late Beach Boy Dennis Wilson star in this 1971, Monte Hellman-directed, existential road picture, playing two nameless drifters who capitalize on traveling from state to state and challenging people to drag races. In the company of a woman the two picked up hitchhiking, they eventually meet a GTO driver played by Warren Oates, after which Oates and the duo of Wilson and Taylor challenge each other to a cross-country race from New Mexico to Washington, DC, with the prize being the rights to the loser's car. The film is being shown as part of UW Cinematheque's Universal '71 series, which pays tribute to several quality and/or adventurous films with smaller budgets that Universal Pictures released in 1971 in response to the success of Easy Rider.

Wisconsin Festival Of Ideas. Wisconsin Union Theater, 10 a.m. (free)

MONDAY APRIL 27

Giant People, Pale Angels, Zinky Boys. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

Pale Angels, whose members are split between Wales and the United States, make driving, hoarse-voiced power-pop that leans just a bit toward raggedy and static-blasted texture. Songs like “If Only You're Gone," from the forthcoming album Imaginary People, strike a nice balance, sounding blown-out enough to create a sense of volatility, but not enough to overwhelm the band's scraggly charm. Pale Angels play here with a newer Madison outfit, Zinky Boys, and a reunited one, Giant People, which broke up a few years ago with members going on to play in bands including Fire Retarded, Proud Parents, and Sacerdote.


Rhiannon Giddens, Bhi Bhiman. Capitol Theater, 7:30 p.m.

TUESDAY APRIL 28

Finding Vivian Maier. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

In this 2013 documentary, filmmakers John Maloof and Charlie Siskel track down the story behind a unearthed trove of extraordinary street-photography negatives they acquired in a 2007 auction. They eventually identified the photographer as Vivian Maier, but knew almost nothing about her until after she died in 2009. In life, Maier worked as a nanny and never published her work, and the documentary's attempt to better understand her somewhat secret life as a great photographer only ends up deepening the mystery.

WEDNESDAY APRIL 29

Janeane Garofalo. Majestic, 7:30 p.m.

It feels strange to call Janeane Garofalo an institution, and yet here we are. Folks maybe know her best from movies ( Reality Bites, The Truth About Cats And Dogs, Wet Hot American Summer), in addition to her work on Mr. Show and The Ben Stiller Show, but she got her start doing stand-up comedy and was part of the first wave of “alternative" comedians who broke down barriers by bringing their notebooks on stage and charted the course for a whole generation of humor by staring at their shoes. She's never stopped doing stand-up, but actual documentation of her legendary material (her 1995 HBO Comedy Half-Hour was #5, and the only one by a female comic, on IFC's 10 Most Innovative Stand-Up Comedy Specials list) is relatively sparse. She's only released one album in her whole career, 2010's If You Will, making the opportunity to catch her act in person a rare one.


Nerd Nite. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m. (free)

This month's Nerd Nite talks include presentations on spiders, the history of televised professional wrestling, and an apparently new theoretical physics/pop-culture concept called the Swayze Theory.

Julian Lynch, Web Of Sunsets, Chants. Mickey's Tavern, 10:30 p.m. (free)

Minneapolis psych-folk trio Web Of Sunsets find virtue in hazy, lulling atmosphere. While often swathed in bright-toned, reverb-soaked guitar, the band's songs have strong enough vocal melodies and smart enough production that they're too engaging to be reduced as passive chill-out music. The band will play here fresh off the release of a new EP, Steel New Days, and Madison's own Julian Lynch and Chants round this out as a great, spaced-out bill.

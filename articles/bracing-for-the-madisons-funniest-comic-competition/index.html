Bracing for the Madison’s Funniest Comic Competition

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Ben Munson
@benjaminmunson /
February 03, 2015 @ 7:16 pm

events

Two Tone writers discuss the strange attraction of the annual competition, which begins Wednesday night.

Between this Wednesday night and next, a record 82 contestants are slated to take the mic at the Comedy Club On State for the preliminary round of the annual Madison's Funniest Comic Competition. Since it began in 2010, the competition has rewarded deserving, if often relatively safe, local comics, but the experience has always turned out to be pretty intense, in a way that makes picking a winner seem almost beside the point. Tone Madison writers Scott Gordon and Ben Munson have both watched their way through several of the competitions, and have both served as guest judges at different times. They traded some notes recently about what audience members can expect. The competition runs every Wednesday in February, with the final rounds happening on March 4 and 5.

Scott: So Ben, we've both been part of the judging a couple times and been to a few of these. This year again, they're planning two nights of preliminaries, which will slash the field down from 82 comics—the most people who've ever entered. Especially given all that volume, I would submit to you that the annual competition is less about finding out who's the funniest comic in town and more about the experience of plowing through dozens of sets, the good and the bad. It's sort of a hyper-concentrated version of what you might experience at the club's Wednesday night open-mic over the course of several months. I'm being somewhat affectionate when I say that you might as well go into it with a masochistic, perverse attitude. I mean, it can take years and even decades to truly determine the merits of a stand-up comedian, or for a comic to find his or her voice. So is it fair to say that a competition like this is more about the spectacle?

Ben: You are correct. If watching one stand-up comic bomb is enough to make you squirm, the potential for carpet bombing here should already have your guts tied in sympathetic knots. Or if you like watching human suffering, this event is good, too. But as someone who's attended multiple consecutive years, you have probably had the good fortune to see standups return and perform much better under the pressure, right?

Scott: Seeing people improve is definitely a part of it, but over the years the more interesting part is seeing the field of local comics, and their relationship with the audience, evolve. I think it's a credit to the competition (or just a sign of how unpredictable these things can be) that the winners have included somewhat broader comics like Sean Moore and Bryan Morris but also at least one genuinely out-there comic, David Fisher. Which is cool not because I care about who wins, but because it's good to see the audience embrace someone like that. In any case, yes, keeping up with local comedy definitely teaches you not to write someone off just because they didn't initially grab you. And of course, some people just never learn, but that's part of the charm too.


Ben: You've been a judge before, right? I was asked to judge once and I was on a panel with Montee Ball. Nate Tice, a former Wisconsin Badgers football player whose dad is Mike Tice, former head coach of the Vikings, was there, too. I remember him being very cool when I asked him what it was like to know that many people in Wisconsin actively hated his father. But my point is, I feel like the voting procedure was fairly complex. Or maybe that was because of drinks.

Scott: I was a judge once and I think I shared the honors with some drive-time radio dudes. The judging in previous years has been sort of a “local celebrity" goof deal, contributing to the feeling that it's more of a promotion for the Comedy Club On State than anything—contestants show up, they bring friends, the friends pay a cover and buy drinks. Like just about any typically comedy club-ish aspect—fluorescent shared fishbowl drinks, the host shouting out to bachelorette parties—I think that's largely redeemed by the fact that the club has made itself a decent hub for local comics. I remember the judging being a little convoluted too—that's usually the case any time someone has a competition or awards thing in local arts (see years of people complaining about the Madison Area Music Awards' voting setup, and organizers going, “But look, we do it that way because [insert long-winded and unhelpful explanation here].") And it's funny considering the audience is supposed to be getting drunk and the judges are supposed to be loading up on free ice-cream drinks. This year, according to Buettner, they're changing up the judging, hand-picking a bunch of comedy fans and frequent customers instead of radio personalities and business owners. Buettner tells me it'll basically be the same judges each week, but with people from talent agencies and other comedy clubs joining the panel in the final rounds.

The changes in judging suggest to me that the club is really beginning to take this seriously, and that's cool. But the real reason audiences keep coming back, I think, is that the folks in Madison who come out and try to do comedy have been able to provide both sloppy spectacle and a respectable amount of funny, compelling stand-up. Again, I think it's a better time if you just forget about the concept of narrowing it down until only the funniest person is left, and just embrace the glory and the squalor. It's essentially a goofy concept turned awesome by the dedication of a core group of local comics, and instead of a generic comedy-club experience, it's become a distinctly Madison one.

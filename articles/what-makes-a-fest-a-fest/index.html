What makes a fest a fest?

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
December 16, 2013 @ 7:22 pm

read

Branding events as "festivals": Spurious, blatant, silly, or all of the above?

(Pictured: Superchunk, which admittedly probably makes a fest a fest.)

I would like to address something that I call "festival creep." This is a phenomenon in which an otherwise pretty normal bill of music increasingly constitutes a "festival," or some other spuriously/sloppily branded event.

(I guess we could also define "festival creep" as a phenomenon in which different kinds of festivals increasingly dominate the live music discussion and economy. At any rate one definition feeds into the other in an obvious way.)

Certainly, one cannot blame Madison's concert promoters for trying to sell tickets and attract touring bands to Madison in the dead of winter (True Endeavors/Frank Production's FRZN Fest), or for hitching their star to local bands with a strong following (the Majestic's onslaught of four "Wisconsin [genre] Fest" shows in January). Neither can one blame local musicians/labels from teaming up to showcase their stuff (Mine All Mine Records' Lost City Music Festival, which has taken place the past two summers, or this past year's inaugural Bubble Music Festival).

If I were crunching the numbers for a crappy city-ranking article, I might be compelled to note that Madison seems to have become a hotbed of new festival action on top of all its long-running annual neighborhood fests and World Music Fest and so forth. But since I actually live here as an audience member/ticket buyer, my reaction is actually to wonder why the concept of a "music festival" still matters so much in a local context.

Now, I'm not saying nothing is ever *actually* a festival or worthy of some kind of theme beyond "a band at a place." But I am saying that the main reason to call an event a festival is simple marketing. It gives the event a semblance of packaging and purpose, therefore making a ticket purchase seem more reasonable.

Most importantly, it gives media outlets and the social-media hamster wheel a greater hook for coverage and discussion. For media types, it's suddenly not just another bill of music, but a whole different story and concept, even if it really just isn't. If a promoter is lucky, a "festival" billing will get those pesky journos thinking "hey, what's the deal with this new festival!?" instead of "what about the music at this show--what's good, what's bad, is it worth everyone's time and money artistically?" Reporters might even run a story that quotes the organizer (inevitably) saying that the festival will be great event that brings people together and celebrates "the local scene."

It magically adds an appearance of excitement and curation, even when the "festival" largely features local and regional bands who already play Madison fairly often (the Majestic Fests, Lost City Fest); comprises a package deal of several club shows in a row (FRZN Fest); or doesn't have any particularly cohesive theme (the inaugural Bubble Fest). Then there's the national Communion series, currently taking place monthly at The Frequency, which revolves around the noble but not novel concept of pairing up local and touring acts for mutual benefit. Not to mention full-on, multi-venue festivals that tank after a few short years because of the logistical and financial strain, and maybe, just maybe, because their format doesn't entirely make sense for local audiences (Forward Music Festival). Or the new Revelry Music and Arts Festival, which actually made sense as a college-targeted event, or Freakfest, which is just silly.

Yes, I realize that such things as "festival" are used precisely for their Pavlovian magnetism. Yes, I realize that the practitioners of "branding" as discussed here will be picking their teeth with my bones one day. And most people are already inured enough to tacky/insulting music marketing that perhaps this entire discussion doesn't really matter. I am not naive.

That said, even from a purely marketing-minded perspective, the use of the term "festival" is going to get watered-down after a while, and perhaps those tempted to use it could find more clever ways to present their events. Maybe start calling them all "pop-ups"?

But the real question is, how much does the purported "festival" experience actually differ from your average show? This January's FRZN Fest at least boasts the mighty Superchunk and several good younger acts, which helps it live up to that feeling of scarcity or exclusivity that I think helps to justify a "festival" billing. Then again, if spending too many nights drinking and listening to music at the High Noon Saloon is a festival, then by god my life is a festival. As for the Majestic's "fest" series, just about every night at the Majestic features $3 tap water and $5 PBR tallboys presented as "a drink special, which is exactly what one would expect in the godforsaken gouge-scape of Bonnaroo or Lollapalooza. And some festivals either raise money for charity or operate at an obvious financial loss for the sake of the music, which helps to firm up that sense of purpose.

But at the end of the day, it's branding, and the branding folks of the world operate like your proverbial man with a hammer. Several of these "fests" are plenty worthwhile musically, and if you want to fest it up, go ahead and enjoy it. But let's not let "festival" terminology override our discussions and decisions about live music, and let's get a little more curious about things that aren't "festivals" or "branded thingies."

Anyhow, I'd now like to announce Arts Extract Club Show Fest, featuring music, shelter, and alcohol sales, together for the first time! Stand by for updates, as we'll be tacking on some "buzz-type" Twin Cities bands that only play Madison every three weeks or so!

Madison calendar, December 10 through 16

Clockwise from top left: Austin Lucas plays the High Noon on December 10, Amber Sowards' Captured debuts at the Arts + Literature Lab on December 10, Vince Staples plays a sold out show at the Sett on December 11, UW Cinematheque screens Guy Maddin's The Forbidden Room screens on December 12.geo

events

Austin Lucas, Vince Staples, Tom Papa, Deerhunter, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY DECEMBER 10

Luke Arvid, Marty Finkel, Vein Rays. Frequency, 9 p.m.

Luke Arvid has emerged as a sleeper gem among Madison’s singer-songwriters in the last couple of years. His two albums, 2014’s Arc and this year’s Day Of The Bu1lder, have relied on unassuming craft and sly but disarming melodies on standouts like “Righteous” and “Pennies.” Live and on record, a solid band that includes Madison guitar heavy Louka Patenaude fleshes out the material with skilled but non-flashy warmth. Arvid plays here as he finishes up recording a new album, Day Of The Bu2lder, which he says will be a more ambient, atmospheric companion to Day Of The Bu1lder. Fellow Madison songwriter Marty Finkel hasn’t put out a release for a couple years, but will have some new songs to share at this show.


Cleo From 5 To 7. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

If the past couple of weeks of UW Cinematheque’s deeper looks into French new wave legend Agnes Varda’s ourve piqued your interest, WUD Film is coming to your rescue with Cleo From 5 To 7, easily Varda’s most well-known feature. The film follows the real-time experiences of Cleo, a pop singer who is waiting to hear from her doctor about a possible cancer diagnosis, so she wanders semi-aimlessly around the streets of 1960s Paris. If you’ve ever had to wait on a heavy thing like that, then you know how things start to fall away and you refocus both externally and internally, and film manages to capture the emotional depth and conflicted energy of such a fraught experience.


Craig Werner and Douglas Bradley. A Room of One’s Own, 6 p.m. (free)

Vietnam veteran and writer Doug Bradley and UW-Madison professor Craig Werner visit A Room of One’s Own here to discuss their recently published collaborative book, We Gotta Get Out Of This Place: The Soundtrack Of The Vietnam War. While the book certainly touches on a lot of the iconic music associated with the Vietnam era—Hendrix, Creedence, Pete Seeger, and beyond—its real aim is to use that as a window into the diverse experiences, emotions, and backgrounds of American soldiers in Vietnam, and the complex, fast-changing times in which the war took place.

Psychoacoustics Remote Broadcast. Tip Top Tavern, 8 p.m. (free)

WORT-FM show Psychoacoustics celebrates 24 years of eccentric music and talk with this live broadcast from the Tip Top. In addition to live-broadcasted antics from hosts Reverend Velveteen Sly and Bad Sister Heidi, the night will feature performances from smart-ass Madison feminist punk duo Damsel Trash and local indie-pop experimenters Lover’s Spit.


Austin Lucas, Christopher Gold, Evan Murdock. High Noon Saloon, 9 p.m.

Indiana’s Austin Lucas approaches his music with a deft grasp of bluegrass, a high but hefty voice, and stark emotion that never tips over into despair. Lucas spent a lot of years in crusty hardcore bands before getting on his current path, and even released his first two solo albums on the punk/metal-heavy Magic Bullet label, but he’s not really a folk-punk hybridizer, even on more rocking efforts like 2013’s Stay Reckless. On songs like the stunning opening/title track of 2009’s Somebody Loves You and “How Are You My Lover” from 2008’s Putting The Hammer Down, he’s channeling the country traditions he grew up around and putting a particularly fearless stamp on them.


Opening Reception: Captured. Arts + Literature Laboratory (2021 Winnebago St.), 6 p.m. (free)

(Art show runs through Jan. 15)

Madison photographer Amber Sowards’ new art show, Captured, goes inside Dane County’s Juvenile Detention Center to capture the stories of its inmates, with a particular focus on LGBTQ youth and youth of color. The exhibit is presented by the Gay-Straight Alliance for Safe Schools and the organizers of this spring’s Justified Art! show at the Overture Center (which also included photos from Sowards). It’s also one of the first art shows to open at the new Arts + Literature Laboratory on the near-East Side.

FRIDAY DECEMBER 11

Vince Staples, CRASHprez, Lord Of The Fly. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. (sold out)

There’s been a lot of talk about the “death of the album” in recent years, especially regarding the seeming data-dump approach to sequencing and time length common to many hip-hop mixtapes. But many of the most important recordings made in hip-hop this year—including Kendrick Lamar’s To Pimp A Butterfly, Earl Sweatshirt’s I Don’t Like Shit, I Don’t Go Outside, and Future’s DS2—were obviously conceived as beginning-to-end statements. Vince Staples’ Summertime ‘06—an hour-long double album, cut in two for thematic impact rather than time constraints—is possibly the most clearly conceived of all of them. The young Long Beach, California rapper’s proper debut is a relentless work, so resolutely unconcerned with commercial potential that it’s surprising to realize that Def Jam released it (albeit with shamefully little promotion). Over consistently sparse and unsettlingly warped beats overseen by executive producer No I.D., Staples initially starts with virtuosically rendered gangsta exploits. But an unmistakable sense of corrosive self-loathing steadily seeps through every song. Disc two sees Staples move further into penetrating social critique, and he increasingly implies that he holds the listener as responsible for the problems explored in the music as anyone else. An increasing sense of fatalism takes hold as the album progresses, along with a hopeless recognition of how class and race is intertwined in American society. It all ends in a burst of static, which is a conceptual coup: the album contemptuously turns itself off for you, indicating that many hip-hop listeners switch the channel when voyeurism becomes too uncomfortable. Staples’ show here was announced late and the free tickets disappeared quickly, so here’s hoping Staples can bring across the album’s focus and emotional complexity on stage.


Tom Papa. Comedy Club on State, through December 12, see link for all showtimes

It might seem like a slight to present Tom Papa as a poor man’s Seinfeld, but when you think of what it costs to see Seinfeld these days, even a poor man’s version of that is pretty damn fancy. When you discover that Tom Papa’s spent the better part of his career as Seinfeld’s go-to opener, the comparison is even more apt. While the two are more than a little intertwined professionally, Papa’s comedy is a bit looser and more off-the-cuff—he’s no less a well-put-together and buttoned-down professional, but he exhaustively attacks each topic from a number of different angles until it is wrung dry. Expect a classy weekend of packed shows (early shows on Friday and Saturday have already sold out). Detroit’s Mike Stanley features and Chicago’s Reena Calm hosts.


Aparajito. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

If you missed last week’s first installation last week in Satyajit Ray’s landmark Apu Trilogy, Panther Panchali, you should maybe catch up on that before jumping into this week’s Aparajito, or, since each installment stands on its own well enough, don’t. Either way, you’re in for a treat with this, the second chapter of Apu’s early-life story, detailing his adolescence and the quiet struggle of Brahmin-class family life. As we noted last week, it’s a miracle that the film exists in this remarkable a quality after having to be restored frame by frame after the master prints were damaged in a nitrate fire. Don’t pass up the opportunity to dive in to this series, even if it is nearly mid stream by this point. The trilogy concludes next week with The World Of Apu.

SATURDAY DECEMBER 12

The Low Czars Hanukkah Hoedown. Mickey’s Tavern, 10:30 p.m. (free)

The Low Czars are a self-effacing Madison cover band with a gift for digging into the deep (and sometimes not-so-deep) reaches of garage-rock, power-pop, and R&B. And not only are the setlists impressive—from familiar Cheap Trick and Kinks songs to left-field selections from Swamp Dogg or Bubble Puppy—but the execution is solid, fun, and unpretentious. Read more in our recent interview with the band.

Gregory Taylor + Tom Hamer, Geodes. Arts + Literature Laboratory, 8 p.m. (free)

The first music event at the new Arts + Literature Laboratory (which also has an art show opening this week—see Thursday’s events above) is a full-on experimental one. The Madison duo of electronic musician Gregory Taylor and percussionist Tom Hamer will headline with an improvised, ambient-leaning set that Taylor says will involve “a mixture of contact-miked objects and tabletop percussion processed in real time.” Milwaukee’s Geodes will also be on the bill, bringing their own mix of computer-generated sound and free improvisation.

The Forbidden Room. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

Guy Maddin is one of those avant-garde directors who ride the line between a much broader pop culture awareness, but never quite cross over, not that he could probably give a shit. His films are all seemingly acid dreams that take place in a derelict 1920s film studio, each shot on equipment from that era of silent film history, or otherwise twisted in some way to that appearance. While the trailer for his newest, The Forbidden Room, gives very little insofar as plot for you to expect, you should be prepared to deal with not only a creepy submarine crew, but also some forest bandit creeps, a child army (creepy!), and Udo Kier. While seemingly every one of Maddin’s films gets tagged early on with the “masterpiece” label, this one seems to be sticking, if the reviews are to be believed.


Wintersong. Barrymore, 8 p.m.

The annual Wintersong charity concert, now in its fourth year, raises money for Second Harvest Foodbank of Wisconsin via a holiday-themed evening be-garlanded in utterly Madison-indie-folk music. This year’s show features some usual suspects in Phox, Anna Vogelzang, Faux Fawn, and Crane Your Swan Neck, though you can expect them to focus on specially prepared yuletide fare and collaborative configurations, with appearances from sonic mischief-maker DB Pedersen.

Reverb: Versus. High Noon Saloon, 9 pm.

We’re pleased to see the High Noon picking up some of the shards of recently shattered goth fixture Club Inferno and lending its stage to some of that venue’s regular DJs and performers. Likely bridging the gaps between dark, pummeling, and dance-powered synth pop, industrial, and moody techno cuts, Reverb: Versus will feature DJs like WhiteRabbit, Psychotr0n, and MC Audio owner and overall Madison stalwart Mike Carlson. Bizarro performance artists Kreskents and Lilly Violet will also appear. We’re not sure what to expect, but it probably wouldn’t be out of line to expect laborious costuming, hooks, and fire being played with liberally.

Ethicist, The Unnecessary Gunpoint Lecture, Tyranny Is Tyranny. The Wisco, 9:30 p.m.

Cincinnati band Ethicist draw on the sustained pummeling of black metal and the expansive song structures of post-rock, a mix that usually leads you to expect billowy and hypnotic results (think Deafheaven or Wolves In The Throne Room). Ethicist certainly realize the potential for texture and beauty in that combination, but their 2013 self-titled album and the new 8-minute song “Crickets” are a bit more austere, keeping the hazy reverb in check and foregrounding sharp-toned guitar and snares that lock into post-punk fury between the heady trudging moments. Madison band The Unnecessary Gunpoint Lecture plays here behind the recent release Sociopathy: A First Hand Account, which showcases the band’s legitimately impressive bizarro-technical-death-metal chops alongside its mangled sense of humor—what with song titles like “We Hate Your Children, But We Love Your Mother's Cornbread.”


SUNDAY DECEMBER 13

The Shop Around The Corner. Chazen Museum of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

If you, like all of us here at the Tone Madison offices, have a soft spot for You’ve Got Mail, Nora Ephron’s delightful Meg Ryan-Tom Hanks vehicle set in New York, then it’s essential to follow up on its inspiration, Ernst Lubitsch’s 1940 film The Shop Around The Corner. Set in Budapest, the film stars Jimmy Stewart and Margaret Sullavan as two professional rivals who accidentally and unknowingly fall in love through private and anonymous correspondence—a plot that’s sweet, but never sticky. It’s a real shame that this erstwhile Christmas classic has seemingly gotten lost in the sands of time in favor of more schmaltzy holiday fare like Miracle On 34th Street, but luckily for those who are looking for a little less treacle in their stocking this year, the UW Cinematheque has come to the rescue gifting us this gem on glorious 35mm. [Update: This will be screening in a digital projection, due to an issue with shipping the print.]


TUESDAY DECEMBER 15

Deerhunter, Atlas Sound. Majestic, 8:30 p.m.

As Deerhunter’s profile has risen steadily over the last decade, frontman and multi-instrumentalist Bradford Cox has reluctantly settled into his accidental persona as a curmudgeonly feather-ruffler who enjoys both screwing with his fans and sporadically talking shit about the overall state of his contemporaries. Despite Cox’s grumpy contrarian shtick, Fading Frontier may be the Atlanta-based outfit’s most agreeable and least confrontational collection yet. And no, we’re not complaining. We’re happy to welcome the band’s hypnotic and polished dream-rock tunes as they take on their most concise pop forms to date. Cox’s simplistic and incredibly effective crooning soars carefully and clearly over the fuzzy guitar layers and electronic flourishes of “All The Same” or the slight rhythmic swing that treads under a wash of heady synth lines and and strummed acoustic guitar on “Duplex Planet.” Another couple of favorites are “Snakeskin,” which somehow owes as much to Tom Petty as it does to Van McCoy’s “The Hustle,” and the deconstructed and sprawling progression of “Leather And Wood.” While there’s been no mention of a new album from Cox’s Atlas Sound project as of yet, Cox will be doing double duty as the long-running, electronically-driven solo endeavor kicks this show off.


Rebel Reporting Book Launch. Rainbow Bookstore, 6 p.m. (free)

Journalist John Ross died in 2011, but left behind many lectures and lessons drawn from the decades he spent covering (and advocating alongside) the political left in Mexico. Those lessons are collected in a new book, Rebel Reporting, edited by WORT-FM’s own Cristalyne Bell and Norman Stockwell. Bell and Stockwell will be talking at this launch event for the book, as will outspoken media scholar and media-reform advocate Robert McChesney, who contributed a foreword to the book.

WEDNESDAY DECEMBER 16

Nerd Nite Madison. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m. (free)

The final Nerd Nite Madison of the year will feature informal talks on the underappreciated nuances of soils, the communication habits of animals, and—this last one from Tone Madison’s own Chris Lay—the sordid reputation of R. Kelly. As usual, we’ll be at the event recording the talks for our monthly Tone Madison/Nerd Nite audio series.

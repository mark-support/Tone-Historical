Tone Madison's Top 20 Madison Records of 2013

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
December 19, 2013 @ 10:00 am

listen

The best local best-of list, by human people with ears.

Here are our favorite Madison-made albums and EPs from 2013, culled from our best efforts to keep up with local artists across genres throughout the year. Ground rules: We’ve chosen a top 20, not ranked but in alphabetical order. Honorable mentions after that. We have included some releases by artists who have left Madison, but only if the release came out before they moved. Some of our friends and acquaintances are included, but not given preference on the basis of personal connections. Selections based on what we enjoyed listening to the most this year, and what we’ll most want to keep listening to in the future. Here goes, and don't forget about our year-end mix, 11 Madison Songs We Loved in 2013.

THE TOP 20

All Tiny Creatures, ‘Dark Clock’

While the tunes on ‘Dark Clock’ are coated in a granulated spray of influences ranging from Steve Reich to Scritti Politti, this electronic-pop album stays faithful to nothing but itself. Throughout the past decade, electronic music and indie-rock have shared a turbulent marriage at best, but ATC’s seamless blend actually leaves the listener feeling that they’re pushing both genres forward. But hey, don’t take our word for it, just cruise through the record and consider the way Thomas Wincek’s falsetto washes over the Cluster-esque swirls of synth and guitar in “Comets” or how Andy Fitzpatrick’s guitar solo falls over the future-pop Peter Gabriel-worship of “Quickest Cut.”

Aquarelle, ‘August Undone’

Minneapolis transplant Ryan Potts’ latest effort finds him throwing down brain-tickling, full-frequency slabs of lush, ambient compositions. After several headfirst plunges into the chiming cascades of centerpiece “A Flare” or sitting under the devastating waterfall of “Clockless Hours,” all we can do is thank the drone gods that Aquarelle keeps his layers vast and the beats far away, no matter how tempting a techno makeover gets.

Nick Brown, ‘Slow Boat’

Nick Brown’s first album begins with the somber, arresting “Living That Way” and proceeds to resourcefully bounce in all manner of directions, all the while retaining sparse, dignified arrangements. Brown plays with classic-country tropes on “Light Beer And Heavy Hearts,” goes on a smart-assed journey of disappointment on “Factory Farms,” takes a jaunty gambling trip to Canada on “Hold On Windsor.” Though this naturally makes for a slightly uneven experience, it’s also one of rewarding surprises, and one of the best singer-songwriter records a local artist has released in recent years.

Chants, ‘I Feel Like I Feel It’

Thanks to his background as a drummer for Mama Digdown’s Brass Band, Jordan Cohen has carried his rhythmic intellect over to the sample-powered bass tunes he crafts as Chants. On is second LP, ‘I Feel Like I Like It,’ Cohen seriously steps his game up. While the instrumental end was already charged with slicker production, more vibrantly hypnotic progressions, and a wider scope of emotional dynamics than last year’s already-great ‘Night After’ EP, we also really dug the welcome addition of vocals via Ramzi Awn (“Don’t Miss U” and “RU4Real”) and Madisonian songwriter Brandon Beebe (“City In A Bottle”). The whole record is tied together by the title-track, in which the beat launches back and forth between a spacey crawl into an infectious, footworking strut.

Conjuror, ‘Left Hand Path Of The Conjuror’

Released on a CD-R with handmade artwork via experimentalist Troy Schafer’s Shifting Sands Congregation label, Ian Adcock’s gorgeous collection of dark, bedroom synth-meditations cannot go unnoticed. It’s getting tougher and tougher to navigate the veritable sea of instrumental synth records that surround us these days, but these longform analog burners are refreshingly restrained and focused. The ways the textured, alien alarms of “Black Poppies At Midnight Hour” fade in and the sparse synth droplets of album closer “Delta Of Venus” spill into an oceanic drone that sinisterly erupts, pans out, and fades to black give ‘Left Hand Path’ a bleakly cinematic and undeniably powerful feel.

Dietrich Gosser, ‘Oh To Begin!’

Dietrich Gosser’s first album in five years offers both complement and contrast in Madison’s well-liked circle of singer-songwriter artists. In some ways it’s less noisy and frizzy than his previous releases, but it takes chances in different ways, opening with the ominous “Reservation” and proceeding to explore a mix of weariness and optimism on its remaining tracks. Drummer Dan Kuemmel used to bring a dramatic cacophony of percussion and junkyard objects to Gosser’s songs, but here he focuses in on conversational rhythm, making room in the mix for delicate electronics and strings (the title track) and hearty arrangements of guitar and vocal harmony (“Cash Crop,” “Wood And Water”).

The Hussy, ‘Pagan Hiss’

Last year’s ‘Weed Seizure’ saw The Hussy graduating from wacky-garage-punks to imaginative songwriters, and this year’s ‘Pagan Hiss’ goes way further down that golden road of smart-assed vocal interplay, infectious riffs, and clever studio trickery (for instance, the warped ending of mid-tempo ripper “Zummer”). We still remember when The Hussy was just that super-fun local band that played too loud and might set their stuff on fire. We suppose that still holds true, but now they’re also a blisteringly dynamic duo of songwriters with an ear for bizarrely potent hooks, which is why they’re finally on the way to much-deserved national recognition.

Lens, 'Visions Meet Light In The Cult House'

Dub abstractionist Dan Woodman first snagged our attention as a member of drone-collage duo Drunjus. However, the last few years have found Woodman breaking away as a solo artist under the Lens moniker, furthering his own mellowly mutant vision—one cassette-only release at a time. ‘Visions Meet Light In The Cult House’ clocks in at a whopping 82 minutes. Side A delivers a more up-to-date look at Lens. It’s loaded with blown-out, jaggedly minimal anti-rhythms, whizzing synth explorations, and creepy tape-loop collaging. Side B offers a reversion to the stoney minimalism of Woodman’s older, dronier material. Definitely a nice introduction to one of Madison’s most exciting experimental dudes.

Little Legend, ‘No Way To Tell’ EP

In its short existence, Little Legend has accomplished the unlikely feat of both being refreshing and drawing hard on Springsteen-type yarns about grizzled, well-meaning tough guys. The ‘No Way To Tell’ EP captures a rougher side of their playing and songwriting, yet there’s lots of finesse and melody in Brandy Tudor and Joe Copeland’s guitar parts. Mostly, though, the power is in how much Tudor believes in his characters: “Heavyweight” certainly isn’t the first tale of someone moping over his past glories, but Tudor’s lines (“respect comes cheap and then it fades”) and Robbie Schiller’s mounting drums get you invested.

Julian Lynch, ‘Lines’

Julian Lynch’s previous albums have tended to feel like worlds in miniature, which was part of the charm, and a product of his playful songwriting and analog home-recording approach. But Lynch’s sound started to breathe a lot more on 2011’s ‘Terra,’ a process that continues nicely on ‘Lines.’ The biggest difference is that Lynch plays so much clarinet on the album, right from the slightly discordant start of “Going.” But for all the eccentric instrumentation, tilting melody, and muzzled vocals he packs into this album, the finest moment might be the swaying guitar phrases of “North Line,” which offers a lovely contrast to the often busy, layered feel of Lynch’s work.

Madden, ‘Make It Go Away’ EP

Many of Solomon Brown’s tunes feel heavily wired for the club (and this isn’t a bad thing), packed with brain-rattling bass, massive rhythms, chirping synths, and poppy vocal samples, but when he scales back he hits a startling emotional nerve. The title track on this almost shamefully quietly released EP spins Madden’s bass-powered sound into a cathartic composition (with a little help from an expertly utilized sample of Maxwell’s cover of Kate Bush’s “This Woman’s Work”). While the other tunes on the EP don’t quite tug at the heartstrings like “Make It Go Away,” the infectious collaboration with ex-Madisonian producer Neuport (“Ice Wall”), the frozen crawls of “Movado Watch” and “Kava,” and the more playful bump of “Mass” still bring a ton to the table in their own rights.

Panther, ‘Speed Bitch Motor Fuck’ EP

Panther’s crushing thrash-metal debut--the aptly-titled Speed Bitch Motor Fuck--drills one point crucial point into the skulls of its listeners; thrash-metal has aged pretty fucking well. This holds especially true when said genre is placed in the gauntleted arms of members of fellow Madison acts Deep Shit, Jex Thoth, Sardonyx, and Pyroklast. Even if the raw power of whiplash destroyer “Ride The Ice” and nasty biker anthem “Leather Rider” sounds at home on cassette, the breakneck rhythm section, infectious riffing, throaty growls, and dizzying guitar solos make ‘SBMF’ more than worthy of a vinyl release. With all the busy musical projects that can often pull the members of Panther away from each other for months on end, we hope they’ll stick it out long enough to make this happen.

Sincere Life, ‘Write Of Passage’ EP

Sincere Life is unmistakably a young MC working his way toward a distinctive voice, but with a determination and versatility that keeps ‘Write Of Passage’ interesting. In short, just look at how different the two clear standout tracks are: “Three Kings” is a gently pitched meditation on the forces and values that rule people (over a plaintive beat by Madison’s own DLO), while “White Grape White Owls” celebrates weed with a funky, slow roll that oozes chilled-out hedonism.

Spiral Joy Band, ‘13 Moons of Doom: Birth Of The Water Dragons’

No matter how much we love watching Spiral Joy Band blast open a portal to the subconscious in a live setting, we know--and more importantly, they know--that their intoxicatingly long sets of sometimes-blissful and sometimes-piercing acoustic (and occasionally plugged-in) drone both engage listeners and seriously test their thresholds. To be fair, in the live setting, you may be stuck standing up or seated in an uncomfortable chair, or worse yet--positioned on the floor. Thankfully, the home-listening experience is far more inviting, as the 50-plus minutes of '13 Moons Of Doom: Birth Of The Water Dragons' is probably ripest for deep listening in the fetal position. Patrick Best and Mikel Dimmick (of Pelt) are joined by on-and-off SJB-member and violinist Troy Schafer and All Tiny Creatures guitarist Andy Fitzpatrick on a live recording from the late Project Lodge in Madison, WI. Harmonium and violin weave in and out of electric guitar textures in a highly rewarding series of dynamic movements. Just make sure you get comfortable first.

Tar Pet, ‘Deaf Drawing Blind Listening’

It would be hard to top the double album Madison avant-folk duo Spires That In The Sunset Rise put out in 2012, ‘Ancient Patience Wills It Again.’ On her latest solo record, member Taralie Peterson pares back on Spires’ psychedelic intensity, crafting eerily beautiful works of deconstructed strings and aching vocal melody. Songs like “Reconcile The Night With The Day” and “Poem By Fineus” are tough to wrap one’s head around, but Peterson steers through them with formidable command.

Jex Thoth, ‘Blood Moon Rise’

Featuring razor-sharp production from Randall Dunn (known for working with Earth, Sunn O))), and Sun City Girls to name just a few) and a few lineup changes, Jex Thoth finally unleashed a properly colossal follow-up to 2008’s self-titled album in the form of 'Blood Moon Rise.' This collection of dynamic dirges ranging from the suspenseful, blazing doom of “The Divide” and “Ehja” to the addicting, near-pop leanings of Danzig-tinged highlight “The Places You Walk” and the somberly crawling “Keep Your Weeds.” Frontwoman Jex Thoth’s voice soars and howls gracefully over alluringly ominous guitarwork (the solos are amazingly restrained and actually serve the songs) and a thunderous rhythm section. An elegant trudge, indeed.

Tyranny Is Tyranny, ‘Let It Come From Whom It May’

When Madison math-rock institution The United Sons Of Toil slowed down and eventually split up, there were no guarantees that anything nearly as compelling would follow (even for avowed fanboys like us). But USoT members Russell Hall and Jason Jensen veered away from that band’s jagged edges, exploring more lush post-rock dynamics in Tyranny Is Tyranny. The new group’s first album, ‘Let It Come From Whom It May,’ is equal parts rugged and graceful in its take on post-rock, from the slow tension of “Manufacturing Truth” to the lurching menace of “Down The K-Hole.”

The Wets/A Haircut, Split Cassette

You could be forgiven if you initially mistake The Wets for just a clattery indie-rock band with a blown-out mic. Benny Arnold’s vocals, Devi Arnold’s drums, and Frank Morton-Park’s guitar add up to far more, though, in the cracked yet tenderly romantic swoon of “Oh Jean,” “Pretty In Mink,” and “High Hopes.” It’s that rare release that’s a credit to low-fi indie rock tapes, and that’s equally thanks to the other side, by the joyfully fuzzy and slashing punk duo A Haircut.

Vanishing Kids, ‘Spirit Visions’

Vanishing Kids draws on post-punk sounds from the prettiest to the nastiest, then throws in histrionic vocals and occasionally prog-influenced guitars on top of all that. And yet ‘Spirit Visions’ somehow doesn’t merely become a slurry of clashing ideas—it allows enough space for all those influences to mesh, with both patient buildup and abrupt dynamic jump-cuts. In their own bleary way, tracks like “Heathen Heart” prove as musically dextrous as they are strangely ambitious.

Youngblood Brass Band, ‘Pax Volumi’

Yes, one of Madison’s more successful and long-running hometown bands is a large horn-and-percussion ensemble with a smart-assed Wisconsin native drummer spewing out the occasional impish rap. ‘Pax Volumi’ marked Youngblood Brass Band’s 15th anniversary with all the boisterousness you’d expect, but also with a sustained interest in the subtleties of both hip-hop and jazz. Sure, drummer/ringleader David Henzie-Skogen sets a smart-assed, feisty mood on opening tracks “20 Questions” and “Cite The Line,” yet there’s equal reward in the gradual burn of “Ain’t Nobody” and the solemn collective sigh of “The Old Rugged Cross / His Eye Is On The Sparrow.”

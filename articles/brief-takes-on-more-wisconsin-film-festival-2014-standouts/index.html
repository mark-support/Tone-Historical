Brief takes on more Wisconsin Film Festival 2014 standouts

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
March 31, 2014 @ 1:00 am

From Particle Fever

watch

Great shorts, a documentary on particle physics, and other WFF '14 selections worth catching.

As I wrap up my efforts to preview the 2014 Wisconsin Film Festival, which begins Thursday, I'd like to comment on nine more selections that have really stuck with me so far. There's simply no way to ever feel like you're casting a wide enough net with such an unwieldy and varied lineup, but then again, that just reinforces why the festival is such an important event in Madison's cultural life: It appeals to a diverse audience in a way that basically no other arts event in town does. I'm going to miss the first few days of the festival, but will be blogging a little more about it as I return and catch some of the films, including '20,000 Days On Earth.' Also, don't forget to check out all the preview coverage we've been doing with the folks at LakeFrontRow and Madison Film Forum.

Particle Fever: Mark Levinson's documentary chronicles the discovery of the Higgs boson, but before it delves into the tricky abstractions of particle physics, it creates a feeling of warmth and intimacy. Levinson and David Kaplan, the film's producer and one of the main protagonists in the film, center their story around the enthusiasm of several key scientists involved in the Higgs search at Switzerland's Large Hadron Collider. Given Kaplan's involvement, this could easily have been a docu-commercial for the project (OK, maybe it kind of is, to a forgivable extent, I think), but instead ends up providing a palatable, friendly introduction to the scientific endeavors that attempt to decode the fabric of existence. Part of the film's success is seizing on some unlikely opportunities: In one scene, Kaplan and a colleague discuss a sculpture on a college campus, which becomes a jumping-off point for scientific debate and a surprisingly resourceful bit of animation.

A Spell To Ward Off The Darkness: Ben Rivers and Ben Russell's film uses fluid, placid storytelling to follow an unnamed character (Robert AA Lowe) through life in a Scandinavian commune, then a period of solitude in the wilderness, then a black-metal show. (More about Lowe's background in my earlier piece on music at WFF.) Lowe's character is neither especially charismatic nor heroic, and yet he manages to draw you into what seems like an endless quest for a place to belong.

Another Song About The Sea: Jeremy Bessoff's stop-motion animation short uses two tense, lonesome-looking figurines and images of an arcane submersible to create a vague yet gripping scenario of a couple struggling to reach each other across some unexplained distance. Bessoff's own description says the film describes a relationship “darkened by mental illness," but the film also opens itself to other interpretations, likely resonating with anyone who's tried to steer a relationship through grief or any other trying stress.

AB-: Daniel Klein's nine-minute live-action film is a reminder of the excitement that simple, narrative short films can offer. Klein uses the film's short window to build suspense and tease out a great deal of morbid detail as a mysterious and ill-intentioned ambulance crew shows up at the scene of a car wreck.

Rat Pack Rat: My favorite of the WFF '14 short films I've managed to watch so far. Todd Rohal harnesses the tension between the glamorous and the hilariously disgusting as a Sammy Davis Jr. impersonator shows up to entertain a bedridden man. The latter is hooked up to a machine that's in itself the funniest, most cruelly fucked-up feat of imagination I've seen this year's WFF lineup so far. This is no mere quirky comedy: Rohal has a masterful command of the gross and uncomfortable.

Bingo Night: This, on the other hand, most certainly *is* a quirky comedy, but a well-crafted and charming one. Jordan Liebowitz shot most of this short in Madison, depicting a few senior citizens who fall into a life of crime in an effort to stay out of the state nursing home. It's excellently shot and crams in a lot of memorable characters and scenes considering it's only 13 minutes long. Full disclosure: My friend Chris Lay shows up as a karate instructor (which is indeed funny—Chris can't do karate!).

The Dog: I've got a few issues with Allison Berg and Frank Keraudren's portrayal in this documentary of John Wojtowicz, whose 1972 robbery of a Brooklyn bank inspired Sidney Lumet's film Dog Day Afternoon. There could be a little more balance between its portrait of the Wojtowicz's charmingly boisterous persona and its exploration of the state of the LGBT community in the early 1970s. (The motive for the robbery, famously, was to pay for a sex-change operation for Wojtowicz's lover.) The film also never makes clear, one way or the other, how Wojtowicz feels about the fact that his accomplice in the robbery was killed. Nonetheless, it's worth seeing to gain a little more insight into the passions that ruled the man and the way he desperately milked his fame. It's also immensely quotable: "Nobody would ever rob a bank to cut off a guy's dick to get a sex-change operation. That's why they made a movie about it!"

The Realist: Strobing and abrasive, Scott Stark's short film delves into the secret lives of department-store mannequins, to put it simply. Stark's chopped-up, animation-like technique, full of fractious layers, can make the film hard to get through (I couldn't get through it all in one sitting, even though it's only 36 minutes long). But I say that affectionately: The film's episodes and corresponding musical movements are a window into how vivid and witty experimental filmmaking can be.

R100: I both admire and hate the way Japanese writer-director Hitoshi Matsumoto turns the thriller R100 into a riff on its own implausibility. The initial plot itself could be enough: A put-upon department-store clerk (Nao Ohmori) joins a secretive S&M club whose dominatrices begin to intrude on his life in increasingly dangerous ways. For better or worse, the film takes even more chances than even this premise requires. Even early on, the film sets out to try one's suspension of disbelief to such an extent that it's hard for the viewer to ever really settle into the story. When Ohmori achieves a kind of climax of pain, his face undergoes a cheesy computer-animated swelling, accompanied by similarly artificial ripples in the air around his head. But then! There's a film-within-a-film thing. There's a dominatrix-in-chief (Lindsay Hayward) who could've burst right out of a John Waters film. There are other dominatrices with utterly grotesque specialties. And also, there are a few elements that are just fucked-up and sad enough to kill the film's suspense and humor (and you can take that from a guy who laughs inappropriately at all kinds of weird stuff in movies). Then again, at times it is incredibly funny, and even when I disliked it I never lost the sense that Matsumoto knows what he's doing.

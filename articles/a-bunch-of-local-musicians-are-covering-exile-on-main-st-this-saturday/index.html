A bunch of local musicians are covering Exile On Main St. this Saturday

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
January 22, 2015 @ 11:32 am

Dietrich Gosser, who will play "Shake Your Hips" with Brian Grimm and Dan Kuemmel.

listen

Dietrich Gosser, Annelies Howell, and others on their plans for this ambitious tribute at the High Noon.

Over the past few years Madison seems to have gone kind of nuts with tribute-band nights, mostly but not exclusively around Halloween and New Year's Eve. If you told me the furniture in my house ran away at night to practice in a tribute band, I'd probably believe you. I'm sure plenty of them are tasteful and fun but I've kind of checked out. That said, I couldn't help but be intrigued last year when Matt Earley, who plays in the band Winn Dixie, told me he was planning to get together a bunch of people to cover The Rolling Stones' 1972 double album Exile On Main St. in full. Because that's kind of an undertaking, and has the potential to be a really cool reinterpretation of a record I love, or kind of a clusterfuck, or a bit of both. This is finally happening on Saturday at the High Noon Saloon, with close to 30 different local musicians pitching in at one time or another.

Earley mostly recruited the folk- and country-leaning artists he knows around town, so the lineup is heavy on Madison singer-songwriters including Dietrich Gosser, Whitney Mann, Nick Brown, Anna Vogelzang, and Evan Murdock. (Count This Penny will be represented on a bunch of the songs by drummer Ben Wolf.) There are a few relative outliers, like indie-rock youngsters Post Social and Annelies Howell of long-running Madison power-pop band The German Art Students. I do wish the lineup reached a little more outside this particular folk circle—it'd be interesting to see how a garage/punk/psych band like Wood Chickens or a blues/folk artist like Boo Bradley would handle an Exile song—but it's still a big circle that includes a lot of talented people, some of whom are my friends and some of whom make music I genuinely like.

The album's 18 songs will be performed in order, and the musicians were assigned songs by lottery. To spoil a few surprises, I asked a handful of the performers what they had planned.

There aren't any real constraints on the performers as far as how they interpret the songs, but some folks are definitely taking more liberties than others. “I'm playing 'Shake Your Hips,' which is the kind of formless 'La Grange'/'Baby Did A Bad Thing'-style vamp from the first side of the record," Dietrich Gosser says. “Brian Grimm is going to join me on cello and Dan Kuemmel on percussion. I might get some help on the vocals, too, to help accentuate some of the falsetto caterwauling. They got it all over and done with real quick on the record. We'll probably drag our feet a bit more."

Annelies Howell, backed by Danny Hicks, Ben Wolf, and Nick Brown, will handle the album's cover of Robert Johnson's “Stop Breaking Down." “We'll be doing kind of a surf-rockabilly version of the song," Howell says. “We thought it would be cool to try to give it the flavor of Wanda Jackson."

Meghan Rose, who plays in bands including Little Red Wolf and Damsel Trash and also records as a solo artist, says she'll try to stick closer to the album when she handles arguably its finest and most intoxicating song, “Tumbling Dice." "I think one of the best parts of the song is the awesome backup singers, so I have Becky Lipsitz singing with me, and she might play trumpet as well," Rose says. "Then Nate Tredinnick is a great singer and he's going to play keys, so we should have a very full sound. I'd like to distort the guitars a bit more too just because I'm a sucker for fuzz wherever I can get it."


Anna Vogelzang drew “Rip This Joint" and “Sweet Virginia," which I love because the latter's chorus (“got to scrape the shit right off your shoes") seems like just a bit of a stretch beyond the gentle, searching tone of Vogelzang's own songs, and that kind of out-of-character reaching can make a cover more interesting. (Come to think of it, it's also a bit of a surprise to hear Vogelzang's fellow pensive spirit and occasional tourmate Gosser cover a goofy groover like “Shake Your Hips.") “We all drew songs out of a hat… though they are two of the songs I would have chosen," Vogelzang says. "Andrew Young (my bass player and husband) will be taking lead on 'Rip This Joint,' and we're keeping 'Sweet Virginia' very close to the album. Members of Corey Hart's band and my band are collaborating to make it a pretty full sound."

The show will begin with Nick Brown singing “Rocks Off," which he says is one of his all-time favorite Stones songs, and I can see it suiting the dry-witted drawl Brown employs on his solo material. "Kyle Motor and I will be on electric guitars, Josh Labbus of The Motorz and The God Damns will be on bass, Ben Wolf on drums, and Nate Tredinnick on saxophone," he says. “I'll be very curious to hear how some of these songs are treated. It's a pretty filthy, good-sloppy electric-guitar album."

For his part, Matt Earley will be playing “Torn And Frayed" and the album closer “Soul Survivor" with Kyle Jacobson, Tredinnick, Eric Levy and his brother Jim Earley, as well as backing up other performers on a few more songs. I guess there's no telling how the show will go down as a whole, but even if it turns out to mostly be a big drunken sing-along, that really isn't off the mark for this album.

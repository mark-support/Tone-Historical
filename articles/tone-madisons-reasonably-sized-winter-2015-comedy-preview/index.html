Tone Madison’s reasonably sized winter 2015 comedy preview

Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Mark Riechers
@markonfire /
Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
January 08, 2015 @ 5:58 pm

Clockwise from left: Tig Notaro plays the Capitol Theater on Feb. 28; Daniel Sloss plays the Comedy Club On State Feb. 5 through 7; Patton Oswalt plays the Orpheum Jan. 14; and Moshe Kasher plays the Comedy Club On State Jan. 22 through 24.

events

Tig Notaro, John Roy, Chris Hardwick, and more stand-up acts we’re looking forward to over the next few months.

As we keep saying, Madison has in recent years become a better and better city for catching great touring stand-up acts. In that respect, 2015 is off to a pretty good start. That said, after late 2014’s insane pile-up of multiple sold-out Dave Chappelle shows on top of a new comedy festival on top of additional shows by solid comics from W. Kamau Bell to John Oliver, we’re glad these next few months will be a bit more reasonably paced. Here are the acts we’re looking forward to—and we’re relieved that there aren’t too many scheduling conflicts among them!

JANUARY 9: Jerry Seinfeld at Overture Hall

Jerry Seinfeld tickets start out at $70+ at Overture Center, so you’re either already planning to see him, or you’ve made your decision that there are better ways to spend your money, such as Patton Oswalt’s Orpheum show a scant five days later and half the cost. Nothing against Seinfeld, obviously, but frankly he’s out of most people’s price ranges at this point, while Patton’s right there in the financial sweet spot by comparison.

JANUARY 14: Patton Oswalt at the Orpheum

If you’re reading this list, we don’t really know if Patton needs much of an introduction, since he more or less spearheaded the mainstream rise of “alt” comedy a decade ago with his “get in the van style” Comedians of Comedy tour, which also introduced the world at large to Maria Bamford and Zach Galifianakis. In the ensuing years, Oswalt's gotten married and had a daughter, deftly folding family man material into his act without losing the rough edges of his early years. It's hard to imagine anyone else twisting an act as literally domestic as house hunting into a story that bequeaths the phrase “twat mist” and “fuck-squatch” into the grand American lexicon (Oswalt's nothing if not a coiner of amazing, perversely pleasing little phrases), and yet...


JANUARY 15 through 17: John Roy at Comedy Club On State

John Roy is a great comedian. His spot on Conan a couple years back is still one of the great late-night comedy moments in recent history. More recently, he was a guest on one of the most over-the-top insane live episodes of Doug Benson’s Getting Doug With High to have ever been casted onto a pod. His energy on stage is sincere and infectious and his jokes and crowd-work are tight as a drum, easily sweeping you seamlesly from joke to joke.


JANUARY 22 through 24: Moshe Kasher at Comedy Club On State

Nature gave Moshe Kasher a twiggy and awkward exterior, but, a bit like John Waters, he carries his weirdness with shameless aplomb. Kasher’s 2013 album/special Live In Oakland (currently streaming on Netflix) is a decent introduction, though Kasher was a lot more loose and fun during his visit to the Comedy Club On State last year. Still, Live In Oakland closes with a wonderfully bizarre bit featuring Brent Weinbach, and is worth watching for that alone.


JANUARY 24: Lewis Black at Orpheum

Too many people simply reduce Lewis Black to a rageaholic caricature—something he admittedly played into by naming his 2013 stand-up special Old Yeller—which shorts the man’s craft and intellect to a tragic degree. Oh yeah, we love his curmudgeonly flip-outs, but they usually don’t feel like low-hanging fruit. Rather, the story at work in Black’s stand-up act is that so much about the world can turn a reasonable person into a spluttering pyre of bleakness. And OK, yeah, sometimes it can feel a bit played out, but there’s still a charmingly put-upon, deeply perceptive guy underneath it.


FEBRUARY 5 through 7: Daniel Sloss at Comedy Club On State

Scottish comedian Daniel Sloss is something of a brogue wunderkind, hitting the big time in his home country with his Jimmy-Carr-meets-Bo-Burnam-on-caffeine stylings before he was even out of his teens. We get the feeling that Sloss’s first real forays into American markets this year are setting the scene for a larger spread if his brand of awkwardly delivered comedy sticks. With any luck, we’ll be seeing more of him, but he’s got some dues to pay round these parts still.


FEBRUARY 6: Demetri Martin at the Barrymore

Comedy Bang Bang has made alternative absurdist comic performance art a nearly commonplace experience, but Demetri Martin has been doing it longer than anyone around. Prop comedy with a giant notepad became his signature closer ages ago, but he also integrates music into his act in ways that you won’t find in a conventional club show. No telling whether he’ll be transitioning to reading tweets on stage as part of a move to all-digital farce, but he seems to be well on his way to adapting his visual wordplay to tinier screens. Perhaps this Majestic show will feature some new SnapChat-based material?

FEBRUARY 28: Tig Notaro at Capitol Theater

Tig Notaro has become widely admired for handling a horrible situation with incredible humor and toughness—she recorded 2012’s stand-up album Live just after being diagnosed with breast cancer, and her show in Madison is a benefit for local cancer charity Gilda’s Club. (She recovered after a double mastectomy, and even went on to joke that her breasts conspired to kill her for making so many jokes about her own flat-chestedness.) So it bears noting that, well before that shitstorm hit, Notaro was crafting brilliantly odd, understated stand-up bits about getting stupid emails from her cousin and her longtime quest to give ’80s pop singer Taylor Dane the same compliment over and over again,as showcased on her excellent 2011 album Good One.


APRIL 11: Chris Hardwick at the Barrymore

As one of the more enterprising comedians (-turned film executive) out of the comedy podcast boom, host of possibly the first game show to actually understand the internet, and human incubation chamber for television recap material, one can sometimes forget that Chris Hardwick is, in fact, a standup comedian. Previous tours have seen him marry the success of his live Nerdist podcast shows with his act, affording him a brief set recycled over a few too many tour dates, but this upcoming tour promises fresh material that he’s been working over since recording a special in 2012, on the road between seemingly endless YouTube and television projects. Hardwick has a frenetic energy to his standup that can be a lot of fun if you’re into his increasingly specific trademark of nerdy-but-still-cool-promise humor–he’s probably caters more to existing fans than haters, which generally makes for a better show most of the time anyway. He excels at conversations, and his personable energy shines through in an act that often sucks fans and their own nerdy obsessions into his orbit in lieu of rigidly constructed chunks of material. Basically, you probably know if you’re going, so yeah.

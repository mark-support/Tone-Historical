Madison calendar, March 19 through 25

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
March 18, 2015 @ 11:47 am

Clockwise from left: Tycho plays the Majestic on March 23; Bone Thugs-N-Harmony plays the Majestic on March 21; Sharkmuffin plays Mickey's Tavern on March 25.

events

Bone Thugs-N-Harmony, Sharkmuffin, Cursive, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY MARCH 19

Cursive, Dylan Rand, See Through Dresses. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

Many writers have been reexamining and reaffirming the significance of Omaha band Cursive since the recent remastering and reissue of 2003's cello-laden and ultra-ambitious full-length (though vocalist Tim Kasher swears it's not a concept record) The Ugly Organ. There's good reason why Cursive never totally faded into the background like so many of their Saddle Creek peers—the uncomfortably raw lyrics spilling from Kasher's cracking voice, the angular cello and guitar interplay, and the detailed songwriting have helped ensure that the old stuff ages well and the new material stays progressive and interesting. We'd imagine that with the rerelease of The Ugly Organ so close behind, this would be a great opportunity to hear a wealth of those tunes live.

Bad Cinema: Road House. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Look, we fully support the Madison Public Library's Bad Cinema series and its efforts to showcase transcendently inept films, but ugh, Road House? Seriously? Sure, it's great to watch Patrick Swayze and his feathery frosted mullet get locked into bar fights, but nobody needs to go out of their way to see this film ever again. Pretty much anyone with cable can probably just flip their TVs on and find Road House in 10 eager clicks of the remote.

FRIDAY MARCH 20

Tyranny Is Tyranny, Knife The Symphony, The Shanks. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

Cincinnati's Phratry Records has spent 10 years releasing under-appreciated post-punk and hard rock records by bands from across the Midwest and beyond (including Madison's Tyranny Is Tyranny and its members' former project, The United Sons Of Toil). This bill of Phratry-affiliated bands showcases some of the label's diversity. Cincy's Knife The Symphony make lurching, misanthropic, yet disarmingly soulful songs, channeling the uneasy melds of melody and dissonance that made bands like Unwound and Drive Like Jehu so compelling. Toronto duo The Shanks share a bit of that austere post-punk DNA, but draw more on burly riffs and power pop-inspired choruses. Madison's own Tyranny Is Tyranny, whose second album is due out soon on Phratry (look for a song debut off that on Tone Madison next week), harness elements of post-rock, prog and metal in an increasingly complex but still viscerally powerful configurations.


Renee Gladman. Rainbow Bookstore, 8 p.m. (free)

Poet, novelist, essayist, and independent-press operator Renee Gladman visits here behind her new novel Ana Patova Crosses A Bridge, the third in a series set in Gladman's fictional city of Ravicka. Gladman's visit here is part of the Felix Reading Series, a local effort to bring more new and experimental literary voices to Madison.

Cinematheque: Touki Bouki. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m.

This weekend, UW-Cinematheque offers a brief taste of Il Cinema Ritrovato, an annual film festival in Italy that focuses on hard-to-find and recently rediscovered titles. The festival's organizers have collaborated with Cinematheque programmers to bring four recent Il Cinema Ritrovato selections to Madison. First up, on Friday, is Senegalese director Djibril Diop Mambéty's 1973 film Touki Bouki, which follows a young couple desperate to leave Dakar for Paris. The Il Cinema Ritrovato program continues Saturday afternoon and evening with three more selections: The 1913 Italian silent film Love Everlasting, the Vietnam-era satire Brand X, and Roberto Rossellini's revered 1954 drama Journey To Italy.

SATURDAY MARCH 21

WORT Record Riot. High Noon Saloon, 12 to 4 p.m.

Because we've had the pleasure of wandering through WORT FM's enviable vinyl library, we're reasonably certain the station can put together a pretty solid record sale. Sure, we've also picked through WORT's rather sad cast-off promo-CD sales at street fairs over the years, but this event should be a more loving and geeky affair, with offerings from the station itself and other collectors from around town.

Bone-Thugs-N-Harmony, David Yang, Sincere Life. Majestic, 9 & 11:30 p.m. (early show sold out)

When Cleveland-based rap-crooners Bone Thugs-N-Harmony unleashed 1995's album E. 1999 Eternal, it shone through the congested sea of break-laden East Coast hip-hop and sample-happy, West Coast G-Funk that had been plugging up the rap-scape for a few years prior. Each of Bone's five members—Layzie, Krayzie, Bizzy, Wish, and Flesh—offered their own distinguished melodic flows and rapidfire deliveries over producer DJ U-Neek's spacious beats and lushly heady sonic backdrops. Every haunting gangsta narrative (I.E. “Down '71 (The Getaway)" and “Mo Murda") is balanced with blissfully cruising jams like the immaculately infectious “1st Of Tha Month" or afterlife-pondering megahit “Tha Crossroads." We're stoked to see that Bizzy Bone has pulled himself together long enough for him to rejoin the original line-up and cruise through Madison to perform E. 1999 Eternal in its entirety. This show is also notable because this is supposedly Bone's last tour (and their alleged final album E. 1999 Legends is on the way). The show initially sold out, so a second show has been added at 11:30 p.m.

Dead Rider, Perverse Engineer, William Z. Villain. Frequency, 9 p.m. (free)

Chicago outfit Dead Rider (formed by US Maple guitarist/vocalist Todd Rittman) has slithered through a lot of warped post-punk and electronically tinged noise over the course of three albums, the latest being last year's Chills On Glass. The best moments come when Rittman's vocals aren't at the forefront and sink into wriggling, simmering ensemble exchanges that recall US Maple's sinister post-punk brilliance but also the open-ended chaos of fellow Chicago experimentalists Ono. Standout tracks like “Weird Summer" offer plenty of jagged weirdness on the surface, but have more subtle, insidious progressions at work underneath.


Love Everlasting + Brand X + Journey To Italy. Vilas Hall, 2 p.m. (free)

SUNDAY MARCH 22

The Beguiled. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

Don Siegel's 1971 film The Beguiled stars Clint Eastwood as a wounded Union soldier waiting out the end of the Civil War in a women's seminary in Louisiana, a situation that leads to twisted romances and unsettling character studies. It screens here as part of a look back at the adventurous films Universal Studios released in 1971.

MONDAY MARCH 23

The Great Duck War, Trophy Dad, Wood Chickens. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

Madison trio The Great Duck War last month put out a self-titled three-song EP that cuts a fast, reverb-smeared course through surf-rock, psych, and snarled indie-rock. It's shambling and fun in the way that a lot of young Madison rock bands are these days, but “Chicago Blastroid" and “Dial Tones" work their way into twisty, dissonant passages that suggest an ambitious, playful approach.


Tycho, Beacon. Majestic, 8:30 p.m.

Much of the massively polished, lush sound of San Francisco electronic artist Tycho's 2014 album Awake seems to be the result of masterminding multi-instrumentalist Scott Hansen's ability to filter down the most digestible elements of post-rock and synth-pop into something incredibly contemporary and instantly ready to come pumping out of your nearest Noodles & Company's favorite Muzak station. That's not to say that instrumental sunrise-pop tune “Montana" or arpeggio-laden Italo disco mutation “See" aren't pleasingly melodic cruisers or that it bothers us that much of the dynamic “Dye" sounds like it would fit perfectly behind the opening credits in Smallville. What we're really trying to say that if you're looking for innovative electronic tunes with tons of fresh ideas, maybe look elsewhere? But if you're looking for something more predictable and less challenging sonically, but insanely tight and lit with gorgeous projections, this one might be worth checking out.


TUESDAY MARCH 24

Danielle Allen. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m. (free)

Political philosopher and Princeton social-science professor Danielle Allen gives a Distinguished Lecture Series talk here behind her 2014 book Our Declaration. Inspired by her experience studying the Declaration Of Independence with an adult night-school class Allen once taught, the book offers a radical interpretation of the Declaration's concepts of equality.

WEDNESDAY MARCH 25

Sharkmuffin, Fire Retarded, The Minotaurs. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

Packed with pounding rhythms, buzzing, surfy riffs, and the rubbery crooning of vocalist-guitarist Tarra Thiessen, NYC-based grunge-punk outfit Sharkmuffin's 2013 EP 1097 clocked in under eight shapeshifting minutes and left us wanting much, much more. This is why we're hoping that the trio's upcoming Madison stop graces us with new tunes from upcoming LP Chartreuse (which features ex-Hole member Patty Schemel on drums), which is supposed to drop this summer. Madison garage-punk outfits Fire Retarded and The Minotaurs round out this bill.


Nerd Nite. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m. (free)

This month's Nerd Nite talks—informal presentations usually given by grad students and other members of the UW-Madison community—will cover the science behind people's attitudes about language, the history of the concept of hours and minutes, and a colorful Athenian intellectual whom the presenter describes as “Classical Greece's most legendary badass and possibly its biggest douchebag."

It Happened Here. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

Lisa Jackson's 2014 documentary It Happened Here profiles five victims of campus sexual assault, an issue that's finally getting some of the attention it deserves, but hasn't quite gotten through the way it needs to, as a certain jaw-droppingly insensitive George F. Will column made perfectly clear last summer.

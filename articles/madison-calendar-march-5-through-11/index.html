Madison calendar, March 5 through 11

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
March 04, 2015 @ 3:42 pm

Clockwise from top left: Jauja screens Saturday at Vilas Hall; Talib Kweli plays Monday at the Barrymore; Duncan Trussell visits the Majestic on Friday.

events

Talib Kweli, Lust For Youth, Duncan Trussell, and more of the best stuff coming to Madison this week.

THURSDAY MARCH 5

Mishima: A Life In Four Chapters. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Yukio Mishima is one of the most fascinating characters in recent Japanese history. The writer, filmmaker and actor was a three-time nominee for the Nobel Prize in literature, and in the late '60s he also formed a private militia that was sworn to defend the Emperor using the ancient samurai code of Bushido. Paul Schrader's film adaptation of Mishima's life story pulls off the herculean feat of presenting Japan's renaissance man in profoundly artful detail and with a Philip Glass score, playing Mishima's technicolor literary creations against the black and white biographical details of his life, all culminating in a tragic and surreal finale that we won't spoil here.


Force Majeure. Union South Marquee, 9:30 p.m. (free)

Swedish director Ruben Östlund's 2014 film Force Majeure turns a family's ski vacation into a cruelly comic examination of masculinity and fatherhood. When a mild-mannered young father (Johannes Kuhnke) and his family have a close call with an avalanche on their ski-resort balcony, his cowardly reaction sets off a slow, quiet, but fascinating erosion of his relationship with his wife and two young kids.

FRIDAY MARCH 6

Line Breaks. Multiple venues, through March 17, see link for full schedule.

Line Breaks is an annual festival in which students, faculty and visiting artists connected to UW-Madison's First Wave program celebrate the convergence of hip hop, dance, and theater. This year's performances of group and solo pieces don't kick off until March 11, but March 6 marks the opening of an art show in the UW-Madison Education Building that features paintings, prints, drawings, and short film works from First Wave scholars including Jill Fukumoto and Hiwot Adilow.

Lust For Youth, Samantha Glass. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 9 p.m. (free)

Swedish producer Hannes Norrvide's three releases for Sacred Bones under the name Lust For Youth have traveled from the starkly abrasive (2011's Growing Seeds) to the polished (last year's International), steadily advancing the project toward an endearingly warped electro-pop vision. Sure, on an International track like “Armida" you could be forgiven for initially lumping in LFY with any number of other synth-pop revival youngsters, but there's still plenty of strange detail bristling beneath the project's newly shiny surface. Madison's own lysergic pop-journeyer and synthesist Beau Devereaux opens the show under his Samantha Glass moniker. Devereaux's live sets almost never repeat themselves in set-up or structure and he's been hard at work on the follow up to last year's moody and challenging Surface Water Perception, so we can safely expect a taste of the next LP, which should be reason enough in itself to scope out this free show.


Duncan Trussell. Majestic, 8 p.m.

Los Angeles-based comedian, philosopher, and serial podcaster Duncan Trussell initially rose to underground prominence while chiming in on Joe Rogan's The Joe Rogan Experience. Eventually, Trussell's meandering, surrealist flow outgrew his supporting roles and even his former co-hosting gig with Natasha Leggero on The Lavender Hour and evolved into the gloriously sprawling, drug-fueled mutant ramblings of The Duncan Trussell Family Hour podcast, which Trussell will be recording on-site at the Majestic with On The Warrior's Path author Daniele Bolleli.

Night Light: Arts & Crafts. Central Library, 8 p.m. (free)

For this month's Night Light event, the Central Library is inviting adult patrons to participate in a variety of screenprinting and other hands-on art activities while checking out new art exhibitions from Jim Escalante, Lewis Koch, Shannon Troxler, Toby Kaufmann-Buehler, and Aaron Laux.

Ashes And Diamonds + Innocent Sorcerers. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

UW Cinematheque is hosting a new collection of Polish films curated by Martin Scorsese and distributed in newly restored digital editions, kicking off here with the 1958 thriller Ashes And Diamonds (7 p.m.) and the 1960 romantic drama Innocent Sorcerers (8:45 p.m.).

Hugh Masekela, Vusi Mahlasela. Union Theater, 8 p.m.

Two of South Africa's most internationally successful artists, trumpeter Hugh Masekela and guitarist/singer Vusi Mahlasela, are pairing up on a joint tour to mark the 20th anniversary of South Africa's 1994 elections, the first in which people of all races were allowed to vote, and which resulted in Nelson Mandela becoming the country's president.


SATURDAY MARCH 7

Obnox, The Hussy, Dumb Beach, Proud Parents. Crystal Corner Bar, 10 p.m.

Obnox, the project of Cleveland musician Lamont Thomas, plays here behind the new album Boogalou Reed, which finds Thomas' gritty, smart-assed, spirited punk/R&B/funk/hip hop hybridizations evolving at as crazy-fast a pace as ever. Learn more about Obnox in this week's interview with Thomas.


Duck Soup Cinema: Chaplin Shorts. Capitol Theater, 2 and 7 p.m.

Overture's Duck Soup Cinema program aims to revisit the glory days of silent film with live musical accompaniment on the Capitol Theater's organ and vaudeville-style acts sharing the bill with the actual films. This time, they've got an impressive little program of Charlie Chaplin's early short-film works: Kid's Auto Race, The Immigrant, Work, and Behind The Screen, in addition to a vintage newsreel that features Chaplin starring in an advertisement.

Jauja + Two Shots Fired. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

The UW Cinematheque and Latin American, Caribbean, and Iberian Studies programs have teamed up for the New Argentine Cinema series, which kicks off with a double feature of two 2014 Argentinian films that are still wandering the festival circuit. Lisandro Alonso's Jauja is set in 1880's Denmark and stars Viggo Mortensen as Gunnar Dinesen, a Danish general who seeks out his daughter when she disappears with a soldier. Also screening is Martin Rejtman's Two Shots Fired, a bleak and bizarre comedy in which a young man known finds a gun around his house, shoots himself, and survives.

SUNDAY MARCH 8

The Last Movie. Chazen Museum of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

Dennis Hopper directed and starred in 1971's The Last Movie as a stuntman who gets mixed up in a series of adventures in Peru after shooting a Western there. The film's experimental, fragmented nature makes it a bold anomaly for some and unwatchable for others; here, it kicks off UW Cinematheque's look at many risk-taking productions Universal Pictures released in 1971.

MONDAY MARCH 9

Talib Kweli, Immortal Technique, Niko Is, CF, Hasan Salaam. Barrymore, 8 p.m.

It's hard to believe it's been 13 years since Talib Kweli released Quality, arguably his masterwork, and that he's just a bit more low-profile these days than what his work in Black Star and as a solo artist deserves. Then again, in recent years he's embraced a bit more of an underground role, wielding his distinctively prickly flow on some decent solo albums (especially 2011's Gutter Rainbows). He returns to Madison on a joint headlining tour with fellow NYC rapper Immortal Technique


Ninja III: The Domination. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

The UW Cinematheque's trashy Cannon Fodder series, the dimwitted sibling to their higher brow Cannon Canon series, continues on this at Union South with Ninja III: The Domination (not to be confused, understandably, with American Ninja 3: Blood Hunt). The Cinematheque write-up promises that this “tale of Ninja possession that starts with a massacre on a golf course and only gets more gonzo from there" and guarantees “female aerobic instructor ninjas, a ninja hot tub attack, a ninja exorcism, and V8 as a sexual aid". No one tell Ezra Klein that this overlaps with his speaking engagement or else he might cancel so as to see it for himself.

Ezra Klein. Wisconsin Union Theater, 7 p.m. (free)

It's not yet clear whether Vox.com founder (and former Washington Post political columnist) Ezra Klein can help lead us into a new era of “[complicated story/issue], explained" journalism. But the only people worth listening to in media these days are those actively taking chances and throwing ideas at the wall, so we're interested to see what Klein has to say about the future of journalism at this Distinguished Lecture Series event.

TUESDAY MARCH 10

Guelwaar. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

In 1992's Guelwaar, legendary Senegalese filmmaker Ousmane Sembéne shares the satirical story of deceased Christian activist Pierre Henri “Guelwaar" Thioune, who is mistakenly buried in a Muslim cemetery in the West African Republic of Senegal.

WEDNESDAY MARCH 11

War Of The Worlds + The Paper. Vilas Hall (free)

Saving Private Ryan was released in 1998 and it's arguable that Steven Spielberg has made anything that lasting since. Between then and now are a whole lot of movies you might have skipped (The Terminal, War Horse), watched primarily because it was an early Oscar contender (Lincoln), accidentally forgot about (A.I. Artificial Intelligence), or deliberately forgot about (Indiana Jones And The Kingdom of the Crystal Skull). Right smack in the middle of that list of films is the 2005 big-budget summer tent-pole adaptation of the sci-fi classic War Of The Worlds, which re-teamed Spielberg with Tom Cruise. We're not chomping at the bit to revisit the film itself (plus it's screening here at 3 p.m. on a Wednesday afternoon), but it's absolutely worth attending for the post-screening discussion with co-screenwriter David Koepp (Pewaukee's own!). War Of The Worlds screens as the first part of a Koepp double feature, followed at 7 p.m. by Ron Howard's The Paper, which covers 24 hours in the life of a New York tabloid newspaper and stars Michael Keaton (towards the beginning of the mid-90s period when he was cinematically in the wind), Robert Duvall, Glenn Close, and Marisa Tomei.

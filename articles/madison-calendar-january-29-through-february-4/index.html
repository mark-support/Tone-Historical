Madison calendar, January 29 through February 4

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
January 28, 2015 @ 7:55 am

Clockwise from left: King L plays Jan. 31 at The Sett; Breakin' screens Feb. 2 at The Marquee; Yonatan Gat plays Feb. 3 at The Frequency.

events

King L, Sturgill Simpson, Yonatan Gat, and more of the best stuff happening in Madison this week.

THURSDAY JANUARY 29

Static Shock, Yung Pook G, Slaughter da joker, Bloodline, Money Mav, Big Hitz Ent, Jay B Coolin. Frequency, 9 p.m.

What with local hip-hop bills popping up lately in spots including The Wisco and Mr. Roberts, maybe we're seeing a good thaw in the bullshit cycle that local hip-hop is subjected to in Madison. Madison's Urban Community Arts Network (UCAN) organized this bill of primarily young Midwestern hip-hop artists, including Freeport, Illinois rapper Yung Pook G and Madison MC Jay B Coolin.

Forward. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

The 2011 protests surrounding Governor Scott Walker's blow to public-employee unions have inspired or factored into a glut of documentaries, some quite good and some less so. We haven't hesitated to complain about the latter, but our friends at Madison film blog LakeFrontRow, who are presenting this screening, consider Dusan Harminc and Matt Mullins' Forward among the better and more reflective ones. Mullins will be on hand for a post-screening Q&A.

Blade Runner: Director's Cut. Union South Marquee, 9:30 p.m.; also Jan. 30, 8:45 p.m. and Jan. 31, 6 p.m. (free)

Did you know that there have been at least seven different versions of Ridley Scott's Blade Runner that have been released? Seriously, there's a five-disc box set that collects em all, if you want to really dig in and spend a whole day comparing and contrasting them. The version that's getting shown across the weekend at Union South's Marquee Theater is the so-called “Directors Cut" one where the horrible narrations of the theatrical version have been stripped out, and a sick-as-hell dream sequence with a unicorn has been added. This right here is in the Science Fiction Film pantheon along with Alien, Terminator, Aliens, and Terminator 2: Judgement Day (among others). You either already know this movie inside and out, or you owe it to yourself to wrap your head around the godfather of Philip K. Dick adaptations.

FRIDAY JANUARY 30

The Go-Go Boys: The Inside Story Of Cannon Films. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

What does John Cassavetes' Love Streams have to do with Death Wish 3? What does Barfly have to do with Ninja III: The Domination? What does Jean-Luc Godard's King Lear have to do with Breakin'? All of them, from the highest highs to the gloriously bonkers lowest of the schlocky lows on the cultural spectrum, were distributed by the onetime low-budget powerhouse Cannon Films, and all will be screening as part of UW CInematheque's season-long tribute to the studio. This winter, Cannon actually gets two series of programming from Cinematheque. The high-minded and likely loss-leading-for-them “Cannon Canon" series launches this Friday at 4070 Vilas Hall with the one-two punch of the brand new documentary The Go Go Boys: The Inside Story Of Cannon Films, followed immediately by the 1985 prison-escape thriller Runaway Train, which netted both Jon Voight and Eric Roberts Oscar nominations. On Monday the comparatively craptastic yet coffer-filling “Cannon Fodder" series kicks off with 1984's dance-sploitation classic Breakin' at Union South's Marquee Theater.

Runaway Train. Vilas Hall, 8:45 p.m. (free)

Saturday Looks Good To Me. Memorial Union Rathskeller, 9 p.m. (free)

Since 2000, Michigan's Fred Thomas has channeled his wondrous pop-songwriting instincts through a rotating cast of singers and other collaborators, and a lot of lo-fi but charming production tricks, under the name Saturday Looks Good To Me. Through all those years of digging through '60s pop and R&B tropes, SLGTM has had remarkably consistent and emotionally resonant results. The 2003 album All Your Summer Songs is the unbeatable SLGTM classic in our humble opinion, but the most recent, 2013's One Kiss Ends It All, ain't bad either. Another part of the fun is how the band mutates live, as captured on this recording the band posted from a November 2014 show.


New Villains, Space Raft, Arkoffs. Mickey's, 10:30 p.m. (free)

Wisconsin's contribution to the garage-rock/power-pop scene is still healthy and ever mutating, as this lineup attests. Milwaukee's Space Raft put out an ambitious and justly well-received self-titled album last year, channeling exuberant hooks through panoramic, psych-brushed arrangements on highlights like “Venus In Transit" and “We Are Not Alone." Madison's New Villains, led by former Midwest Beat member Logan Kayne, haven't put out any recordings yet, but have a solid set of scrappy, sharply melodic power-pop tunes. Rounding out the bill are The Arkoffs, a long-running crew of Madison miscreants who plow deep into the dusty back pages of garage-rock.


Chicago Afrobeat Project. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. (free)

While the Chicago Afrobeat Project's filtration of Fela Kuti's mighty influence into something much more easily digestible and jam-band-circuit-friendly may be a massive turn-off to world-music diehards, it's tough to deny the long-standing poly-rhythmists' entrancing grooves and airtight musicianship. C.A.P. isn't terribly prolific—the band's last record was 2013's playfully titled Nyash Up, which featured mostly covers and included two bizarro mash-ups between Fela Kuti and both Radiohead and Fugazi, and that was their first release since 2009. However, the band have filled the downtime by contributing slanted, funky backdrops to collaborations with powerful vocalists like JC Brooks and Tony Allen and touring. Look, we're not saying that this is going to be your most "authentic" possible Afrobeat experience, but hell, who doesn't want to hear entrancing organ weaving in and out of modulating guitar fuckery over off-kilter rhythms once ikn a while?

SATURDAY JANUARY 31

The Magnificent Ambersons. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

The great Joseph Cotten stars in Orson Welles' 1942 classic The Magnificent Ambersons (based on the Booth Tarkington's 1918 novel of the same name), which offers a darkly heart-wrenching, multi-generational look into the lives of the Ambersons, the richest family in Indianapolis. Let's be real here, this could well be your only local opportunity ever to catch a 35mm print of this film. This one's a serious must-see and it's part of the UW Cinematheque's extensive Welles centennial program.

The Stranger. Vilas Hall, 8:30 p.m. (free)

In his 1946 thriller The Stranger, Orson Welles plays a Nazi war criminal hiding out in Connecticut under an assumed identity and official film-noir stamp of hard-boiled-ness Edward G. Robinson as an investigator out to bring him to justice.

King L. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. (free)

Hailing from the same dark corners as Chicago "drill" scene prototypes Chief Keef and Lil' Durk, King L (also known as King Louie) tightropes the same line between painting bleak pictures of the violent street culture of his neighborhood and auto-tuned pop hooks on his most recent mixtape, 2014'sam Tony. But King L's approach feels significantly more cerebral and flexible than the intriguingly greenish delivery of his contemporaries. In fact, King L's refinement and experience (he's actually about eight years older than Keef) earned him a killer guest verse on Kanye West's "Send It Up" (on Yeezus) and also an invitation to camp out with West in Paris and help write lyrics for him on tunes like "New Slaves," which featured a few L-penned couplets. While we're not totally sold The Sett being the most appropriate venue for this, at least someone's putting up the loot to bring such a killer underground rap titan to Madison.


Prof, St. Paul Slim, Charles Grant, Chris LaBella. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m.

The self-consciously sophomoric prankster you didn't know Midwestern rap was asking for, Twin Cities MC Prof is definitely a proposition that depends on how much trashy overstimulation you can embrace in your hip-hop. His antics have included posing for a lot of photos with scantily clad pregnant women and starting a Twitter fight with John Stamos, but then again he can also hold his own on a track with a seeming opposite like Brother Ali.


SUNDAY FEBRUARY 1

Big Deal On Madonna Street. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m.

UW Cinematheque kicks off its tribute to Italian filmmaker Mario Monicelli's comedies with his 1958 crime caper Big Deal On Madonna Street.

MONDAY FEBRUARY 2

Skizzwhores, Damsel Trash, Wood Chickens. Frequency, 8:30 p.m.

On account of it's Groundhog Day, Ashle Quinn of Madison punk band Skizzwhores is throwing herself this Bill Murray-themed punk party. In addition to Skizzwhores' own solidly scuzzy take on punk, the bill boasts the over-the-top theatrics of Madison duo Damsel Trash and the psych-frazzled cowpunk of fellow locals Wood Chickens.


Breakin'. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

TUESDAY FEBRUARY 3

Aesop Rock, Rob Sonic, DJ Abilities, Homeboy Sandman, WebsterX. Barrymore, 8:30 p.m.

Last year Aesop Rock and Rob Sonic released Bestiary, their second album under the name Hail Mary Mallon, and it's an energizing reminder of Aesop's intricate, peanut-butter-mouthed wordplay and what a great foil he has in Rob Sonic's slightly more laid-back, way less appreciated, but still formidable flows. Rob Sonic, also a producer with a heavy and punchy style, released his third solo album, Alice In Thunderdome, in 2014, and while Aesop Rock's solo work has remained solid, HMM is definitely the most fun things he's ever done. Here the pair will play with DJ Abilities. If you're going it's also essential to show up for NYC underground standout Homeboy Sandman's opening set—read more about that in our recent interview.


Yonatan Gat, Lovers Spit, Fire Retarded. Frequency, 8:30 p.m.

Away from the hirsute proto-punk gymnastics of his band Monotonix, guitarist Yonatan Gat has been pursuing a sort of sweaty rock 'n' roll exotica. The instrumentals on Gat's 2014 EP Iberian Passage sprawl across references to Caribbean melody, Middle Eastern scales, and a bit of Link Wray and Dick Dale for good measure. He plays here ahead of the full-length Director, due out in March.


WEDNESDAY FEBRUARY 4

Madison's Funniest Comic Competition Preliminary Round. Comedy Club On State, 9 p.m.

Usually a local stand-up comedy competition might not sound like a very enticing prospect, but since 2010 the Comedy Club On State has built its annual Madison's Funniest Comic into something of a glorious, five-week clusterfuck of everything that's great and awful about local stand-up. It attracts plenty of Madison's open-mic regulars, from the broadest to the weirdest, but also the occasional brilliant newcomer—oh, and a fair number of shaky but well-meaning newcomers, and crazy or excruciatingly unfunny people, too. During the preliminary rounds—this year, there are two—the judges will weed out literally a few dozen entrants, and the audience will plow through some pretty ferocious ups and downs. But the competition's organizers have figured out how to keep the momentum going so that all but the most painful failures feel like part of the fun.

Sturgill Simpson, Anderson East. Majestic, 8:30 p.m. (sold out)

Sturgill Simpson's second solo album, 2014's Metamodern Sounds In Country Music, comes off as at once very clever and completely guileless—which makes for very good country songs that, for all their ambition, click immediately and aren't too obviously aligned with any one period or subgenre.

Rewind the Jewels: Six Killer Mike and El-P deep cuts

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Andrew Winistorfer
@thestorfer /
November 20, 2014 @ 12:00 am

listen

Ahead of Run The Jewels’ Friday show in Madison, we pick some favorites from the members’ back catalogues.

(Photo by Vic Michael.)

We’re glad to live in a world where Atlanta rapper Killer Mike and NYC rapper/producer El-P found each other. This Friday, they’ll play a sold-out show at the Majestic behind their second album under the name Run The Jewels. While the so far short but explosive existence of RTJ is worth celebrating in its own right (as is Killer Mike’s 2012 solo album R.A.P. Music, which El-P produced), new fans should also use it as a point of entry to dig back into the great music Killer Mike and El-P have been making for years and with far less recognition. To suggest a few starting points, we picked a few of our favorite moments from both artists’ solo careers.

Lil Flip, “We Ain’t Playin’,” ft. Killer Mike, Baby D and Pastor Troy (From U Gotta Feel Me, 2004)


Andrew: Unlike El-P, who arrived seemingly fully formed, Killer Mike took a while before he settled on the indignant, weaned-on-AmeriKKKa’s Most Wanted, polemical MC he is today. His first recorded music was a guest verse on Outkast’s classic Stankonia—that’s him trading verses with Big Boi on “Snappin’ and Trappin’”—and apart from his 2003 debut, Monster, he spent a lot of the years between 2000 and 2006 (when he released his first I Pledge Allegiance To The Grind album) coming through with showstopping guest verses. His turn on a lesser known Lil Flip single, “We Ain’t Playin’” imagines a world where Mike ends up as the muse for early Trap producers instead of waiting for his bromance with El-P. His hefty voice and acrobatic flow smash through the track like a two-ton elephant.

Killer Mike “My Chrome” (From Got Purp? Vol. 2, 2005)


Andrew: When Outkast went on hiatus, so did Big Boi’s record label, which meant that Killer Mike was sent adrift from the comfortable environs of the Dungeon Family. But not before Big Boi’s label, the Purple Ribbon All Stars, released the compilation albums Got That Purp and Got Purp? 2. Mike makes a bunch of great appearances on Purp 2—his bridge on “Kryptonite” works in references to West Atlanta and Pimp C in a few bars—but his showcase is “My Chrome,” a song that sounds like a Speakerboxxx deep cut. The song also makes the case for a Killer Mike-Big Boi joint album, should El and Mike stop hanging out together long enough to make that happen.

Killer Mike, “Blind Justice” (From BangX3, 2011)


Andrew: Killer Mike really hit his stride as a solo artist with his I Pledge Allegiance To The Grind mixtape series, the best of which, Pl3dge, might go down as the lost classic of Mike’s oeuvre. In the run-up to the release of that album, Mike released a free mixtape, BangX3, that pointed towards the hard-earned confidence of Pl3dge. “Blind Justice” is the tape’s denouement, a soulful, classic-sounding track that has Mike effortlessly weaving in and out of the beat’s lush environment.

El-P, “Jump Fence, Run, Live” (From Weareallgoingtoburninhellmegamixxx3, 2010)


Scott: I’d recommend the instrumental album Weareallgoingtoburninhellmegamixxx3 for anyone who wants to hear El-P’s production style minus the sometimes overbearing persona he (and, come to think of it, a lot of the other artists he’s produced for, like Aesop Rock and Cannibal Ox) tends to project as a rapper. That doesn’t make it less distinctively El-P, just looser and more melodic. It’s understandable to peg El-P as being rather heavy-handed, but the descending synth line that kicks in at about 1:18 on “Jump Fence, Run, Live” feels effervescent, nimble, and almost triumphant. This track kind of changed how I hear El-P—when I listen to him now, I keep my ears open for melody.

El-P, “For My Upstairs Neighbor (Mums The Word)” (from Cancer4Cure, 2012)


Scott: Run The Jewels, especially on RTJ2, touches a lot on power, violence, police brutality, and surveillance. But it’s still not quite as thoroughly dark as either Mike or El-P get on their own. In particular, it doesn’t much bother with the tunnel-visioned, song-length narratives that mark El-P’s solo albums. In the grim domestic-violence tale “For My Upstairs Neighbor,” he sets up a world that encourages isolation and apathy (”on my New York shit… don’t talk to anyone, look forward”), but gives us a glimpse of depth and empathy, even if it’s couched in something of a revenge fantasy.

El-P, “Tuned Mass Damper” (from Fantastic Damage, 2002)


Scott: Going back to El-P’s late-’90s work with Company Flow and his first solo album, Fantastic Damage, I’m actually surprised by how little it resonates with me now. I still enjoy it, but I've maybe outgrown it, and El-P has definitely outgrown it. There’s plenty to admire still: Fantastic Damage showcases some of the most nimbly disjointed verses El-P has ever recorded, and its production is daring and gritty, proof that he could make compelling beats well before arriving at the the synth-driven approach he’s so commonly associated with. It’s just that 2007’s I’ll Sleep When You’re Dead and 2012’s Cancer4Cure feel like such leaps ahead—they’ve got so much more headroom, and elegant, suspenseful songs that feel like so much more than well-paired verses and beats. “Tuned Mass Damper” is a happy medium: Unlike a lot of Fantastic Damage tracks, it’s not too claustrophobic for its own good, but it still jams in plenty of El-P’s surly surrealism: “Alarmists and opinions get taxed/ Motherfucker, did I sound abstract? / I hope it sounded more confusing than that / ‘Cause my clarity was found under the arm of an economy-sized mouse trap.”

Layering textures: Whilden Hughes of Double Ewes

Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna
July 01, 2014 @ 1:41 pm

listen

The Madison/Janesville band has released an album of gorgeous electro-Americana tunes.

When Double Ewes played at the Rock County Folk Symposium in Janesville last year, it seemed like the Janesville/Madison band was playing much more for the surrounding trees and the Rock River, which ran only yards away from the stage, than for the audience. This shouldn't be surprising, as the show was basically in vocalist-guitarist Whilden Hughes' backyard, which is in all likelihood pretty close to where most of the lush, midnight freeway, electro-Americana tunes on Double Ewes' new self-titled album started roughly two years ago. After a lot of hard work in the studio, Hughes and bandmates Jeremy Nealis and Max Jewer have created a contemporary folk record far from the confines of the offensively inoffensive preciousness of indie-folk or the hyper-indulgent reaches of the mutated electro-folk of Animal Collective. The album's 12 beautifully-paced cuts are centered on with Hughes' tastefully reverb-soaked crooning, meandering guitar-work, and wandering keys, which are then deeply processed and laced with cascading synth washes, sculpted samples and electronic rhythms, with an overall effect that's remarkably cohesive.

Ahead of the band's July 12 show at the Wisco and return to the Rock County Folk Symposium on Aug. 23, Hughes talked with about his melancholy lyrical themes, working towards something he can't anticipate, and how sometimes the tunes that come together the quickest end up being favorites.

Tone Madison: The album has this really cohesive midnight-highway, psychedelic feel. How did everything come together?

Whilden Hughes: We just experimented, and at some point we found a formula that worked for us. This is our first album, and we just wanted to have something we were proud to put out. Max recorded and produced it himself. I owe a lot to him for really finding the songs. Anders Peterson of the Ghost Sounds label mastered it.

Tone Madison: How long did you spend recording the album and what was the recording process like?

Whilden Hughes: From the very first track to the final mastered version was about two years. We started, I think, with "A.I." and a couple other tracks we’ve had for a couple years. It was a challenge on settling on the definitive version of each song because no two takes were ever identical. Then about a month before its release we sort of accidentally came up with tracks like "Phosphenes," and "Leave The Dance (With The Girl You Brought)." We didn’t know if we should add them or put them on the next album, but we said fuck it. They are easily my two favorites. Gearing up for the next release, everything about the process seems more lucid. I'm in a good place creatively, having learned a lot from this record. I think it will translate into some really solid work.

Tone Madison: How would you describe your headspace in terms of lyrical content this time around? The vocal work seems very detailed, but also pretty deep in the mix, which I assume is deliberate.

Whilden Hughes: Kinda melancholy. I’m not sure why. Reflecting back I’m seeing that i leaned towards darker themes. self-doubt, isolation, even drought. I think it's because the music, for the most part, is so upbeat. I had to juxtapose it somehow. "Phosphenes" is a good example. The song centers around the sense of sight. Its a song about eyes, basically, and their interaction with light. The first half of the song tells true tales about events my sister has witnessed. finding my grandfather's last breaths, seeing my dad's car wrapped around a telephone pole outside of the farm. The second half of the song is more abstract and asks you to close eyes, face the sun, and reflect on what you see. It's a moment of introspection for me.

Tone Madison: The album uses a lot of samples. What is the songwriting process like? Do you build the sounds first and then write with/around them?

Whilden Hughes: Everything is rhythm-oriented. I prefer to craft the vessel before I set sail on my lyrical voyage. Generally the drum track, or rhythm guitar are the first pieces of a new song for me. The lyrics are the final touch. Music to me is about layering textures. Working towards something you can’t fully anticipate, finding myself in a new situation, and providing a moment of self-reflection to those that listen.

Tone Madison: There seems to be a ton going on in these recordings, with lots of meandering keys, guitar lines, and drones. What are some of the challenges of pulling these tunes off in the live setting?

Whilden Hughes: A lot of the core pieces of this album were recorded in a live studio setting, so a lot of the transitions and fills you hear on the record are exactly how we play them live. The rest we are still learning. Max and Jeremy are both really talented at arranging and producing, so they handle a lot of the load. Everything is MIDI-clocked and interconnected, so not only are we communicating with each other on stage, but so are our instruments. Every sound is either played, looped, or triggered in real time. It can get a little hectic singing and playing guitar, while controlling the drum sequencer and triggering samples, but I’m just kind of used to it. I actually think it’s funny and provides comic relief, but I'm one set of knee cymbals away from overdoing it.

Tone Madison: What are a few straight-up Americana and straight up electronic records that you were thinking about during the creative process of this record?

Whilden Hughes: Waylon Jennings' Dark Side Of Fame. Mason Proffit, Wanted. Pretty much any '60s-'70s outlaw country. Bob Dyan’s Self Portrait. The Cave Singers' Naomi album. Dustin Wong is a big influence on my guitar approach, as is Devendra Banhart.

Conjuror's Ian Adcock on making synth-driven drone

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
January 06, 2015 @ 12:00 am

Photos by Jess Horn.

listen

The Madison musician discusses his new cassette, Black Velvet, Dead Flowers.

Just as 2014 was wrapping up, Madison musician  Ian Adcock put out a new cassette EP under the name Conjuror, titled Black Velvet, Dead Flowers. Released on fellow Madisonian Beau Devereaux’s Golden Cloud Tapes label, the four-song EP dives right into dense, droning, and sometimes ominous layers of sound, all built from multi-tracked analog synth. Where 2013’s album Left Hand Path Of The Conjuror (one of our top 20 local records of that year) had plenty of eerie open space on it, Black Velvet, Dead Flowers pretty much constantly tries to fill one’s ears, playing off the textures and harmonics that arise in near-overstuffed soundscapes. He finds a lot of emotional range in the process: On the title track, the results are warm and comforting, with synth sounds that evoke organs and clarinets, but on “Trance-Chiante” and “Cry Of The Phoenix,” Adcock channels the sleek foreboding of Tangerine Dream.

Adcock grew up in northwest Ohio, where he played in no-wave and noise projects like Naughty Arabia and Puffy Areolas. Since moving to Madison, he’s played synth, guitar, and other instruments in a number of live improvisational settings, including with Second Family Band. He’s also put on a lot of shows by touring and local experimental artists at venues including Good Style Shop and the Dragonfly (full disclosure—I’ve helped out with/co-sponsored some of those shows). Until recently, he also co-hosted the In One End experimental music show on WORT. Adcock met up with me recently to discuss the new Conjuror release and his development as a musician.

Tone Madison: Was there anything you were trying to do differently on the new tape?

Ian Adcock: In Conjuror I’ve just been trying to do less, whittle things down to a very fine point. I did a few different things that I hadn’t done on the last one. It’s about half built similarly, and then I started doing a lot of stuff with tone clusters, so this stuff is just really a lot more abrasive.

Tone Madison: And it’s pretty much all one synth the whole record?

Ian Adcock: Yeah. The last album had some other keyboards on it, and this one I wanted to have it only out of one synth. So there’s a lot of overdubbing. The tones I got out of this one I’m very happy with. There are some very realistic-sounding things on it.

Tone Madison: How do you go about recording stuff for Conjuror? Is there a lot of looping, or just straight overdubs?

Ian Adcock: No, I don’t use any looping. What I do is I usually do a layer or two of the bass drone, and a lot of harmonics start to overlap and create these sort of microtonal or melodic structures that aren’t actually there. You can hear them, but they don’t actually exist. It’s just your brain and your ears thinking that they’re there. So then I try to write those out on the keyboard and get actual melodic structures out of those. I did all of the Black Velvet, Dead Flowers tracks within a couple months of each other. It was all recorded really fast, which is pretty unusual for me. A lot of it’s reflective of a lot of ideas I had going on in my head, researching the sho, which is this Chinese instrument, and it ended up influencing the mock pipe organs and stuff. I love organs and organ sounds and I was really fascinated by this sound when I first heard it, and I looked at it. That’s where a lot of the tone clusters come in—it can be really abrasive, weird-sounding instrument. The songs on the second side, “Cry Of The Phoenix” and “Mysteries Of The Free Reed," are kind of based on those sounds.

Tone Madison: What kind of synth are you using on this record?

Ian Adcock: It’s a Korg Mono/Poly. It’s an '80s monophonic synth. I like working on it because it’s got four separate oscillators that you can control quite a bit, so I can get quite a lot of different sounds out of it and got a really full tone. The Conjuror project kind of started when I got the synth, because I’d never owned a synth before. I was kind of fed up with my project at the time and I wanted to get a clean slate. I kind of sat on it for a year or so and maybe played a couple shows. I’m always really self-conscious about putting out recorded music. I’m so busy listening to it that I lose my frame of reference. I know what it sounds like and I’m really comfortable with it, but I’m not always sure how it’s going to be received, or if it’s necessary.

Tone Madison: On “Cry Of The Phoenix,” I can’t help feeling there’s a big Tangerine Dream influence, in that you have kind of this big mass of sound but gradually have a very definite melody coming through it.

Ian Adcock: Oh, yeah. Tangerine Dream’s always been a huge influence on me, one of the first bands I got into that used electronics. All the German dudes of that era were pretty important for me growing up. I listened to rock music, but then I heard that stuff and my head just kind of got split open.

“Cry Of The Phoenix,” I just went down in the basement and did it. I had no idea what was gonna happen, I just recorded it and then came upstairs and listened to it. I feel like it was funny because the sho is supposed to sound like what a phoenix would sound like, screaming at you, and so I kind of went into it with that in mind, and then I went upstairs and listened to and realized that I had sort of subconsciously ripped off Alice Coltrane’s version of Stravinsky’s “Firebird” melody. It was just a funny moment, like, “wait a second."

Tone Madison: What are some other things you’re hoping to do with music in the near future?

Ian Adcock: I’ve got lots of of unfinished stuff that I need to work on. I’ve been tossing around ideas in my head. I want to maybe write out some parts for other instruments that could play on it. I’ve been playing improvised music for a long time, and for a long time I was the least musically talented person in the room. I trained to play trombone but I definitely lost my lip—I was trying to record some trombone for Conjuror and it was not really working out. It’s been a really long time since I played guitar and other things. I feel like I play better at most instruments when I’m playing with other people in an improvised setting. It’s more challenging. Unfortunately I don’t do it much anymore, but I feel like that’s when I grow the most as a musician, when I’m in a room with people, improvising.

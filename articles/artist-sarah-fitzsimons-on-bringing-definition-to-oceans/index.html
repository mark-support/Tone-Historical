Artist Sarah FitzSimons on bringing definition to oceans

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
April 16, 2015 @ 8:57 am

Notes, sketches and reference materials from Sarah FitzSimons' studio, as installed in the Watrous Gallery. All photos courtesy Wisconsin Academy of Sciences Arts and Letters.

read

A talk with one of the three Wisconsin artists behind the “Waterways” show at the James Watrous Gallery.

Waterways, running through May 10 at the Wisconsin Academy's James Watrous Gallery in the Overture Center, brings together three Wisconsin artists to consider how the ubiquitous forces of water shape us, both in our all-important interactions with Wisconsin's lakes and rivers and on a global scale. Milwaukee artist Marsha McDonald's glittering installation "1,000 Canoes," curving around the Overture Center's third-store atrium, is both a show-stealer and a through line, trying together McDonald's works with those of Madison-based artists Sarah FitzSimons and John Miller. FitzSimons' section of the show combines porcelain sculptures of waves, her ongoing effort to stitch a large quilt representing the Pacific Ocean, and a wall of notes, sketches, and background materials from her studio. Miller, in a series of brightly colored screen prints, contemplates the varied and fascinating ways that light plays off the surface of a body of water. On another wall, visitors to the show can write Post-It notes sharing their own experiences and thoughts on water. FitzSimons, who also works as an art professor at UW-Madison, talked with me recently about the show as a whole and how she tackles water through an evolving mix of quilting, sculpture, installation art, and scientific inquiry.

Tone Madison: Have you had a chance to go to the gallery since the show as been up and experience how all the art sits together?

Sarah FitzSimons: I was there for the installation, but after that I haven't really been back. I think it works well together overall. My work that I have in the show, it's all sketches and models proposals and preparatory drawings, and so I don't necessarily have finished pieces in the exhibition in the way that Marsha and John do. In John's case it's nice because they included his finished prints plus the drawings. His sketches are just so beautiful. In my case, since the projects are so large and ongoing, there just wasn't room and the timing wasn't right to do a huge actual piece. In that case I think it worked really well. Group shows can definitely be challenging if you work with space, but from what I saw I felt it flowed really well, and I was there while people were setting up.

Tone Madison: And for something like the "Pacific Quilt," it's got a large scope and maybe it makes sense to see it in various stages of completion.

Sarah FitzSimons: Yeah, and to be honest it's going to take another year to finish it. So, to be practical, and also I think with a lot of my work the sketches and preparatory models become part of the piece and I don't look at them as something lesser, necessarily.

Tone Madison: With the "Pacific Quilt" piece, you're dealing with the shape of an ocean, and it makes you realize that we usually need the continents as a reference point to visualize an ocean. Here you're taking it out of that context—is that a disorienting process, and is there an interest there in sort of breaking out an ocean as a geographic entity?

Sarah FitzSimons: Certainly. And you could say the same thing about the continents—that they exist in relation to the oceans. We're just more used to seeing those taken out, for example the shape of North America, a territory we're familiar with partly because of the political divisions. Whereas the territory of the whole Pacific Ocean is a shape we're not used to seeing on its own, because one, it's not under one country's control, it's a shared territory, and two, it is a more fluid entity. With a few works in the show, I was thinking about ways to turn bodies of water into objects and turn that around in my head and better understand them, so it made sense to solidify it into this object. I'm not really solidifying the ocean into a block of stone, like Maya Lin, who's done some beautiful pieces where she takes the symmetry of a lake and will solidify it and show it in three dimensions. I'm using a solid material, which is fabric, but there's a lot of flow to fabric. We talk about water flowing and fabric flowing. Water covers things, and fabric also covers things and can move.

I've been thinking about these constantly shifting ocean boundaries. Maybe in the future, I might do some works where it's a projected ocean perimeter, meaning, what does it look like when sea level rises 50 feet. It'd be a very different shape. Indeed, the continents would be a different shape.

"Silence! Boo! Thank you!": Seven Patton Oswalt deep cuts

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi
January 13, 2015 @ 12:00 am

listen

Ahead of Oswalt's show this Wednesday at the Orpheum, we revisit great tracks from all of his stand-up albums.

Even as Patton Oswalt has branched out into writing books, voicing a lovable rat chef, and acting in increasingly nuanced and challenging film and TV roles, that hasn't diminished the greatness of his stand-up act. If you're a casual fan headed to his show this Wednesday at the Orpheum, don't expect to just yell out “famous bowls!" and get a result. Instead, start digging into Oswalt's five-album, decade-spanning discography, because all in all it's a rich and enduring one. Here are a few great places to start.

Singing like Leon Redbone on The Pennsylvania Macaroni Company (2006)

With someone as unabashedly geeky as Oswalt, it's only appropriate that his career should yield up some rare recordings for the unhealthily invested. Several have come courtesy of Chunklet's Henry Owings (“Henry will make you cool!" Oswalt declares), including a short CD called The Pennsylvania Macaroni Company, recorded at Logan Square Auditorium in Chicago during Oswalt's 2006 tour with Maria Bamford, Brian Posehn, and Eugene Mirman. During the encore, Oswalt's fellow comics tormented him with a game that started as a tour-van in-joke about a restaurant. “I made the mistake of singing the name of their company the way that Leon Redbone would," Oswalt explains, and things drunkenly, brilliantly degenerate from there, with Posehn, Bamford and Eugene pelting him with increasingly difficult questions for him to improvise around. Perhaps I just love this CD because I was at that show, but it also shows the endearing, and at times mildly sadistic, chemistry Oswalt enjoys with the other great comics he's championed.


Making puke jokes funny again on Finest Hour (2011)

A guy using a vomit bag on an airplane shouldn't make for that good of a stand-up bit, but Oswalt, having encountered “the Zorro of vomit-bag users," manages to turn the subject into an object of wonder, and expands it into a character and a story that keeps building for nearly four minutes. Four minutes of vomit-based material that's actually funny and interesting.


Destroying a heckler on Werewolves And Lollipops (2007)

Oswalt's crowd work is just as gleefully odd as his prepared stuff. In one of his best recorded moments, some idiot interrupts the act with a stupid rooster noise, and Oswalt just fucking savages the guy, turning the heckle into an opportunity to spin a bizarre tale of “rape goblins" and neuromancers. And it never quite feels like just some petty revenge-of-the-comic moment—at least Oswalt completes the interrupted bit before turning his attention back to the heckler and delivering a final blow: “You're gonna miss everything cool and die angry!"


Reliving the glory days of magician-comedians on My Weakness Is Strong (2009)

There's always been a fair bit of inside-baseball comedy-scene stuff in Oswalt's act, but it doesn't come off as self-important. Instead, Oswalt lets us in on both the wonder and the squalor of the stand-up life. He closes the album My Weakness Is Strong with this story about sharing a bill with an irate magician-comedian, and turns the bit into an exploration of the many textures of human rage and the ways in which a disaster can become a cherished memory.


Complaining about Florida on Comedy Plus Tragedy Equals Time (2014)

Oswalt's gained a lot of popularity and opportunity since his first stand-up album came out, but he hasn't lost the important things. And one of the important things is this sense that he's still a bit of a fussy aesthete who feels ill at ease in a grotesque world. In one story on his most recent album, he walks down a historic street in Miami—“'cause I wanted to see something pretty for 10 fucking minutes"—but still fails to escape the “all-alphas" reality of Florida.

Revisiting a great painting lesson on Feelin' Kinda Patton (2004)

Another thing that's remained important all along is Oswalt's ability to weave epically bizarre bits, taking some almost trifling subject and investing it with a feast of hallucinatory detail. I've never actually seen footage of late TV painting instructor William Alexander, and anyway he'll never be as vivid as he is in this older bit—and describing Alexander as “Klaus Kinski on crank" is just the beginning of a blisteringly insane caricature.


Begging the CIA not to bug his car, on Finest Hour

Lastly, it's important to remember what Oswalt can do even with subject matter that's not particularly esoteric or edgy—in this case, what he mumbles and sings to himself when he's alone in his car. Sometimes he's our beloved, geeky, surreal Patton, but the more straightforward and silly Patton is pretty great too.

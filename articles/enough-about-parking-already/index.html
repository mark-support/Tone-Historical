Enough about parking already

Ben Munson
@benjaminmunson
November 18, 2014 @ 10:49 am

read

Parking concerns have become too much of an obstacle to enlivening Madison's neighborhoods and cultural offerings.

A parking lot in downtown Madison closed last week.

Only two really unfortunate things came out of this: an agonizing Joni Mitchell reference—or Counting Crows, if you wish—and the Madison Children’s Museum feeling it had to explain its decision.

In a blog post about the opposite of paving paradise and putting up a parking lot, the museum’s executive director, Deb Gilpin, acknowledged the concerns and questions about the closure and sympathized with the museum’s disabled and less mobile visitors, who likely valued the proximity of the lot.

But perhaps Gilpin didn't need to be so conciliatory. This a parking lot we’re talking about. A parking lot less than a block away from Capitol Square, the hub of the city, a place that should strive toward giving rise to worthwhile endeavors. I want to see things that lift Madisonians' spirits and make visitors ask, “Why in the hell don’t we live here?”

You know, the sort of things Gilpin mentions in her blog: a park, an ice skating rink, or an urban growing space. My daughter would melt down with joy if they put in an ice skating rink. Even as the museum develops a plan using community input, it’s handed over the lot to the Funkyard, a Mad Max-like play structure built from forgotten wood.

The Children’s Museum has given up the parking revenue from this lot in order to serve something greater. It’s glorious to watch a beloved Madison institution slip the surly bonds of parking stress.

But parking hasn’t entirely relinquished its role as fun police. Madison city planners recently squashed a proposed development including a 2,000 to 2,500 seat multi-purpose venue on East Washington Avenue. One of the primary concerns was, yep, parking.

Neighborhoods near the proposed development no doubt have valid concerns about potential overflow from such a big theatre. The city has a legitimate interest in enforcing parking standards for new developments. And nobody much likes the idea of not being able to park near where they live, but those are the perils of living relatively close to downtown and in a neck of the woods that could use a little sprucing up.

Marquette-Williamson festivals guru Bob Queen told me once about the potential for letting people park at the Alliant Energy Center parking lot and then shuttling them over to La Fete de Marquette in order to avoid clogging up the isthmus. That’s one potential solution for parking woes surrounding an East Wash development, but that’s beside the point since the project may not even happen there.

The real point is that I can’t be the only one who’s tired of talking about parking. Parking sucks and it’s stupid and it always will be. There is no way to fix it. (Madison, a far more car-centric town than it fancies itself to be, could also learn more from the movement to discourage driving in more central parts of cities.) We should stop trying to maximize the convenience of parking and instead focus on things that will help us stop grinding our teeth after the long search for a parking spot.

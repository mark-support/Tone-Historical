Madison calendar, February 19 through 25

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Joel Shanahan
@golden_donna /
February 18, 2015 @ 9:49 am

Clockwise from bottom left: Doomtree plays the Majestic on Feb. 21; Damien Jurado plays the Majestic on Feb. 22; CRASHprez plays the Play Circle Theater on Feb. 20.

events

CRASHprez, Damien Jurado, Disappears, and more of the best stuff in Madison this week.

THURSDAY FEBRUARY 19

Anthony Jeselnik. Comedy Club On State, through Feb 21. see link for all showtimes.

The bits on Anthony Jeselnik's two stand-up albums, 2010's Shakespeare and 2013's Caligula, attain bleakness through elegant concision, as if with each punchline Jeselnik is handing you a delicate origami crane with a dusting of anthrax spores. This one, from Shakespeare, is on the gentle end of the spectrum for Jeselnik: “This chick is trying to get me fired because she claims I've been giving her inappropriate massages in the office. Well, I say, good luck with that, sweetheart—I don't even work here." So, the progress of a Jeselnik stand-up set can get a bit exhausting, but only because he's so thoroughly on top of his brutal, sick-minded craft.


Karaoke Underground, Dave Norwood. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

The Karaoke Underground is a karaoke program from Austin, Texas, that skips over your usual karaoke fare and lets attendees draw from the annals of punk and indie-rock. (Need to belt out a Wire song in front of strangers? Well, there you go.) But perhaps the real treat here is an opening solo set from Dave Norwood, who sings in Austin band The Gary. Learn more about him in our interview this week.


Marc Lamont Hill. Gordon Dining and Events Center, 7 p.m. (free)

A renowned anthropologist, journalist, and academic, Morehouse College professor Marc Lamont Hill heads up UW-Madison's observance of Black History Month with a talk titled “Creating Community In An Hour Of Chaos: Progress In The Age Of Obama."

The Linguists. Union South Marquee, 6 p.m. (free)

The 2008 documentary The Linguists chronicles the efforts of scholars David Harrison and Gregory Anderson, who travel to remote locales around the world to document the endangered languages of isolated and/or politically oppressed people. Harrison, who is currently a professor of linguistics at Swarthmore College and serves as vice president of the Institute For Endangered Languages, will be present for a post-screening Q&A.

Bad Cinema: Runaway. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. (free)

Written and directed by Michael Crichton, 1984's facepalm-inducing techno-dystopian thriller Runaway stars Tom Selleck as a cop trying to figure out why household robots are going homicidal—a search that eventually leads to a villain played by Gene Simmons.

Portland Cello Project. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m.

The cellists of Portland Cello Project make eclecticism their mission, with a repertoire that has spanned from Beethoven to modern classical to Kanye and Fleet Foxes. At this show, they'll be paying tribute to the songs of late fellow Portlander Elliott Smith.


FRIDAY FEBRUARY 20

CRASHprez, *hitmayng, Lord Of The Fly, The BellHops. Memorial Union Play Circle Theater, 8 p.m. (free)

On CRASHprez's forthcoming album More Perfect, the Maryland-raised, Madison-based rapper meets God and comes back from the dead to avenge his murder (continuing a long story that began on last year's Fear Itself EP). Such a conceptual arc would normally just seem a bit egomaniacal for a 21-year-old artist on his first full-length. But Crash, real name Michael Penn II, needs an outsize space for a personality that's constantly bouncing around between full-on indignation and stinging, over-the-top satire as he restlessly digs into identity, youth, and American racism on standout tracks like “Paranoia III" and “Postapocaniglet." Other strong points on the album, which he'll be performing in full here ahead of its release next week, include “Little Death" (a sensual duet with fellow Madisonian MyriahB) and the playful “Pollo Y Porno."

King Lear. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

A Girl Walks Home Alone At Night. Union South Marquee, 9 p.m. (free)

SATURDAY FEBRUARY 21

No More Road Trips?. Chazen Museum Of Art, 7 p.m. (free)

Filmmaker Rick Prelinger will be here in person to discuss and screen his experimental film No More Road Trips?, which strings together home movies from dozens of different sources to create an unconventional journey across America.

Doomtree, Open Mike Eagle, Greg Grease. Majestic, 9 p.m. (sold out)

The Minneapolis hip-hop crew Doomtree's new all-members album, All Hands, finds the seven rappers and producers growing more dark and grizzled with the years, but no less spirited. As with Doomtee's previous three full-on collaborative LPs, it's really a distinct experience from any of the members' solo albums and a chance for those newer to the group to appreciate it beyond its superstars POS and Dessa—songs like “Heavy Rescue" and “80 On 80" are all about embracing the crew's overload of personalities and aggro but slyly self-aware rhyme skills. This show is sold out, but if you're going, make sure to show up in time for an opening set from the eccentric but equally sharp-minded Open Mike Eagle.


Rob Delaney. Barrymore, 8 p.m.

Known for his insanely prolific and often rampantly funny Twitter presence, his outspoken advocacy for public health care, and now his new TV show Catastrophe (alongside Sharon Horgan), London-based and Boston-born comic Rob Delaney and his majestic jawline have risen to major prominence over the last few years. Make no mistake, Delaney's success has every bit as much to do with his clever shredding of Mitt Romney during the 2012 election and the disarming flow of his standup as it does with his social networking savvy. Hear more about where Delaney's at these days in our interview this week.

Paper Wasp, Scrimshaw, Czarbles. Mickey's Tavern, 10 p.m. (free)

On 2014's h/m+t=-h, Milwaukee band Scrimshaw borrows bits from Brew City legends like snotty folk-punks Violent Femmes and funky and fractured post-punks The Oil Tasters, tossed it all into a blender, and filtered the gunk through a child's weirdest nightmares. The result is a timeless pastiche of playfully deranged, lo-fi mutant-pop tunes that feel completely out of place with any of the myriad garage-revival, psych-rock, or indie-folk bands that have been bubbling out of the region in droves over the last decade. This special show also features an opening set from the Madisonian prog destroyers of Czarbles, who are currently putting the finishing touches on the long overdue follow-up to their self-titled 2007 debut, as well as a performance from Screamin' Cyn Cyn And The Pons drummer Stephen Shah's latest band Paper Wasp.


F For Fake. Vilas Hall, 9 p.m. (free)

Orson Welles could pull off charming, alert, and challenging work well into his later years, as 1973's F For Fake demonstrates. Part documentary and part essay on Welles' fascination with deception and authenticity, the film centers on fakes of various kinds, from art forgeries to Welles' own infamous radio-drama prank The War Of The Worlds.

Confidential Report. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. (free)

SUNDAY FEBRUARY 22

Damien Jurado, J.E. Sunde. Majestic, 7:30 p.m.

Damien Jurado's been doing some of his best and most adventurous work the past few years, especially with the companion albums Maraqopa (2012) and Brothers And Sisters Of The Eternal Son (2013). Underneath the albums' psych-brushed production and hazy conceptual narratives, Jurado remains the unpolished, un-forced, and frequently heartbreaking singer-songwriter he's been since putting out his first solo albums in the late '90s.


Suuns, Disappears, DJ Tolerance. Frequency, 8 p.m.

Chicago band Disappears has grown steadily more sinister, and immersed in its production approach since releasing its 2012 debut album Lux. The band's initial mix of driving garage-rock and eerie, repetitive sonics has given way to a steely, meticulous sound that leaves rock behind almost entirely on this year's Irreal. Read more in our interview with Disappears guitarist/vocalist Brian Case.


We Want The Colonels. Chazen Museum Of Art, 2 p.m. (free)

MONDAY FEBRUARY 23

Robyn Hitchcock, Emma Swift. High Noon Saloon, 7:30 p.m.

We were a bit lukewarm toward bizarro folk-rocker Robyn Hitchcock's 2014 album The Man Upstairs, as the aging anti-troubadour and former Soft Boy abandoned the brazen ambition and warped detail of albums like I Often Dream Of Trains or Fegmania! for a more traditional singer-songwriter-y approach toward an uneven collection of originals and covers that take on his own mortality. But fuck it, the butter-voiced paisley-popster is 61 years old and has a whopping 20 solo records ranging from good to mind-blowing under his belt, so he's got plenty of cerebral folk experiments to choose from at this show. Here's hoping we get a taste of Hitchcock's creepy, synth-laced, and harmony-rich pop-rocker “My Wife & My Dead Wife" or haunting piano ballad “Flavour Of Night."


TUESDAY FEBRUARY 24

Jesus Of Montreal. Union South Marquee, 7 p.m. (free)

In Denys Arcand's 1990 drama Jesus Of Montreal, a group of actors are tasked with creating a modernized Passion play. They end up creating a bold interpretation that infuriates religious authorities, setting off an ordeal that parallels the final trials of Jesus as described in the Bible.

Kevin Devine And The Goddamn Band, Dads, Field Mouse. Frequency, 7:30 p.m.

WEDNESDAY FEBRUARY 25

Nerd Nite. High Noon Saloon, 8 p.m. (free)

This month's informal Nerd Nite talks will cover plasma physics, the nature of visual systems, and the science behind love and sex.

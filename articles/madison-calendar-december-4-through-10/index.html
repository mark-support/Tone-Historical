Madison calendar, December 4 through 10

Scott Gordon
@scottgordonwi /
Chris Lay
@TotesChris /
Ben Munson
@benjaminmunson /
December 03, 2014 @ 8:42 am

read

Nick Lowe, Jad Abumrad, John Oliver, and more of the best stuff happening in Madison this week.

THURSDAY DECEMBER 4

Nick Lowe’s Quality Holiday Revue. Barrymore, 7:30 p.m.

Nick Lowe has aged with a certain comfy, white-haired aplomb. Over the last couple of decades, his solo albums have tended more toward understated wit that nods toward classic country songs (the 2009 compilation Quiet Please: The New Best Of Nick Lowe, is an excellent place to start with all that), with just the slightest hint of the button-pushing smartass who made albums like 1978’s Jesus Of Cool and 1979’s Labour Of Lust. We wouldn’t mind seeing another solo-acoustic show, but this time around he’s on a holiday-themed tour that builds off his playful 2013 album Quality Street: A Seasonal Selection for All the Family. Update: Ian McLagan, who was scheduled to open the show, has died.


Merry Christmas Mr Lawrence. Central Library, 6:30 p.m. 

Nagisa Oshima’s 1983 film stars David Bowie as a British soldier in a Japanese prisoner-of-war camp during World War II.

Barry Rothbart. Comedy Club On State, through Dec. 6. 

Not too far down into The Wolf Of Wall Street’s cast list, you’ll find the name Barry Rothbart. That film credit is displayed prominently on all the graphics associated with his appearance this weekend at The Comedy Club On State (despite me having zero recollection who he was in that movie) but don’t get it twisted: Rothbart’s stand-up chops are the best reason to go. "Tonight Show with Jay Leno", "Conan", "The Late Late Show with Craig Ferguson", "Adam Davine’s House Party", he’s done them all in the past couple of years. He was named a “New Face” and the “Best of the Fest” performer at the 2011 Just For Laughs Montreal Comedy Festival, and in 2013 he ended up on Variety’s "10 Comics to Watch” list. Rothbart's new stand-up album, Streets Of Fire, is heavy on crowd-work, but in between working the room he’s got some real wonderfully complex jokes in there too that, when delivered with his high-energy charm, build some really great momentum.

FRIDAY DECEMBER 5 

Night Light: Rob Dz Listening Party. Central Library, 8 p.m. 

Madison-based rapper Rob Dz has been hard at work this year on a new album, The Good Guy Memoirs, which you can read more about and hear a track from in our interview this week. At this installment of the Madison Public Library’s adult-oriented Night Light series, Dz will share some tracks for the album and kick off a crowdfunding campaign to support its release sometime in 2015. One reason this is happening at the library is that Dz made extensive use of the library’s free Media Lab resources to record the album, create the art for it, and make music videos.

MMOCA Nights: Narayon Mahon opening, Coby Ashpis. Madison Museum of Contemporary Art, 6 p.m.

Madison-based photographer Narayan Mahon traveled to countries that aren’t technically recognized as countries for his new MMOCA show, Lands In Limbo. And in travels to places like Northern Cyprus and Somaliland, Mahon found that “limbo” usually means political upheaval, war, economic hardship, and the lack of stable governments. Madison producer Coby Ashpis, whose music ranges from left-field hip-hop instrumentals to more fractured and abstract electronic work, will play at this opening reception, debuting new material that incorporates samples of folk songs and percussion from the regions depicted in Mahon’s photographs, as well as more ambient passages. Ashpis is currently working on a new EP for release early next year.


Cinematheque: Life Of Riley. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. 

Cinematheque wraps up its fall showcase of Madison premieres of new films with Life Of Riley, the final film by French director Alain Resnais, who died earlier this year.

Shane Shane, Paper Wasp, Cowboy Television, Futebol. Mr. Roberts, 9:30 p.m.

The headdresses. The unapologetic hirsute carnality. The ridiculously funny songs. Shane O’Neill left Madison for New York City a few years ago, but the theatrical insanity of his solo project Shane Shane is part of the reason we still miss and embrace the guy. He’ll be doing a solo show here, and playing a set with his old band Screamin’ Cyn Cyn And The Pons on Saturday at the High Noon. The all-local bill Friday at Mr. Roberts also includes the twisty post-punk of Paper Wasp, the dusty garage-rock of Cowboy Television, and the goofy electro-pop of Futebol.


Go Go Slow, Morbid Tales. Mickey’s Tavern, 10:30 p.m.

Milwaukee’s Go Go Slow comprises Die Kreuzen’s still quite awesome vocalist Dan Kubinski, Boy Dirt Car’s Dave Szolwinski, and fellow Milwaukee veterans Tammy Raeck and Paul New. What we’ve heard of them so far has a lot of straightforward, burly hard rock to it, but definitely with the mutated and noisy touch you’d expect from a band with this pedigree.


Dizzy Wright, Jarren Benton. Union South Sett, 9 p.m. 

Shakey Graves, Sean Rowe, Esmé Patterson. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m. (sold out)

SATURDAY DECEMBER 6 

Faux Fawn. Gates Of Heaven, 7:30 p.m. 

Madison/Stoughton band Faux Fawn (formerly Paul Otteson And Faux Fawn) plays here to celebrate its new album Lonesome Loon. Centering on Paul Otteson’s mournful vocals and artful, narrative songwriting, the album also shows Faux Fawn really cohering into a band, one that’s more subtle and sonically curious than your average chamber-folk outfit. Read more and hear an exclusive preview of a song in our interview this week.

Bereft, Whisky Pig, Tyranny Is Tyranny. Dragonfly Lounge, 9:30 p.m. 

Madison metal outfit Bereft has shown a lot of promise over the past couple years, crafting doom- and black-metal-influenced songs full of both heavy impact and nuanced moods. That promise comes through nicely on their first album, Lost Ages, which they’ll be celebrating at this show. Read more in our interview with members Alex Linden and Zach Johnson.

Women’s Medical Fund Benefit: Samantha Glass, Miserable Friend, A Haircut, Proud Parents, DJ Golden Donna. Phoenix Co-Op, 7 p.m. 

The Women’s Medical Fund helps protect low-income Wisconsin women’s access to abortions, a task that’s only getting harder in the days of Scott Walker and his unchecked far-right legislature. This show raises money for WMF, with help from Madison electronic standout Samantha Glass, echo-smeared Appleton punks Miserable Friend, and charming Madison punk outfits A Haircut and Proud Parents. Madison’s Golden Donna (aka Tone contributor Joel Shanahan), fresh off a European tour, will be doing a DJ set.


Screamin’ Cyn Cyn And The Pons, Sexy Ester, I Saw The Creature. High Noon Saloon, 9:30 p.m.

Various things, like members moving away and having babies and stuff, have deprived us of the cracked punk cabaret that is Screamin’ Cyn Cyn And The Pons. Luckily, their infrequent reunion shows are pretty much just as sweaty and frantic and ridiculous as the band was in olden times. If they still have copies of last year’s 7-inch Girls Are OK EP, get one.


Cardinal Bar 40th Anniversary Celebration. Cardinal Bar, 7 p.m. 

This week the Cardinal Bar has been marking 40 years of hosting an array of jazz and electronic music, and especially its legacy of welcoming Latin dance music. Saturday night’s celebration includes music from long-running local band MadiSalsa and DJ Jo-Z, as well as a chance to check out a new documentary about the Cardinal.

House Of Love: Paul Johnson, Lovecraft, Wyatt Agard. Jolly Bob’s, 10 p.m. 

Chicago DJ and house producer, Paul Johnson is probably best known for his 1999 hit “Get Get Down.” But he’s been DJing since the ’80s and played a big role in American dance-music history, so we can reasonably hope for a pretty masterful DJ set here.


Cinematheque: The Conformist. Vilas Hall, 7 p.m. 

Bernardo Bertolucci’s 1970 film The Conformist stars Jean-Louis Trintignant as a secret-police operative in Mussolini’s Italy. Trintignant’s performance, as a man who turns to fascist conformity as a way of coping with sexual ambiguity and sadism, combines with Vittorio Storaro’s cinematography to create a mesmerising yet icy character study. The Conformist reaches deep into the protagonist’s history and psyche, yet without ever letting the audience acquire an intimacy with him, and perhaps that’s why it’s remained a fascinating classic.

SUNDAY DECEMBER 7 

John Oliver. Orpheum, 10 p.m. (7 p.m. show sold out)

After an excellent run on The Daily Show, comic and commentator John Oliver began to outdo himself on the new HBO show Last Week Tonight. Oliver’s long-form segments on subjects like drone killings and net neutrality have elevated him to a still very funny but also poignant and penetrating voice in news and politics (whether he likes it or not). Oliver also co-hosts the Bugle podcast and, from 2010 to 2013, hosted one of TV’s better stand-up comedy showcases in John Oliver’s New York Stand-Up Show. Oliver’s own stand-up features the same sharply observant and mildly exasperated persona that’s served him so well in his new gig.

Cinematheque: Spellbound. Chazen Museum of Art, 2 p.m.  

Alfred Hitcock’s 1945 film Spellbound boasts a tense chemistry between leads Gregory Peck and Ingrid Bergman and a dream sequence designed by Salvador Dalí. But in addition to those enticements, it’s also simply one of Hitchcock’s best, an unconventional psychological thriller that makes ingenious use of questions of memory and dreams.


Boo Bradley, Mal-O-Dua. High Noon Saloon, 3:30 p.m. 

Boo Bradley’s often playful but also deep-reaching interpretation of acoustic blues and ragtime is something of a Madison treasure. It’s easy to take guitarist Scott Mullarky and washboard percussionist Bradley Selz for granted, as they’re often found busking downtown and playing regular gigs at Alchemy and the Weary Traveler, but really Boo Bradley makes some of the most charming and intriguing folk music in town. At this show they’ll celebrate their recently released album, Rub Rub Rub. They printed the album’s CD covers with a hand-carved stamp, and will be bringing along their hand presses to let fans print their own copies.


Riff Raff. Majestic, 8 p.m. 

Riff Raff set out to become an impeccably groomed pack of tropical Starburst and he achieved his dream. While making the jump to Hypercolor, Riff Raff has emerged as an unearthly and often hilarious punchline rapper who can make pulling up in a Versace coffin sound equal parts vapid and profound. His major label debut, Neon Icon, pairs Riff Raff with better rappers (like Mac Miller) and outstanding producers (like Harry Fraud) leaving the California by way of Houston MC outshined a bit as he yelps his one-liners. But live, with a captive audience, Riff Raff will likely expose the full spectrum of his laconic wit, definitely spark a sing along on "Tip Toe Wing in my Jawwdinz," and set the absurdity to Baja Blast.

MONDAY DECEMBER 8 

Distinguished Lecture Series: Jad Abumrad. Union Theater, 7:30 p.m. 

Jad Abumrad co-hosts and produces the playful, engrossing, and often experimental public-radio program Radiolab, which makes him one of the more refreshing and inventive people in storytelling and journalism right now. (He also makes music.) At this Distinguished Lecture Series appearance, he’ll give a talk titled “Gut Churn,” which centers around the question of what it means to innovate.
